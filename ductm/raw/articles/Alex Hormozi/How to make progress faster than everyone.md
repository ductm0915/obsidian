# How to Make Progress Faster Than Everyone

## 1. Do Not Judge Your First Chapter

This is the first piece of content I ever made. The first ad I ever made. Fast forward today: we make 450 pieces per week. I broke the Guinness World Record for the fastest-selling non-fiction of all time with $16 million in sales in a weekend. We own a portfolio of companies that did north of $250 million in aggregate revenue last year. 

In this video, I want to show you just how far anyone can come. Do not judge your first chapter by someone else's 25th or thousandth chapter. 

## 2. On Shame, Guilt, and Being Cringe

Let's define these terms real quick. Shame is breaking someone else's rules. Guilt is breaking your own rules. "Cringe" is supposed secondhand embarrassment—someone saying, "I'm embarrassed for them." In reality, it's a defensive status play. If someone says, "Oh, that's cringe" to you, it means you're beginning to change your status relative to them, and therefore you're on the right path.

Whose rules are we breaking? Did we agree to their rules? People seeing you try hard will say, "Why are you taking this so seriously? Why do you even care?" But the truth is, they've never cared about anything in their lives. 

I had a guy coming up to me saying he saw how hard I was trying with content, bragging that he had outsourced all of his down to two hours a week. I told him: "Yeah, it shows." He was trying to be cool about not trying hard, but all it looked like was that he was losing.

Is it cringe to care? Yes, of course it's cringe. You can make anything cringe by just caring about it. And the only people who think caring isn't cool are people in high school or people who got older but never grew up. Think about competitive chess, bodybuilding, acting, writing a book, making YouTube videos—anyone can make anything cringe because anyone who cares about anything is cringe. 

But the cringiest thing of all is to be scared about looking cringe. Do we really want a world where people care less? 

## 3. Document the Struggle

I want to show this in the realest form I possibly could. I went through the archives. I am a weirdo; I try to keep everything—my lifelong texts, videos, and pictures. And I'm very grateful for that. 

Here is a piece of advice to anybody on the journey right now: Document more. I don't have many regrets in life, but one of them is that I didn't document the struggle early on. I didn't document the journey because I was ashamed of it. I didn't want anyone to know. It's like people who are overweight not wanting to take pictures of themselves. 

But you have to face reality. If you believe that you're going to win, this will be part of the story you tell. Kanye was documenting in the very early days because he believed so hardcore that he was going to win. The moment I actually started documenting purposefully was the day I had $1,000 left in my bank account after losing everything. I told myself I would never let this happen again, and this would be the beginning of my comeback story. 

## 4. You Must Deserve What You Want

Unfortunately, the world is too rational of a place to reward the undeserving. In order to get what you want, you must deserve what you want—which really means that you've got to be good. And the only way to get good is to be willing to be bad for a very long time.

My promise to you is that you will be cringe. Your first videos, pieces of content, and your first product—they will all be cringe. But you will not be alone. You'll figure it out as long as you start, take feedback to get better, and never stop. 

## Key Takeaways

1. **Do not judge your first chapter by someone else's chapter 1,000.**
2. **"Cringe" is a defensive status play; someone calling you cringe means you're on the right path.**
3. **Caring about things is always "cringe." Embrace it.**
4. **Document your struggle. It will be the beginning of your comeback story.**
5. **The world rewards the deserving; the only way to get good is to be willing to be bad for a very long time.**
