## 3. Bài Học 2: Bản Lĩnh Hơn Động Lực

So that was number one. The second one is that the longer I've been in this game, the more I feel like **mental toughness** matters more than **motivation**.

**Mental toughness** comes down to four things. When my mother died, I had to think through: how am I going to respond to this obviously negative event in my life?

She died all of a sudden — no one thought it was going to happen. It was just very fast, which in some ways I can be grateful for: she didn't have a long suffering period.

There are four things that happen when any **negative event** occurs — something against your preferences, something bad.

### Bốn Yếu Tố Của Bản Lĩnh

1. **Threshold —** How much bad stuff can happen before it forces you to change the way you live?

2. **Behavior shift —** Something rattles you enough that you change the way you behave. Once you change your behavior, how upset do you get? How low do you go?

Someone who's more **mentally tough** takes longer to get upset, and if they do snap, they only change their behavior a tiny bit.

**Example —** They're not like: > "Oh, I'm going to go start taking heroin and I'm going to go cheat on my wife and I'm going to go, you know, drive drunk."

They're like: *"I might have some ice cream tonight."* 3. **Resiliency —** Once on that negative path, how fast do you come back? How fast do you rebound back to where you were?

Some people let a cross word bother them for a week, two weeks, a month before they rebound — even if it was a small thing.

## 4. Bốn yếu tố của sức bền tâm lý

With a small change of behavior: if you have **low resiliency**, it just takes you a long time to get back to **baseline**.

**The fourth element:** when you do rebound, how much do you rebound?

- **Below baseline** → a **traumatic event** — it permanently changed your behavior.
- **Back to baseline** → not traumatic; did not permanently change you.
- **Above baseline** → you **adapt**. You're better than you were before the bad thing happened.

By the way, that also traumatizes you. Translate the word **trauma** into: a **permanent change in behavior** from an aversive, bad stimulus. So **trauma** simply means you permanently change the way you act when something bad happens.

Is there a world where you can change how you act permanently — where you behave *better* than you did before? Yes.

**Example —** Many humans, a generation or two back, were traumatized by their parents many times — with a belt, a hanger, a spoon, a hand, a shoe. You did something, you got smacked, and then you permanently stopped doing that. They traumatized you. Was trauma bad in that situation? No.

**Key insight:** Trauma is only bad if it **permanently changes your behavior in a way that is against the type of person that you want to be** — or against the types of behaviors you prefer to act in or do.

## 5. Định nghĩa và đo lường mental toughness

4. **Growth — Phát triển qua nghịch cảnh:** Sau trải nghiệm đó, trở nên tốt hơn — không phải tệ hơn.

## 23. Tự suy nghĩ hoặc thế giới sẽ suy nghĩ thay bạn

Most people pick their **identities off the shelf**. They come with pre-canned beliefs and a set of apparel that signals who they are.

**Example — Hipster:** gauges, beanie hat, black combat boots with a chain — and all the beliefs that go with it.

**Example — Redneck:** flannel, trucker hat, jeans, super conservative views on X, Y, Z — lives in the country, wants a ranch.

## 40. More, Better, New — Bài Học #10

So number 10, **more**, **better**, **new**.

Now, this isn't going to be a new concept for anyone who's listening to my stuff, but I will have a slightly different take on this. **New is incredibly risky.** **Analogy —** Imagine you have these candies thrown all over the place. If you keep doing this, how likely is it that all of them are going to end up in the exact position — separated by color and stacked together? Very unlikely. You would have to hit refresh and keep doing that many, many times until eventually all of these are lined up, stacked up on each other super nicely in a way that's organized.

Could it happen? Of course it could — it is statistically possible. Is it likely? No.

This is six pieces of candy. There are **variables within your business** that create the business that works. When you have your business and it is stacked, it is working, it is generating revenue — the likelihood that you changing one of these variables makes the business better **goes down**.

The taller the stack is, the more evolved your business is, the more bricks in that building have been properly placed — and so the higher the likelihood the **next move is the wrong move** if it's something different.

### New Là Rủi Ro Cao Nhất

**Example —** If you have something called a **control** and you have a **variant** when you run an **AB split test**. If you have a control that you've run 36 split tests on and it has won 36 of those, it is the number one control. The likelihood that you changing that resulting in an improvement is low.

Think about your business as it currently stands — there are many different ways to align these variables. Almost all of them are **not profitable businesses**. You happen to have one that actually works.

If you have that, then the **highest risk-adjusted move** that you can make is that you do **more of the thing that already works**. You might try and do **better** — which is a tiny variation off of what you're currently doing. But something truly **new** rarely works. And it also usually takes the most resources.

**On new things —** I give myself **one new thing a year**. One.

- This year's one new thing: the **book launch**.

I know what the tremendous drain of resources is — **one big thing, one new thing**. The bigger the companies that we have, the more resources we have, the **fewer new things** we're doing.

When **nothing works**, you want to hit refresh as many times as you can — you want to do lots of new [ __ ] cuz nothing's working. But as soon as something works, then you start to have direction. You want to have **less and less deviation** from that, unless you really believe there's an **existential risk** that would require you to change everything — which is significantly less likely than you really think it is.

### Đừng Giải Quyết Vấn Đề Chưa Tồn Tại

Many business owners will **destroy their businesses** in reality for **problems they have manufactured artificially** — problems that have not even yet occurred, because they are afraid of them happening.

For those of you who are old enough to have businesses during co — if you had a business for at least five or six years — you know that you'll survive. You'll adapt, just like you always have.

Do **not** try and change the business to solve **problems that do not exist**. There is more than enough **problems that absolutely do exist today** that you should be solving.

## 44. Bài 11: Được Phép Đơn Giản Là Kiếm Tiền

So number 11, **it's okay to just make money.** Hear me out.

There's something I like to call the **third marshmallow fallacy.** Many of you have heard the research study: they videotaped kids and told them, *"We'll put one marshmallow in front of you — if you wait 10 minutes, we'll give you a second one."* The kids who waited were more successful in life because it's a proxy for the ability to **delay gratification.** What's interesting is that people then assume **delaying gratification is always the best course of action.**

### Cái Bẫy Của Việc Trì Hoãn Vô Hạn

If you **delay gratification indefinitely**, you will work your entire life for nothing — because you have delayed gratification, and then you will die.

Said differently: if you are the ant that always saves up for winter, keeps saving and saving and saving, and then dies — you have a big stack of crumbs you will never have done anything with. The question is no longer about *whether* to delay gratification.

**Key insight:** Once you have learned to **delay gratification**, you then have to learn the even harder task of the **appropriate time to accept gratification.** This is incredibly difficult especially for **winners** — because in the earlier part of your career you get rewarded for delaying gratification. Often there is a **dose relationship**: the longer you delay, the bigger the outcome.

So you continue to learn to delay more and more. But there is a time to reap, **because you cannot sow forever.**

### Điều Chỉnh Rủi Ro Theo Thời Gian

I was having a conversation with a friend who said, *"Hey, I don't want to do this thing because it's not going to add enterprise value."* But the deal we were talking about was worth hundreds of millions of dollars — and the alternative was uncertain and 10 years away.

**Key insight:** We need to allow **risk adjustment for outcomes** to make their way into the decision.

If you can get paid **today** versus in **5 years**, there is a value to that money — because you have five more years of using those resources to acquire more resources.

Something that has shifted: **it's okay to make money.** You can just make money for the sake of making money — and that's fine.

**On service/software businesses:** If you don't want to sell your own time one-on-one — totally okay. If you don't want revenue that doesn't carry a high multiple — that's fine too.

### Case Study: Khách Hàng PT Trả Tiền Mặt

When I started my first gym, I had one personal training client who paid me $4,000/month in cash — 90 minutes/day, 5 days/week. A lot of personal training.

- That money was **not scalable**
- It was not something I could ever sell
- But it **paid for me to eat**
- It **allowed me to delay gratification** on the rest of the business

A purist would have said: *"You shouldn't have taken that distraction — you should have worked even more in the business."* I just don't think that's true.

**Note — this is only for winners.** If you've never learned to delay gratification, if you still overspend your income — **ignore this entirely.** For the **savers** — those who always live under their means, willing to delay 1 year, 5 years, 10 years — you also have to learn to ask: *when do I reap?*

### Case Study: Creator Không Dám Kiếm Tiền Từ Audience

This is super common in **content creators** — it's actually why I ended up writing this.

I had a conversation with a creator who had built a large Twitter following over many years. He said he wanted to better **monetize his following.** I asked what CTAs he had, what he told his audience to do. He said: > "Oh, nothing. I never want to sacrifice the goodwill I have with my audience."

I asked why he built the brand to begin with. He said he wanted to make money. I asked: > "Okay, how do you expect to make money if you never ask for it?"

He had been taught: **delay, delay, delay.** And to be fair, of course you have to delay. But at some point you have to make the decision — *now is the time.* This doesn't mean asking every single day. It just means **beginning the process of reaping.** For **forever savers** — those who always underspend and always delay to the future — there is a line you need to be able to draw for yourself, for today.

## 46. Bài 12: Rõ Ràng Hơn Khéo Léo

**Clear beats clever** in all things.

You will need to **repeat yourself until you die**. No one cares. No one is listening.

**On the book launch:** I made **5,000 ads** that pretty much said the same thing — that was it. And still most people didn't attend the launch.

- even if they did find out, didn't register
- even if they registered, most didn't come **The math:** If you have 100 people who register for something, **less than half will show up**. If you have 1,000 people who find out about something from an ad, **less than 2%** will even click.

You're dealing with such small percentages that the idea of wanting to be clever in any way is **ridiculous**.

The longer I've been in business, the more I emphasize **clarity** over cleverness and **simplicity** over complexity. *How do I make this as simple and as clear as humanly possible?* That effort of continuing to learn to **compress what you're saying** — getting it simpler and simpler — is where it becomes **elegant** and where it becomes **effective**.

**Case study:** A friend had a massive campaign for his company. He spent a day with me going through everything — his team had a really complex marketing structure. He's a phenomenal marketer. He simplified everything down to **one big benefit** and **10 reasons** for that thing — and had the **highest converting campaign he had ever done** in the history of his company. He has a very successful company and has been doing it for a very long time.

After the day, he came to me and said: > "My team has heard me say this so many times. They needed somebody from the outside to say it. You got to be the bearer of bad news instead of me." — Friend **Application** — whether you're writing copy, making sales pitches, writing emails, or making content: **One big idea with 10 reasons** — not 10 big ideas with one reason each.

It's in the sub-headline.

## 63. Tìm Và Giữ Lãnh Đạo Cấp Cao

You need leaders who can **drive the revenue lines** and are **incentivized on the bottom line**. If they need you to drive the revenue, they are not the driver — you are still the driver.

If you really want to build something big, you have to be able to find people who are **exceptional**, compensate them well, and **get out of their way**.

**Missing piece:** The thing that was missing was **pattern recognition on what high-quality talent looks like**.

### Nhận Diện Chất Lượng Talent

**Framework —** If you have a **$100 million business**, you might have one, two, or three founders with **nine-figure** skill sets. The people who run within that — the next tier — are **eight-figure** or **multi-figure entrepreneurs**.

To create the **$100M business**, you might have:
- Two **$25M entrepreneurs** running major divisions
- Five **$10M entrepreneurs** running the next tier
- Many **seven-figure entrepreneurs** underneath them The **aggregate revenue** of the business is the added-up potential of all the skill of all the players within it.

**Test:** If your **head of sales** couldn't go do sales independently, or your **marketing person** couldn't start a marketing agency — they're probably not that good.

**Rule:** If none of the people you have could go do it on their own, you probably don't have **good enough people**.

### Thu Hút A-Player Bằng Uncapped Earnings

The fear: *"If I bring somebody in who can do it on their own, they'll just steal my stuff."* **Counter:** If you don't bring them in, you will literally do it for the rest of your life.

**Solution:** Create a vision and opportunities big enough that it still makes more sense for the person to work here — because they can have **uncapped earnings**.

The distinction isn't about **owning the whole business** — for the vast majority of businesses, many people own them. What's more key is that someone has **unlimited earning capacity**. That's how you attract **A-players**.

If they are true A's, they'll light up — because the **return on effort is higher** due to the aggregate infrastructure already built:
- **Brand recognition**
- **Reputation**
- **Delivery mechanisms** already in place They can focus on what they're really good at — at a higher level than they could achieve on their own — without getting **bottlenecked** by tasks outside their superpower.

## 67. Lãnh Đạo Luôn Là Vấn Đề

If there's ever a problem in an organization, a department, or a line of revenue — look at the **leader**. **The leader is always the problem.** Not sometimes. Always.

If a **function is mediocre**, it's because the **leader is mediocre**. Period. Full stop. No questions.

So it's either the leader and the team, or just the leader. But it's **never just the team**.

- **Leader mediocre + brought in mediocre people** → leader and team problem
- **Leader mediocre + great team** → the leader will turn the great team into a mediocre team

**Case study:** A business owner had four locations — two doing well, two doing poorly. After many questions, the key fact: the person running them was a longtime friend, and also an addict — showing up to work inebriated every day.

He was asking for **tactical business advice**. But there is no tactical business advice that will matter — no one will listen to or respect that leader.

> "Why don't you show up to work sober? Why don't we start there?"

Imagine that person walking in and saying *"Hey guys, let's pick up the pace."* Pricing, sales — **none of it matters until we fix the leader problem**.

That's an extreme example, but it happens at every level. **Media team not working well?** Leader. **Sales team not performing?** Leader.

**On the team:** After pulling the leader out, there's a **50/50 shot** the team is also compromised.

- **Bad leader + low bar** → likely brought in low-bar people too
- **Great leader + mediocre team** → a great leader can turn a mediocre team into a great team

Have you ever seen a good coach turn around a bad team? There are countless stories about it.

I spent too much time early in my career trying to pinpoint problems. **The razor:** There's a problem with a function → the **leader is the issue**. Just solve it. This is so much faster.

## 68. Brand Là Lợi Thế Cạnh Tranh Trong Thời Đại AI

**Lesson 24.** In the world of AI, your **brand is your moat**. As **services, products, listed goals, and content** become increasingly **commoditized** and easy to reproduce, **branding** — the act of *who and what you associate with* — is what will matter.

**On local businesses:** Your brand will be mostly **word of mouth** and **online reviews**. For almost every other business, it's that *plus* all of the **content you make publicly**.

**Core principle:** Give away the **secrets**, sell the **implementation**. People do not want to do the work for whatever it is that you're showing.

**Examples:**
- Show them how to fix toilets → they'll just call you to do their plumbing
- Tell them how to fix relationships → they'll still call you to help them fix their relationship
- Tell them how to fix their business → they'll call you to help them fix their business

### Chiến Lược Xây Dựng Brand

People do not want to do the work — or they don't want to do it alone. **Key —** myself included. I will always pay to **go faster** and **decrease risk** so they don't have to do backtracks.

I get the DIY. I will consume something and be like: > "This person clearly knows what they're talking about. I will pay to go faster and I will pay to have you take this generic thing, make sure it's applied to my business so that it's right because I don't want to pay the tax of ignorance."

**Application:** To build a **brand in 2026**, think about the **curated associations** you want to make that will reinforce the **position** you want to have in your prospects' minds. What things do they like that you want them to think of when they think of you?

**Strategy:** It's going to be **demonstration of skill**. I'm going to do a lot more business fixing next year and build a lot more infrastructure to do that. What's something that very few people can do in your market that you can **demonstrate better than anyone else**?

## 72. Rủi Ro Lớn Nhất: Founder Mất Ý Chí

**The greatest risk to the business** is that you don't feel like doing it anymore. This is super real.

Most businesses don't actually run out of money — **the founder runs out of will.** You have to have the frame shift: it has to be **worth suffering for.** I do think that **making money** is a component of it. I had somebody the other day who was like, *"Hey, I'm thinking about switching this business."* And that's how most businesses die — the entrepreneur just switches and says, *"It's not worth it for me anymore."* I said, *"If this thing made 10 times the money, would you keep doing it?"* And they're like, *"Hell yeah."* I'm like, *"Would you look at this other business?"* They're like, *"No."* I'm like, *"Well, then dude, let's just fix the business."* So, making more money can help, but I will say — kind of like **electroshock therapy** — if it made 10 times the money, it will help in the short term, but not forever.

**The shrinking and the growing** are the ones that are the most painful. **Plateaus**, people can just stay in them for years because it's just not painful enough to quit, but still very painful.

- **Growing fast** — In lots of pain. Eventually they don't need the money anymore at all. If you've grown a lot and made a lot of money really quickly, you then stop wanting to be in all the pain that this **tremendous growth** provides — because you're like, *"I don't need to do this anymore."*
- **Plateau** — Not painful enough to quit, but still very painful. People can just stay in them for years.
- **Shrinking / volatile** — The most pain. Same volatility and pain as the growth phase, except you also aren't making money.

That is the most pain that you will be in business.
