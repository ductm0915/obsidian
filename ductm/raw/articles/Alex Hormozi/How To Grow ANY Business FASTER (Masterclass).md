## 3. Các Điểm Chặn Tăng Trưởng

But then you'll hit these **breaking points** that stop your growth flat.

## 8. Phụ thuộc chuỗi cung ứng

**Super uncommon** — Are you the one who's manufacturing the stuff? Are you the one who's actually doing **pick, pack, ship** — both locally and internationally? Are you sourcing **raw materials**, or do you have a raw material mine — collecting milk from cows and turning that into protein?

**Core dependency:** You have core dependencies on other businesses. And if those businesses fail, you fail — very real.

**Solution:** Having many **redundancies** within each element of the business: - **Raw materials** — How do we source them?

- **Logistics** — Pick, pack, ship locally and internationally

**Key insight:** Your **growth rate** will be dictated by the amount of **free cash flow** the business kicks off → that will be the only growth rate you'll be able to sustain, and you'll be **constrained by that capital**.

There are **lending partners** because this is clearly a **capital-constrained business** if you're growing quickly.

## 10. Brand — Lợi thế cạnh tranh bền vững nhất

The next issue is going to be making sure that the product you have has a **brand** behind it. And this is the real nasty one because if you just have a pure — I call them **smash and grab** businesses — which is just a **media arbitrage business**.

**Smash and grab:** You buy eyeballs at $10 and sell them something where you make $11 in gross profit. And if somebody else sees you smashing and grabbing, guess what they do?

**The downside of physical products** is that because anybody can own it, anybody can also sell it. If you've got a widget, somebody else can go to China and make the same widget as you.

**Competitive moats** for physical products: - **Patents**
- Owning your **manufacturing**
- **Special sauce** in terms of how you make your stuff But the largest of all of them by far is **brand**.

**Why?** Nike shirts are inherently the same shirts that other shirt companies make. They literally buy the same raw materials. But why are you willing to pay so much more for a Nike shirt versus an unnamed shirt? Because of the **brand**.

Even if someone can copy every single thing down to the thread count of your business, they still can't copy the **brand**.

### Ba lợi ích cụ thể của brand mạnh

The value that **brand** delivers — especially critical in e-commerce where commoditization is so easy: 1. **Higher click-through rates** — more people show interest because they have a history of good experiences or good word of mouth
2. **Higher repurchase rates** — people will buy once and continue to buy again
3. **Higher premiums** — able to charge more for the same thing Those translate to very real business outcomes: - **Lower cost of acquisition**
- **Higher LTV** — because they're buying more
- **Higher gross profits** — because they're buying at premium prices Those are very real benefits of building a big **brand**.

### Đầu tư liên tục vào brand

When these businesses are kicking off cash, that means you have to **continually invest** to expand and reinforce the associations that you want your products to stand for.

This also means making deliberate choices about: - Which **affiliates or influencers** you will *not* associate with, even if they can drive quick sales
- Which **discounts** you choose to make — or not — based on what kind of brand you want to demonstrate for your target audience Some brands **never do discounts**; some **live and die on discounts**. It really depends on what kind of brand you're trying to build.

But you've got to be specific about it, and you want to be as **deliberate** about it as you can from day one.

## 15. Giữ Chân Khách Hàng và Word-of-Mouth

As you scale businesses, the **cost of acquiring customers** continues to rise.

The only thing that acts as a **compounding vehicle** — scaling proportionally to increasing acquisition cost — is a **referral word-of-mouth chain**. It does two things: 1. **Repeat visitors** — What percentage of your sales are customers coming back because they really like the product?
2. **Customer referrals** — What percentage of those customers are bringing more customers?

**On the right percentage:** It depends on the **growth rate** of the business. If you're growing 1000%, you'll always have more new customers in the beginning.

**What matters for sellability** — demonstrate with a new cohort: how many of them are going to come back again?

**Benchmark:** If you can get **60–70%** to buy again after the first purchase, you likely have a **strong product**.

## 21. Volatility Trong Sales Và Delivery

**Inevitable volatility** tồn tại trong mọi business. Volatility xuất hiện khi có người mới gia nhập bất kỳ bộ phận nào — outbound, ads, sales, hay delivery.

**Example —** Nếu bạn có một **sales guy**, khi thuê người tiếp theo, anh ta sẽ không giỏi bằng người đầu tiên vì cần thời gian training.

**Solution:** Bạn phải có khả năng **swallow the slowdown** — cần có **cushion** (đệm) trong **gross profit** để chịu đựng giai đoạn này. Nếu **conversion rate** giảm một nửa vì sales guy mới chưa quen việc, bạn phải có thể *eat that* trong 30 ngày đầu.

**On the delivery side —** Giả sử bạn có một hoặc hai **account reps** giỏi, gắn bó 5 năm. Khi bạn scale marketing và sales, bạn cần thêm account rep mới — nhưng họ sẽ không deliver được ở cùng mức chất lượng.

Lúc này **reputation** của bạn bị đe dọa vì người mới không thể maintain cùng **scale of delivery**.

**Key insight:** **Service businesses** rất dễ bắt đầu vì chi phí ban đầu gần như bằng không — bạn chỉ đơn giản là bán **thời gian của mình**. Đó là lý do đây là loại hình kinh doanh phổ biến nhất mà mọi người khởi nghiệp.

## 36. Hai Lựa Chọn Khi Cần Scale Delivery

### Phép Ẩn Dụ: Sữa Tươi và Bài Toán Pha Loãng

I have two options: 1. **Shot glasses** — Do 10 shot glasses and pour the milk into those. A much **smaller amount** of the **same quality**.
2. **Diluted glasses** — Glasses of the same size, all filled with water, with a shot glass worth in each. A much larger amount of a **shittier** product.

Which one would you rather have? Most people would rather have a **smaller amount of a good thing** than a **larger amount of a bad thing**.

**Application:** This is what the vast majority of businesses choose to do by saying: > "Oh, I'm going to hire this person. I'm going to train them for 5 hours and then I'm going to sell the services at the same price that I sell for myself for this person."

If you have the **best milk in the world**, you can give the tiniest shot — and it's still good and better than one mediocre guy giving 100% of his attention.

**Key insight:** Technology has **democratized access** for people to get educated. **Democratized access** → **consolidated production** — meaning if you're the **best in the world**, you get to do it for *everyone*.

## 38. Vấn Đề Cốt Lõi: Tạo Độ Bám Trong Mô Hình Không Bám

So what's the big problem that you have to solve if you have this business?

**Number one** — and this is the nasty one, but it's the reality — is that you have to find a way to **create stickiness in a fundamentally unsticky model**. Meaning, how do we create **revenue retention** in an education business?

Here are a couple of tactical strategies for this.

### Chiến Thuật: Yếu Tố Tiêu Thụ Định Kỳ

**Strategy 1:** You want to have some component of the business that's **consumption-based**.

If we were to teach someone a **core skill**, there's going to be some components that need to be refreshed. If I teach you how to run ads, you're going to need something on a regular basis. What's that? **Creative**.

**Example —** maybe I sell you a **one-time price** that's significantly more expensive on how to run ads, and then my **recurring price** for the creative might be lower.

A different version: *"I'll teach you this education, but you probably want a community to be with other people on."* That's what School.com is about.

### Vấn Đề Định Giá Community So Với Education

**Key insight:** People want community, but **community is less valuable than education**.

Where people get into trouble: - They charge **$25,000** for education — appropriate
- They make a payment plan: **$2,000/month**
- The **first payment** is amazingly worth it — they just got the full education upfront
- But once they have the knowledge, **the value of the course drops to approximately zero**
- Now they're paying **$2,000/month** for a community, which is not worth that price **The math problem —** the day before you know how to do math, a math course is unbelievably valuable. The day after you know how to do math, the value of that course is approximately zero.

**Application:** When designing a product offering — especially in education — think about which components are **consumed on a regular basis** vs. which are **one-time**.

- **Consumable:** Community, weekly trend reports, recurring research deliverables
- **One-time:** Core skill education, foundational curriculum

**Example —** If I taught someone 3D printing and delivered *"the hot trending items this week"* every single week — that's **consumable**. We have to identify what elements of the business are consumable and what elements are one-time.

- **Promoter** (really excited, super promotional, doesn't like details, loves promoting) → **E-commerce** or **Education**
- **Exceptionally skilled + great teacher** → **Service business**
- **People person** (loves people, loves building culture, loves developing and leading) → **Service business** **On promoters:** Good promoters do really well in **e-commerce** and **education**. You still have to have a good product — like all businesses — but these tend to be more **marketing-heavy**.

**On culture vs brand:** If you love building **culture** — which is the **internal brand** of the business — you have to get many people to behave in a specific way.

- **Brand** — how you do that to *customers*
- **Culture** — how you do that to *employees*

**On service businesses:** If you're somebody who loves people, this is a business you want to be in — you're always going to have people around you. If you love **developing people**, love **pouring into people**, like **leading people** — *people* being the main word for all of this.

The three other business models have far fewer people — for sure there are people, but far fewer than a **service business**. Service businesses — you've got to **love people** in order to do them.

That should give you a little bit of a mix of which one might be right for you and what path you might be on.
