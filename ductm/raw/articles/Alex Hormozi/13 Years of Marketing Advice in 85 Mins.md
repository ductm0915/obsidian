I built and sold nine companies. I sold my last company for $46.2 million to American Pacific Group. I've also written two books on marketing that have sold over a million copies. Here's 13 years of marketing advice that will make you more money.

## 1. Start with Low Prices or Free

Start with low prices, or even — gasp — free. The reason that I say that is because if you start there, then you can go up. It's very difficult to come down.

In the beginning, you want to get flow through the system, and so it's totally fine to start it free and then start bumping up from there. I'll give you three quick examples, and then I'll show you how to do this tactically.

When I started my first ever business, it was an online personal training business — this is 13-plus years ago. When I started, I didn't know anything, and I was so afraid to charge people money to begin with. But I did want them to pay something, because I wanted them to be invested — otherwise people don't follow through with their workouts or their nutrition plans, etc.

So I said, "Hey, just donate to the charity of your choice — whatever charity you want to — in exchange, I will then train you for 12 weeks." People would donate. I think it was $500 or $1,000, was their choice, to a charity of their selection. By doing that, I actually got my first 13 or so customers just by making posts and DMing people that I knew.

It didn't feel weird because I was like, "Hey, I'm doing this kind of nonprofit thing. I want to help you out if you want to lose weight, and you can feel good about it — and it's a write-off."

We were like, *"Oh, this is pretty cool."* But the thing is, that was the first time I ever did it. Then from there I said, *"Huh, would you guys be willing to pay me now, after we finish this 12 weeks?"* A lot of them were like, *"Sure."* I was like, *"Yeah, I'm the charity now."* So after I did a good job, they were willing to do that and refer me customers. The next business that I did this with was Gym Launch, when I actually started flying out to other gyms. I used to do turnarounds, and I did that for two-plus years.

I'd fly out to a gym, set up all the pricing, do the marketing — all that stuff — and I did it all for free.

I did it that way because I didn't know how I could sell those gym owners. The deal that I made was: I only make money if I make sales, and I'll front the cash for the marketing. So they took zero risk on.

I would do everything — including pay for my hotel, my flights, my food, my rental car, everything. But being able to do that, it allowed me to get way more yeses quickly, test out the system in 30-plus markets, which then eventually led me to licensing that system to 5,000-plus locations. That was the company that I sold to APG.

The third time that I did this was when I started my software company, Allen. When I started the company, I was like, "I need to get some case studies. I need some testimonials.

I need some stories to show successes." I think a lot of people in the beginning think this is only a beginner thing, but even if you're a big business owner — when you start out a new product or a new division, starting with free customers just makes a lot of sense.

**It's for four reasons:** 1. Free customers become paying customers after the period, as long as you do a good job — so it makes money in one way.

2. They'll also refer you customers, because if you do a great job, they'll just send you other people — and that can be contingent on why you do it for free for them.

3. They'll leave you testimonials and reviews, so those reviews and those testimonials also help you advertise, and you can get more customers from them.

4. Whenever you do stuff for free, I do it in exchange for feedback. I say, *"Hey, tell me what I could do to make this better."* It's not saying, *"Tell me all the ways I suck."* It's just saying, "Tell me how I can make this better. Tell me how I could make it worth it for you to want to stay and continue to pay."

Ideally, the easy test of knowing whether or not this works is that they actually stay and pay afterwards. It's the easiest way to get going on any new product. I think so many people start trying to sell stuff too soon without enough proof.

In the beginning, you suck — that's why it's a new product, you're a new division. I'd rather not ruin my reputation. Say I'm giving away for free, give the reason: "I'm brand new at this thing," or *"I'm trying this new thing out."* That way I'll get a little bit of grace, and I can make the thing better in an iterative process much faster, with way more people, because I'm giving away for free or at cost.

Once I get those massive success stories — that they feel like they owe me because I've done a great job — then I can market those and go at full price.

**How to do this tactically:** Going from starting at low prices to moving all the way to where you want to be, which is high prices with big premiums. What I'd say is: after you have your first test case study batch — call it 10 or 20 people depending on the size of your product or your services — after that, every five, I just go up by 20%.

Let's say I started at zero and I have a marketing agency. I might say, *"Okay, the next five I'm going to do for a thousand bucks."* Then from there, *"The next five I'm going to do for $1,200."* The next five, *"$1,500."* You just keep going up by 20% until the conversion rate times the price goes down. That way you can just calculate the total amount of money made off of X number of sales calls, and you'll be able to see, "Okay, here's the sweet spot where I sell the most amount of units at the highest potential price."

My company Gym Launch, that I sold for $46 million — at its peak, before I was selling it, we were taking home over a million dollars a month in personal income. But that's what a lot of people think happened the whole time. It wasn't like that.

The actual very first business that I started in fitness — I actually gave away my services for free. I did it as a nonprofit for charity, so that I could get clients in the door and test out whether my stuff actually worked, and that I could deliver services.

When you're having the conversation with those first few people — whether you're doing it for free or for a big discount — you say, "Hey, I plan on going to this price point, but I'm willing to do it for free in exchange for X," which is usually feedback for me and a public testimony if you're comfortable doing it. By having those two things: one, you guarantee that you get the feedback, which is the thing you need most; but you also can get the testimony that you can leverage. Then finally, you can also tack on a referral if you want to.

Again, I'd say the testimony and the referral should be contingent on you doing a good job. You say, *"Hey, if I do a good job, would you be willing to do these two things?"* If they say yes, then you're good to go.

**The fourth one:** which is: do they actually want to buy at full price later? — that will be the real test of how good you were. The bottom line here is that when you start out, you're not that good. Even if your ego is kind of convincing you that you are good — which I think is silly — just imagine two years from now, you're going to be much better than you are now. Well, that guy is probably way more experienced, so give that guy a running start by starting free now and then building momentum into it.

I have this saying that I repeat all the time in my team, which is: **"Create flow, monetize flow, then add friction."** You have to run water through the machine. You have to get flow into the system to see where it's going to break, so that you know what real actually looks like — rather than just trying to get rich on Excel.

## 2. More Better New

**The framework that I use for scaling advertising or scaling marketing in any niche:** **more, better, new**. When you're starting out, more is almost always the answer.

I remember when I was thinking about getting into content, I paid someone who had made a lot more content, had a much bigger brand. I said, *"Hey, what should I be doing? What should I do to make my content better?"* He's like, "Dude, you don't need to make your content better.

You're just not making anything."

What he did was: he pulled up his profile on LinkedIn and he pulled up my profile on LinkedIn. He was like, *"I posted five times a day. You haven't even posted."* Then he pulled up Instagram — he's like, *"I posted two times a day. You posted once a week."* He just went platform by platform and showed that he was doing 3x, 5x, 10x the volume that I was. He was like, *"Dude, you just got to do more."* That was embarrassing, because I just paid all this money to have someone just look at me and say *"do more."* But hopefully I can save you the money and just tell you: you probably need to do more.

The interesting thing about this is that, later on — at the point when we had maybe five-ish million subscribers across all platforms — I had a friend of mine who's a billionaire who wanted to start getting into personal branding. He said, "Hey man, can you check out my Instagram and see what's going on, and why we're not growing as fast as I want to?" I said, "Well, before I look at it — how many posts a week are you making across all platforms?" He's like, *"Oh, got that nailed. I'm posting once a day."* I was like, *"Dude, we post 50-plus times a day."* He was like, *"Got it."* He just was like, *"You don't need to say anymore. I understand."* He had enough experience in other realms to know that all of the things in volume that work in sales, that work in customer success, that work in product in terms of iterations — the same concepts apply to advertising and marketing. You just need to do a lot in order to figure out what works, so that you can do better later.

This also applies on the paid side. I remember there was a chiropractor agency that approached me. They were doing about $2 million a year, and he was like, *"Hey man, I think I've saturated Facebook."* I was like, *"What do you mean?"* He's like, *"I just think I've saturated the chiropractor niche."* I was like, "This is a $10-billion-a-year industry, and you feel like your $2 million per year agency has saturated Facebook?"

I was like, *"Do you know of any other chiropractor agencies that exist?"* He's like, *"Yeah, there's loads — hundreds of them."* I was like, "Okay, so explain to me why every sale that is keeping all of them in business is somehow not sales that you should be making." He was like, *"Got it."* You're spending $40,000 a month when, in aggregate, all the agencies that are selling to chiropractors are probably spending $4 million a month. All of the spend that those businesses are able to make in your market is your opportunity — if you can outcompete them, you can provide better service, you can have longer **LTV**, which means you can spend more to acquire the customer than they can. Then you can eventually price them out of the marketplace.

Because fundamentally, paid ads is actually an auction for attention. People bid against each other for ad space. If you can outbid your competition, you can have an ethical monopoly by literally bidding more than everyone else while still making money.

That's what you fundamentally want to build in terms of the mousetrap of your business: how can I make more money from every customer than anyone else does, so that I can spend more than anyone else can to acquire them?

**I had a portfolio company CEO:** good marketer — come up to me and say, "Hey man, I think we really need to scale our ads." I was like, "I agree. Well, how many pieces of creative are you putting out per week?" He's like, "Oh, I just record once a month." I was like, "Okay fine, then how many ads are you making per session?" He was like, "We do about 15 — so about 15 new ads per month." I just kind of looked at him. I was like, "Dude, when I was trying to go from your size to five times your size, I was making 35 a week."

Nowadays we make way more than that, in terms of content and in terms of the ads that we do. The big saying that I have — that's plastered on the wall — that actually applies to sales and marketing is: **"Volume negates luck."** If you don't want to rely on luck and rely on one of those 10 ads or five ads that you make to be amazing, you just make more ads.

I can't tell you the amount of times that it just reset the bar. The amount of volume that the people who are ahead of you are doing is not 2x or 3x more — it's oftentimes 100x more. Even the 35 times 4 — 140 pieces — the guy was making 15.

It was 9x the volume of what he was doing. Some of you guys are making five ads a month or one ad a month, and thinking that that's somehow going to be sufficient to get to your goals.

**When to shift from "more" to "better":** Once you've done more, at what point do you start shifting to better? The way you figure that out is when the return on effort for "better" exceeds one incremental unit of "more." Meaning: if I've got one sales guy, and I add a second sales guy, I have a high degree of certainty that I can probably double my sales by doing that. But when you have 10 sales guys, adding one more is the same level of effort as going from 1 to 2, but it's only a 10% increase.

So if I can increase the overall throughput of my entire team by 20% or 25% by tweaking conversion rates, doing a headline split test, or changing CTAs on my ads — those are things that would make me more money per unit of effort. In the beginning, you usually just need to blow as much volume through there as possible. That's the **"create flow, monetize flow, then add friction"** approach.

The optimization steps really only matter when you have flow going through.

You do more first. Then, once you're big enough, better becomes a better return on effort. But once you go through the better steps, you usually have to go back to: "Okay, how do we do 10 times more than we are now?

How do we go from 10 sales guys to 100 sales guys?" That's the thinking that you have to go through in order to start scaling in terms of orders of magnitude.

When you have so much volume, you're able to iterate so much faster. Even us recently — we started making more content on YouTube, and we already noticed as a team: *"Oh, this is awesome. We're learning so much faster."* We're literally putting out four or five videos a week instead of one video a week, and we're learning about headlines and thumbnails.

There's less pressure per video because we're like, *"Cool, learned that. Let's move on to the next one."* It allows you to iterate so much faster without getting your ego and your emotions tied into it.

**Here's how I do it:** I ask the question, "What would stop me from 10x-ing the business right now?" If the answer is nothing, then we 10x the business. Most times, there is something that would break. So as soon as we identify what is the first thing that would break if we 2x, 5x, 10x — we then isolate that thing, put all of our focus towards fixing it, and then we do the increase in volume. That is how we continue to de-constrain, until we go to the next bottleneck, de-constrain that, and then grow into the next bottleneck. We have a fundamental belief that all businesses will continue to grow until their point of constraint, and then they will grow no further. You identify the constraint, relieve the constraint, and allow it to grow again. Growth occurs as an after-effect of the inputs you put into the system.

**When to try something "new":** Once you've done as much as you can more, and you've done it as well as you can better, when you can't do any more or any better — you start trying something new. The reason I have "new" last is because the cost of change of doing something new is guaranteed, but the return on something new is not.

The vast majority of entrepreneurs want to spend 80% of their time on doing new stuff, when really they should spend 90% of their time doing more and better of what is already working for them. The only time you do new stuff is when nothing is working. When you're starting as an entrepreneur, everything's new, and you keep trying and keep trying until something works.

Once it works, you have to unlearn the whole experimentation idea and say, "Great, I need to do as much as I possibly can, and do it as well as I possibly can." It's a different mindset, but that's how you start scaling.

At what point do you start doing something new? Well, if you've already said, "Okay, I can't do more for a very clear reason," or there's a big incremental cost past a certain barrier. Let's say you're a local gym and you're spending $10,000 a month on ads — you're reaching every single person in your radius multiple times a week on one platform.

So you're like, "Okay, in order for me to scale this, it's not really about doing more or even doing that better — I've saturated the eyeballs here." Well, that's when doing something new will yield a higher return.

That's where you're like, "Okay, maybe I'm going to start doing outbound. Or I'll start doing the paid ads but just on another channel — YouTube ads, Google search ads, display ads, Direct Mail, radio." There's a number of things you could do in terms of other channels, but they would be new.

**Here's the thought process:** if you could double the sales of your company and it would take a year, would you do it? If you had to invest half a year's profits in order to get your business to double — would you do it? Yes, that'd probably be a really good return on investment. You put half a year's profits in, and you double the business — you're getting a 200% return. That's awesome.

The thing is, people start something new, they spend one month of ad spend, and they say, *"Hmm, it didn't work. I lost money. I'm going to stop."* But the reality is that it's going to take you 3 months, 6 months, sometimes 12 months to get that return to actually pay off.

What I mark is progress, not the dollar. It's like: "Okay, first, are we getting clicks? Okay, we're getting clicks.

Are we getting opt-ins? Okay, we're getting opt-ins. Are the opt-ins the right type of people that we're looking for?

Yes — or if they're not, then we start tweaking the messaging, we start tweaking the funnel until we get the right people. Now we have the right people who are opting in. Great.

Can we get them on the phone? Can we get them in person? Can we get them to show up to some sort of conversion event, some sales event that we can then try and sell them something?"

You keep walking the dollar over the bridge until eventually it comes to the other side. That takes money, that takes time, and that takes learning — but it's a return on investment. Because if you do double your lead flow in a year: one, it doubles the business; two, it more than doubles the value of the business, because now you have two different ways to get customers.

It makes the business more stable, because if one goes down, you still have the other one.

## 3. How to Do Better (Optimize Front to Back)

This is double-clicking into the "better" concept, because this is especially for the bigger business owners. If you're at a few million dollars a year to tens of millions a year, this is really going to start paying massive dividends.

**Number one: Optimize front to back.** When it comes to marketing, that's the 80/20. David Ogilvy said famously, "After you've written your headline, you've spent 80 cents of your advertising dollar." He knew that 80% of people aren't going to do anything past reading the headline, and so he'd put 80% of his effort towards those first few words.

The really experienced marketers who are watching this are nodding their heads right now. The first 5 seconds, the headline, the packaging — in my history of the 13 years that I've been doing this — has only increased in importance. I haven't even gotten to a sweet spot; they just continue to become more and more important in my mind, because it's the frame through which everyone contextualizes what they're about to consume.

One, it increases the likelihood they consume it. Two, it introduces the frame that they consume the advertisement or the promotion through.

**We now have such great data:** even across the content side — that if we have two different thumbnails, you can literally see the average view duration go up by two or three minutes sometimes, simply by changing the thumbnail that people click before they watch the video. It's wild. How people consume the content — the frame — has a massive impact on how effective it is.

**The Dean/Larry King story:** A friend of mine, Dean, had this massive advertising campaign. He had written a new book and went on Larry King Live. He wanted to use the Larry King interview to advertise the book. He shot the whole interview, basically wanted Larry King to introduce him in a cool way. He knew that the ad was killer, so he ran it — $250,000 — and nothing came back. He was like, "What's going on? I know this ad's a killer."

So he went back through his old history of ads, looked at the top performing ads he'd ever done, and as soon as he started watching, he realized that he missed the hook — the hook was wrong. So he flew Larry King back out, paid him again, set up a whole new set again — just to re-record the first 30 seconds. They re-recorded the first 30 seconds with the hour-long interview, then reran the infomercial — and did $100 million in sales.

When I heard that story, I was like, *"This is why the first 5 seconds, the headline, the hook is so important."* That's what I spend now a disproportionate amount of my time on.

I do 10 times the effort on the first 5 seconds. 10 times. Actually 10 times. If I have an hour, I would spend 55 minutes on the first 5 seconds and then five minutes on everything after that.

That's the proportion of how much time I'm spending on just getting that first part right.

**Content example:** I was invited to this content creator thing with all the top guys — probably 10 guys there. The lowest guy in the room was me, and the next lowest was at 10 million-plus subscribers on YouTube only. One of the guys had about 15 million subscribers, and he was pissed that another content creator had basically copied his video — and the copied video had done better, like three times more: 30 million views versus his 10 million views.

The main guy there, who had several hundred million subscribers across all his channels, was like, *"Let's pop it in. Let's roast it."* They press play on the video — 5 seconds in, he stops the video. He's like, "Dude, you didn't confirm the thumb.

The headline and the first five seconds weren't confirmed." He was like, "I don't need to see anymore," and he just walked out of the room and got lunch.

This guy had spent weeks making this thing, obsessing about every detail of it — he had a 20-page-plus brief on how he was making this video — and he missed the first 5 seconds. The guy with the multiple hundred million subscribers was like, "That's it," and just walked out.

Recently, at a micro level: one of our portfolio companies — this is an 8-figure-plus business — we simply changed the headline on the landing page. We did nothing else. Same ads, just changed the headline.

We increased booking rate by 62%. The opt-in rate remained the same, but simply because of the frame of the promise in the headline, 62% more people chose to book calls for that business. It just shows you that how you frame what people are then going to consume has a massive impact on how much value they perceive and how likely they are to take the next step.

**How to optimize 80/20, front to back:** Assume that they have no idea who you are, what you do, how it works. They're in a rush, and they have a third-grade education. **Clear beats clever. Deletion beats explanation.** You want to try and make it as short as humanly possible. Then, once you have it as short as possible, put it into a third-grade reading calculator, look at the grade level, and keep editing it down until it's third grade.

**You're not going to attract dumber people:** you're going to make it accessible to everyone. A smart person needs to use less brainpower to understand simple language, and it makes it understandable to everyone else. As a reminder, 30% of people can barely read. It has nothing to do with intelligence — it has everything to do with how they're educated, which may have no connection with their purchasing power.

**Tactically:** I make about 50 hooks every time I start recording ads. I have 50 hooks that I've written down that I think are awesome, then I have three to five meat pieces of an advertisement, and then I do one to three CTAs (call to actions) at the end. That means I have way more on the front end in terms of effort compared to the 3-to-5 and the 1-to-3 that I do on the back. That's because I know that's where all the effort needs to go — because that's where all the returns are.

This is why I say you optimize advertising specifically from front to back: it's very rare that you're going to be able to double or triple or quadruple the amount of throughput you get in a funnel by optimizing the back end. If you have a 50% show-up rate and you get it to 75%, that's massive — that's a 50% jump. But I can absolutely get CTRs from 1% to 4% by just optimizing the hell out of the front end — and that's a 4x increase.

That's four times the throughput that goes through the entire funnel.

**Call outs:** From page 136 of my book — a call out is whatever you do to get the attention of your audience. Call outs go from hyper-specific, to get one person's attention, to not at all specific, to get everyone's attention.

If someone drops a tray of dishes at a party, everyone looks. If a child yells "Mom," then the moms look. If someone says your name, only you look.

But they all get attention. I try to make my call outs as specific as possible to get the right people, but broad enough to get as many of them as I can.

Pay close attention to advertisers' call outs, especially the ones targeting your audience. What I'll do is look at all the ads from other competitors who are targeting the same audience as me. The ads that are running for a very long time — I know that call out is working well. I will write down all the call outs that I see, and then I will write out the words that I know my customers go by. I'll either start with a problem that I think they're struggling from, or a question that I know they'll say "yes" to, or I'll simply state: "Moms in Nevada," "Homeowners in Clark County" — very clearly calling out, *"This is for you."* **That's exactly what you want the person to say when they read the headline or hear the call out:** *"This is for me."* The more specific you get, oftentimes you'll be surprised — you think that going as broad as you can is going to increase the number of opt-ins, but when you get really specific about the type of customer you're really trying to attract, you will often find that they're like, "Oh, I wouldn't have responded to the broad thing, but this one's just for me." You get more people in terms of qualified leads in the door by being more specific.

## 4. LTV to CAC Is the Only Thing That Matters

If I had only one metric that I could find out about a business — if I only had one that I could pick — it would be the **LTV to CAC** ratio.

Let me tell you a quick story. A friend of mine owns a bow manufacturing business. They do about $23 million a year — relatively large, established business. He was like, *"Hey, we're trying to get into paid ads."* They had all these distribution relationships, but they wanted to open up this new channel. He says, *"Hey, you're the marketing guy — what would you do here?"* As I was explaining the strategy, he was like, *"Well, what's the benchmark I should be looking at in terms of leads?"* I was like, "Dude, the benchmark is irrelevant. Everything just comes back to what's **gross profit**."

**So:** what's the average gross profit on a bow? Say it's $500. If we get leads for $20 and we know that we can get one in five leads to buy a bow, then our **CAC** is $100, and our gross profit from selling each bow is $500. So it costs us $100 to make $500 in gross profit — 5:1. Awesome. So fundamentally, is $20 a good lead cost? Yeah, he's getting 5:1. If it was $10, he'd be getting 10:1, assuming the same ratios.

It just depends on what percentage of customers you convert and at what price point you're converting them at.

**LTV** (or **LTGP**, to be more specific) — lifetime value is just the amount of gross profit you make from a customer over the lifetime of them being a customer of your business. **CAC** is the cost to acquire a customer, which is the all-in cost including sales, marketing, software — everything to get someone to give you money. The **LTV to CAC** ratio is simply how much money it costs you to make more money. It's the foundational economic unit of any business.

**Making sure that you know how much money it costs you to make money:** that fundamentally feels like a very important metric in order to scale the business. Over time, as long as you don't have some big capital expenses, that fundamental return that you get will reflect the compounding growth of the business, provided you don't have other constraints to growth.

**Example:** If I have meals that I sell for $10 and it costs me $9 to package, ship, and deliver them, then I have $1 of gross profit per meal. If someone buys 10 meals from me, I'm not making $100 — I'm making $10 in gross profit. If the average person buys five weeks of meals, then I would make $50 in lifetime gross profit. The only way I would want to acquire customers is if the cost to acquire a customer is probably $15 or less — because you can't do it at even, because then there's no money left over.

**Rule of thumb:** You want to have at least 3:1 on LTV to CAC. But again, LTV is not lifetime revenue — it's lifetime gross profit. That's what people mess up a lot of times: they don't even know what their gross profit is, and then they spend blindly.

**Starbucks example:** They make about $14,000 in LTV per customer. So they can spend absurd amounts of money to get customers in the door. Even if it costs them $500, they're still making 28:1 on the money they put in. That is why that company's been able to scale to the moon in terms of the number of locations.

**All the money that I've made in my life:** the crazy money — has come from crazy LTV to CAC ratios. The smallest ratios that I've made crazy money on is 30:1, all the way up to 100:1 to 200:1 returns in some of the businesses I have. Those windows don't stay forever, and when you do have one of those, you just want to dump as much money into that machine as you possibly can. A lot of wealth is made in very short, punctuated periods and then sustained by long periods of maintenance, where you're getting 5:1, 8:1, 10:1. Those are fine numbers.

Most of the time, when you're at 10:1, as soon as you start scaling it, it might drop to 5:1. The bigger the ratio is at the beginning, the more indication it is that I can scale profitably. If I'm spending $1,000 a day and I can get 100:1, then I know that I'm probably going to be able to get to $100,000 a day and get 5:1.

I care about absolute return, not relative return — but the LTV to CAC ratio gives me an indication of how much room I have to blow this thing out of the water.

**How to calculate your lifetime gross profit:** Look at all the customers that you sold over the last year. Look at gross profit. (If you don't know what gross profit is — it's the price you charge minus the cost of goods sold. If it's services, it's the labor used to provide the service. If it's a widget, it's the cost of the widget and the shipping.) Divide that amount of gross profit by the total number of customers — that'll give you a broad-brush stroke of what you make per customer.

That'll always underestimate it, especially if you have a recurring revenue business. But I would always rather be on the safer side and underestimate my lifetime gross profit rather than overestimate and potentially lose money.

If I want to then take that number and say, *"Well, what am I willing to spend per customer?"* At most, you want to have a third of that number. But for me specifically, I want to make sure that whatever I collect in the first 30 days, I collect that back from customers. So if I sell something for $10 a month, people stay for 10 months, and there's 100% gross margins — I make $100 in gross profit.

I want to see if I can find a way to make $10 back, or get my CAC back in that first 30 days. Sometimes it means having a one-time upcharge up front, so they can offset the cost of acquisition, and then the remainder of the customer relationship can be profitable.

## 5. My Ad Creation Process

I've been running ads for 13 years — and when I say running ads, I mean I've been making ads or directly influencing ads that have been run across my portfolio or companies that I directly owned or founded, for 13 straight years. I have this process pretty nailed.

**Step 1: Always be collecting data.** If I see ads on Instagram, I see ads on YouTube — I have no premium accounts on any platform, because I want to hear the ads. I want to hear the hooks. I want to hear what they're doing to draw attention. If you are your avatar, the ads count double for you. I screenshot, I record the stuff that I find interesting, and I add it into one folder.

**Step 2: The ad creation session.** I do this once a week. I sit down first thing in the morning, and I do these the same day I record — that's a piece of advice that has worked well for me. Like cramming for a test, I cram for my ad sessions, because then everything's top of mind as I go into the day.

**I pull up all my swipe files:** all the things I want as inspiration. The second thing I pull up is all my historical best-performing ads. I look back one year, two years, three years — sometimes at all my best ads — and I drink those back in. I watch the first 10 seconds, the first 30 seconds. I see: okay, how was I capturing? What was the angle? What was the hook?

**Step 3: Hook creation.** Now, 40 of my 50 hooks that I'm going to write out are going to be stuff that is tried and true. I'm not going to try and reinvent the wheel — we've already spent zillions and zillions of dollars. I know that these hooks, these entrances work well. 20% — the last 10 — is where I get to use all my inspiration and other ideas to experiment. But most people flip this: they try and do a little bit of the stuff that worked, and then they have lots of new ideas.

I'm telling you, I've done this a long time — it's way more efficient to just do more of what worked, and then leave a little bit of experimentation so that, if some of those things stop working over time, you have this fresh batch that you've been trying out that can then become the control that you try and beat.

**So:** 80% of your effort into the hooks, 80% of your effort goes into stuff that's tried and true, 20% for the things that are new. I make about 50 hooks: 40 out of 50 for things that I already know have worked in the past, 10 for my new creative ideas. From there, I have my three to five angles — educational, some sort of value I'm going to deliver, some belief I want to break, some list or steps or stories to tell as the core of the ad, some proof I want to demonstrate — and then finally, a quick CTA.

The majority of my effort is all on the 50 or so hooks. 40 are recycled versions of things I already know work, and 10 are new. This sounds boring — and it is boring — but it's also what works.

**The Kaleidoscope Process:** Let's say you have a mega winner. These happen, and this is where you get the power law — that top 5% makes 95% of your income. We run all these ads to figure out which is the one ad that can sing and just crank and print money. What I do is use something I call the **Kaleidoscope process.** **Example:** I had an ad at Gym Launch that the now-president, Kale, made when he was a customer — him in front of his gym, the gym being full, his wife's next to him, and he's like: "Six months ago, I was working a full-time job in order to pay rent at this gym. My wife was pregnant, I had a two-month-old, and I had no idea how I was going to make ends meet. Then here we are six months later — the gym is full, it's completely outsourced, and I've already got an offer for $150,000 on the gym. If you've ever been worried, just take the jump. Follow the process — it works." Very simple ad, but it murdered. Absolutely crushed.

Rather than say, "Wow, that was nice. Let's try something new," we squeezed the living life out of it. Here's 10 things I did to get more out of that ad: 1.

Changed the color of the backgrounds/backdrops 2. Introduced different props — visuals, physical props 3. Reenacted it — just literally did the same ad again, different t-shirt 4.

Reordered the ad — changed the sequence up 5. Added visual filters — black and white, sepia, high contrast, low contrast 6. Added visual effects — lower thirds, annotations 7. Changed fonts and captions 8. Changed the pacing/speed of the video (1.2x or 1.5x, or slow down at portions) 9. Added music 10. Used the same hook with a different body — re-recorded with the winning hook All of those are different ways to take a winner and then 10x or 100x how much that winner gets you. If you take one thing from this video for advertising: **squeeze the living hell out of the winners.** ---

## 6. The Four Ways to Advertise

There are only four ways that you can let other people know about your stuff. Think about this like a four-box. You've got one-to-one conversations and one-to-many (which is broadcasts).

Those are the two styles of communication: you either communicate to a group of people or one person individually. You're either talking to people who know who you are, or you're talking to strangers.

If you cross that, you've got four ways: 1. **Warm Outreach** — one-to-one to people who know you 2. **Cold Outreach** — one-to-one to strangers 3. **Content** — one-to-many to people who know you 4. **Ads** — one-to-many to strangers Those are the only four things that you can do to advertise, to let people know about your stuff. If you're not spending your day doing one of those core four things, you are not advertising. You are not marketing your business.

Sometimes it gets to that simple. Look at how many hours per day you're spending letting people know about your stuff. Most people spend zero.

Zero per day. And then for some reason, they're curious as to why their business isn't growing. Well — no one knows you exist.

**I was at a conference:** Gym Con — recently, and I asked the audience, "Who here wants to grow their gym faster?" Everybody raised their hand. These are all small businesses — most of them doing $500,000 to $3 million a year. Normal one-location, three-location, five-location owner-operators of service-based training businesses.

I said, "Okay, who here is spending the first four hours of their day promoting their business — either doing cold outreach, making content, or running ads? Who is spending the first four hours every day on that?" Literally no one in the audience had their hand raised. And I was like, *"This is why you're small. This is it."* If you're making less than, call it, a million — maybe $3 million a year — all of your effort, the first four hours of your day (if you have to wake up early, awesome; if you have to stay up late, awesome — I don't care), you need four hours that you're dedicating to promotion. You've got to do the job of promoting, and then you've got to run your business. But if you're just running your business, you're never going to get ahead.

If you want it to grow, you have to force it to grow. You have to force people to find out about your stuff, and you do that through advertising.

**Scaling framework (back-of-napkin):** - **$0 to 6 figures:** Sell one product to one avatar on one channel. That's it. No shiny objects, no multiple channels, no trying to scale yourself out of it.

- **6 to 7 figures:** Sell one product to one avatar on one channel — consistently. In the beginning, you're super volatile. When you get to seven figures, it's more consistent.

- **7 to 8 figures:** One avatar, one channel, two products. That means you have an upsell — you sell something else — and you do that consistently. That's what gets you to $10 million a year.

- **Multiple 8 figures:** Two channels, one avatar, two products — consistently.

Can you find somebody who disobeys this pattern? Sure — AG1 has one product, one avatar, a zillion channels. But by and large, the vast majority of businesses follow this structure.

You'll notice that scaling up to eight figures, and even sometimes a little beyond that, you're still only focused on one avatar.

## 7. State the Facts and Tell the Truth

This is probably one of the most repeated phrases, besides **"volume negates luck,"** that I have internally to my business.

I remember at that same little meetup with the super creators, I was talking to the guy who had the most subscribers there. I was like, *"Yeah, we don't really like this whole clickbait title thing."* He looked at me with disgust. He was like, *"You absolutely make clickbait titles."* He said, "You just back it up with truth.

So it's crazy clickbaity — but it also happens to be true."

When he said that to me, I was like, *"That's real."* It's funny because that was a concept I had in terms of paid ads, but I hadn't thought about it in terms of content. Sometimes you have to relearn the same lessons, which I hate doing — but I did it anyways.

**The problem:** Most businesses don't track results. If you don't track, you don't care. They don't track partially because of ignorance — they know they don't deliver good results, and if they track it, they'll have facts and truth proving they're delivering bad results.

But the first step is tracking it, because once you start tracking it, you can improve it. **Measurement as intervention** is actually a scientifically proven method — to lose weight, to improve sales, to improve anything you want. Simply tracking it — not trying to do anything else — simply tracking your weight, your close rate, your ad performance — will improve it, because you're just putting attention on it.

Once you do start tracking it, you can start doing things to actually improve it beyond just measurement as intervention. Lo and behold, your stats get better. Then you can **state the facts and tell the truth** — and have that truth be wildly compelling.

**Real talk:** part of the reason that Gym Launch, to this day, has still not been beaten or dethroned as the largest company in that space, is because we still have better stats and better results than anyone else does. The average gym who goes through 12 months of Gym Launch makes over $100,000 a year in added profit by using the systems we have. That's just what it is. If it makes sense for somebody to triple their money, then they make the purchase. I like to take a lot of emotion out of the sale: "If this return makes sense for you, then do it. If it doesn't, then don't."

What about the bottom 20%? The nice thing is that we track that too. The bottom 20% averaged $40,000 in additional revenue — so they basically broke even.

That's the bottom. The top 20% did $500,000-plus per year.

If you track the stats, then you can promote the stats — and you can do so truthfully. They go from unsubstantiated claims to substantiated claims.

**Tactical steps:** 1. **Collect data first.** Implement a data step where you collect data from people: Day 1, Day 30, Day 60, Day 90, at the end of the program — whatever your implementation is. Track data throughout.

2. **Keep improving things until the data becomes compelling.** 3. **Present the data.** There are four variables that matter: - **Who** — the percentage of people (median, average, or overall percentage) - **What** — achieve X outcome - **When** — in Y time or after X attempts - **Conditions** — under Z conditions The fewer conditions you have, the more compelling it is.

## 8. Say What Only You Can Say, Show What Only You Can Show

If you're the only triple black belt in Ohio for Muay Thai, then say that you're the only triple black belt in Ohio for Muay Thai.

This is talking about the things that you have done, rather than telling people what to do. It's "how I" versus *"how to."* My concept for making amazing ads and amazing content is two steps: **do epic stuff, talk about what you did.** You can out-outcome people, or you can outwork them. It starts with the outworking, and then the outworking creates the out-outcoming.

The out-outcoming is a much shorter headline, but the outworking still works. I can start by saying, *"Hey, I wrote these two books."* Writing two books would be an outworking thing — anyone could do it, there's no outcome. But then, if the books are good, I get the outcome: we sold over a million copies, they're still number one and number two in marketing to this day, multiple years later.

First you do the work, and then you get the outcome. Most people want to say epic stuff having done none of it.

Everybody wants a return on their time. I remember hearing this concept from Airrack, who's a YouTuber. He said, "Dude, I made this thing where I went on 100 dates.

I basically did an entire season of The Bachelor, but I did it in 16 minutes. What a steal." When he said that to me, I was like, *"Man, such an interesting concept."* He took something that was really long and made it really short, and he crammed it in there — so **value per second** was super high.

**It's not about seconds of value:** it's **value per second**. That's what matters. Everybody has access to infinite information. You just saying, "I'm going to give you more stuff," is not helpful. What you want to do is curate it, pack it, distill it, and crystallize all the value in as tight a package as humanly possible, so that their value per second skyrockets.

You can do that by saying, "Hey, I spent 20 hours making a summary of this book, and you can read the whole thing in 10 minutes." That's why summaries always still work — because people want to get a deal on their time. If they get the same result for a fraction of the time, it's always a good deal. By the way, if you ever want to sell to rich people: just promise the same result in half the time for twice the price.

**Agency example:** If I had a marketing agency and I wanted to show what only I can show, I'd want to demonstrate. Instead of saying, "Hey, I'm going to get you leads," I'd get on a sales call and play a live lead that I got for one of my clients. I'd say, "Hey, listen to this call. It's a minute long. Do you think you could take 50 of these a month?" Wow — so much more compelling than saying, "I'll just get you 50 leads." They're actually getting a taste of what it looks like to be in the future. They're experiencing the result vicariously through the other person.

**Software example:** This is why demos are so effective. "Hey, you have a video clipping software? Great. Do you have a video on you? Cool, send it to me. We're on the phone. Great. I'm going to pull this video up. This is what we do to put the captions on, shorten, and find those moments — click, click, click. Okay, normally how long does that take you? Well, it takes me this long. Well, watch me do it — click, click, click. Wow, would that make your life easier? Yes? Cool — boom." They go in. You're demonstrating in advance.

**Gym cleaning supplies story:** When I was at my gym in Huntington Beach — my first location — I had a door-to-door sales guy who came up. This guy was a clear sales pro. He was really good. He sold me cleaning products — probably the only door-to-door thing I've ever bought. He was in and out in like 13 minutes. He just said, "Hey, I've got this stuff. It'll take care of any stain that you've got on this floor."

I had turf, and there was gum that had gotten into the turf — really black, gone in there. He just took that thing, poured it on the gum, got on his hands and knees, scrubbed it — and it actually came out. The gum was impossible to get out.

I was like, *"Wow."* I didn't have to think, *"Will this work for me?"* He just did it in front of me, on a stain I actually had. He's like, "Cool, looks like you got 20 other stains. If this is worth it, it's $100 for this stuff." So I paid $100 for cleaning supplies, and I ended up using that stuff for years.

**The key point:** Step one is do stuff. Step two, talk about it. Everyone else is stuck at the step where they want to figure out what to talk about — but it's because they haven't done anything. If you do stuff, you have more than enough to talk about.

## 9. How and When to Expand Your Market

There are five ways. But big picture — the vast majority of people need to do way more with their current market.

I had a guy who came out to a workshop. He said, "Hey, I'm a coach who coaches coaches. I'm making $2 million a year.

It's a really saturated space. Should I go broader to general business?" I was like, *"Dude, how many coaches coaching coaches exist?"* He was like, *"A zillion."* I was like, "Dude, I think you just need to get better. Because if you were the best coach of coaching coaches, you would be able to take all of the market share that all of those guys have, and it would come to you.

Winner takes all if you're the best."

**Gym Launch example:** Everyone knows it's a gym company, but you don't know how niche it really is. One — we didn't work with personal trainers. Two — we didn't work (for the first four or five years) with big box facilities. We didn't work with yoga, spin, or Pilates. We literally only worked with micro gyms that did before-and-afters of weight loss and transformations. They had to do fitness and weights, or some combination. That's a niche of a niche of a niche — and that business still cranks out $30-plus million a year.

The market, if you are the best, is still massive. You just need to solve the thing that matters most: being the best.

**Five ways to expand:** 1. **Up Market** — Go to higher-value customers (health clubs, big boxes, multi-location owners, franchisors). More valuable, but fewer of them.

2. **Down Market** — Go after lower-value customers (personal trainers). More volume at a lower price. You'd need infrastructure to support that volume.

3. **Adjacent** — Serve a similar avatar but a slightly different business model (chiropractors, weight loss clinics, med spas).

4. **Narrower** — Niche down even more (only boot camps, only semi-private, only cardio).

5. **Broader** — Open up to *"anyone in health and wellness."* Despite these five ways, most of the time I'm going to start with: you can make $10, $20, $50 million a year just serving one very specific avatar. The vast majority of businesses — including more than half the portfolio companies we've purchased — we did this exercise and we made the avatar *narrower*, not broader.

**Vista Private Equity lesson:** I talked to their head of pricing and packaging. He said, "You want to know how we get the crazy returns that we do? We just know the company that we're buying's customers better than they do." They go into the data room, analyze all the customers, order them by who pays the most and who's worth the most. They say, "This is the 20% that does 80% of the revenue." Then they look at what channels those people come in on, what offers, what marketing — and they basically stop doing the rest of the 80% and expand that 20% to be 100% of the business. By doing that, they can have the same-size business but 5x the revenue.

**How to do it for you:** Look at all the customers you have up to this point. Look at the top 20%. Say, "What do these people all have in common?" Think in terms of psychographics (what do they think), geographics (where do they live), demographics (what do they look like), and actions (what have they done, what did they do to get this result). Eliminate as many variables as I can, so I get to two or three variables that clump all the best customers together. Then I make that my marketing, and I tell my sales team: "We only sell these people going forward." Because I know these people are worth 5x more, 10x more.

Then guess what happens? We run ads for those people, our LTV skyrockets, so we can spend more to acquire customers. **This is what allows you to scale by going narrower.** The problem is that most business owners can't say no to fast money — and so they never get the big money.

**"Better creates bigger. Bigger creates bloat."** If the goal is to get bigger, you want to focus not on getting bigger but on getting better — and then getting bigger occurs as a result. Growth is not a goal; growth is an outcome of inputs. You get far more leverage by doing the same thing 100 times than doing 100 things one time.

## 10. Provide Value (The Value Equation)

**Everybody talks about providing value:** but what does that even mean? In my book, I talk about something called the **value equation**. It's probably the most cited chapter in the whole book. There are four elements of value: 1. **Dream Outcome** — What does somebody actually want? Making money, for most men, is more valuable than getting in better shape. The relative outcome will dictate how much money you can charge. That's why, in general, B2B offers are higher ticket.

2. **Perceived Likelihood of Achievement** — Or the risk associated with the purchase. We want to increase the perceived likelihood as much as possible. If you have one surgeon who's his first day out of medical school, and another who's done 10,000 surgeries — which can price higher? Obviously, the 10,000 one. Same procedure, same outcome — but the perceived likelihood of you actually getting what you want is higher, and the risk is lower.

3. **Time Delay** (the equal opposite: speed) — How quickly between when someone buys and when they get the promise. Liposuction: you make the purchase, go to sleep, and wake up thin. Very condensed, which is why it has a lot of value. Versus eating right and exercising — there's a lot of delay. The faster it is, the more valuable it is.

4. **Effort and Sacrifice** (the equal opposite: ease) — How easy can you make it?

**In simple terms:** How risk-free can I make it? How easy can I make it? How fast can I make it? And is the outcome something they actually want? If you have all four — something people really want, with no risk, that comes immediately, and it's as easy as pushing a button — you've got an incredibly valuable thing.

When people say "add value," think: What does my customer want to accomplish, or what do they want to avoid? You've got "towards good stuff" and *"away from bad stuff."* Then ask: How can I help them get that good stuff — faster, easier, and risk-free?

**Where to find problems to solve:** - Comments on your content — the endless content wheel. You make content, people have questions, you make content about the questions, they have questions about those questions. Problems never end, and people never stop complaining.

- Internal to your business — what are the friction points along the way for whatever thing you sell? How can you make it easier?

## 11. Give Away the Secrets, Sell the Implementation

A fairly well-known marketing saying: **"Give away the secrets, sell the implementation."** The way I think about this is: **make your free stuff better than their paid stuff.** The real real is: you actually have to think — if I give away something for free, I have to be willing to charge for it. If you were willing to charge $100 or $1,000 or $10,000 for the thing that you give away for free — my God, must that be valuable. Yes.

And what that does is: it increases the likelihood of conversion, increases the likelihood the person consumes the thing, and then buys the next product.

A lead magnet, or content, is a complete solution to a very narrow problem. A lot of people are afraid of giving away value, but you shouldn't be. Give away all the secrets, give away all the things that you do, and then — if someone wants help with implementation, they can call you up.

This allows you to provide value to the masses, build your brand up, and then they use "results in advance" as an approximation of the results they're going to get after they purchase.

**The dietitian story:** I just recently met a lady who was doing $1 million a year in profit — from 5,800 followers on Instagram. She had no other platform. 100% sourced from Instagram. This blew my mind. When I looked at her page, it wasn't like she had crazy deep engagement either — one, five, maybe 10 comments on a great post; 10 likes, 20 likes, 30 likes on a crazy one. But 100% of her content was about how registered dietitians can more accurately bill insurance to make more money. A niche of a niche. I'll bet that of the 5,800 people that follow her, 5,700 of them were registered dietitians trying to figure out how to make more money by billing insurance.

She was selling 5 to 10 little cheat sheets a day at $99, plus two to three high-ticket people per week into her association. Deep is where the dollars are.

**My own experience:** We used to make some wider content for about six months. We got the data back — it turned out we got lots of views, but we didn't get lots of business owners. So we switched back to hardcore business stuff (which I love anyway), and because of that, all of our stats are up: more opt-ins, more people buying books, more people going to workshops, more people applying to the portfolio. All of those stats were up, despite the fact that general views per video went down. I'm here for the money, not for the fame.

**The SEO guy:** A buddy of mine runs a multi-9-figure performance marketing company, and he makes content online. He was like, "Oh, dude, my personal brand makes no money." He goes super in-depth tactical — charts, graphs, all data-supported. His content gets so few likes and engagement. But his lead quality? They're all Fortune 500, Fortune 100 CEOs and executives that are like, "Hey, you obviously know the stuff. Can we just hire you?" He gives away everything, and he says it: "You could just follow all my stuff and do it." Most people are like, "Man, this is too much work — can I just hire you?" Which is an exceptional way to get customers.

**Three steps:** 1. **Give away your best stuff.** All of it. If you have a low-ticket thing that sells to lots of people, just give it away. You're probably not making much money on it anyway. Show them: "Hey, I used to charge for this. I'm giving it to you for free because I love you."

2. **Put heavy effort into follow-up and calls to action within the content itself.** Follow up with your team to send them into the next thing, if they're qualified.

3. **Make the CTA about personalization, not about more.** Instead of saying, "If you want more stuff like this, go here," say, "If you want me to help apply this stuff to your business (or your body, or your life), then let me know." Personalizing is significantly more valuable than just more stuff.

**Pro tip:** 99% of people who consume your free thing will never buy from you. That means your reputation is getting made by the 99%, not the 1%. If you want to improve your reputation in the marketplace, make sure the stuff you give away for free is awesome. Also make sure the stuff you sell is awesome. If both of those things sound like a four-letter word (which is "work") — welcome to business.

## 12. All Advertising Works

**All advertising works:** it's just a matter of efficiency.

**The amount of times I've heard:** "Facebook ads don't work for us. Google ads don't work for us. TikTok ads don't work for us. Direct mail doesn't work for us. Radio ads don't work for us. Content doesn't work for us. Outreach doesn't work for us."

**Fundamentally:** if you have a qualified person on the other side, and you make an offer to them that solves a key problem for them, if you can get them on the phone — you can make money. Period. If you can rephrase the problem to, "I don't know how to make Facebook ads work for us," or "I don't know how to make TikTok work for us" — then that becomes a problem you can solve.

**Optimize advertising front to back; optimize the business back to front.** The back — the LTV, how much you make per customer — enables how much you can spend on the front end. You optimize the throughput on the front end, because that's where you can get the highest returns. But the engine, the amount you can spend, depends on how much you make per customer.

You can either be really exceptional at lowering CAC, or really good at increasing LTV. If you do both, you have huge arbitrage — and that's where you make stupid money.

**The $50 million celebrity example:** If I wanted to become an A-list celebrity, I've back-calculated what I believe it would cost: about $50 million. People who were no-name actors who become the star of a big Marvel hit — with $50 million in marketing budget, they went from no one knowing who they were to being famous. If you had something you sold for a billion dollars, you could spend that and still make 20:1.

**Eugene Schwartz's Five Levels of Awareness:** 1. **Unaware** — No idea who you are, what the problem is, or anything.

2. **Problem Aware** — "Having trouble sleeping at night?" *"Do you have back pain when you lean over?"* 3. **Solution Aware** — They know solutions exist, but don't know the specifics.

4. **Product Aware** — They know your specific product/option exists.

5. **Most Aware** — Past and existing customers.

Most times, smaller advertisers and beginner advertisers get returns marketing to "most aware" — emailing your list, making a post, sending an email. For almost no money. One degree above that: other people in the market who are product-aware.

You have to be a little bit better to market to those people. One level above that: solution-aware — they know there are people solving this problem, but they don't know how. You need another order of magnitude improvement of your ads and hooks to call out those people.

Most times, the problem isn't that your market isn't big enough — it's that your ads aren't good enough and they're not specific enough to hit the next massive tranche of the audience.

**"The size of the plane is directly correlated with the length of the runway."** The bigger the plane, the longer the runway. If you want to sell a very expensive thing, or sell to a very cold person — the longest sale would be someone who's completely unaware for a very expensive thing; the shortest sale would be someone who's very aware for a very cheap thing. The amount of runway, selling, and advertising you need is directly proportional to those two things.

If you're having trouble scaling, it usually means there's a mismatch — you're sending a warm message to a cold audience, or you're getting people on the phone who aren't prepared to spend a huge amount of money because they haven't been warmed up enough.

## 13. We Need to Be Reminded More Than Taught

I want to really emphasize this one because it's important.

Once you start making content, running ads, and putting stuff out publicly — you're going to get to a point where you're like, *"I feel like I've said everything that I was going to say."* My mom said this to me once, and I always remembered it: *"The news never changes — just the names."* There's always a war, there's a murder, there's a car accident — the actual story doesn't change, only the names.

If you think about that with content — there is no new content. The human condition has not changed for thousands of years. The older the problem, the older the solution.

I follow philosophy pages because I'm okay rereading Epictetus' stuff. I read it before, and I read it in quote form again. When it comes up again, I like to be reminded — I like to keep this stuff top of mind.

**Many people follow your pages for that exact same reason:** the stuff you talk about is stuff they want to keep top of mind. They want to be reminded of that stuff. Getting taught is much more difficult than being reminded — but they're equally valuable, because if it changes their behavior, then you still did teach them. You just reminded them.

**The Friends example:** The Friends show right now is being watched more now than it was a few years ago. Reruns. People watch reruns because there's nostalgia. People are willing to rewatch the same thing because they know how it ends, they know they liked it.

You can make the same thing again, because your audience who's new a year later hasn't seen that thing. If you make something new to them — even though it's quote "old" or a reminder to your existing audience — your existing audience doesn't mind their reminder, and the new people never saw it to begin with.

**How to create variety around the same topics:** Instead of trend hacking and talking about the news — talk about *your* news. What are the new things that are happening now? This is the "do epic stuff, talk about what you did." We tell new stories to explain the same concept. For that specific audience, you'll reach a different type of person or different avatar that still might be within your market — with a story about a mother and a daughter versus a father and a son versus a military vet versus a small business owner. Different avatars, same story, different name — just like the news.

**Same concept, different formats:** I explained the value equation in my book. I also have a course on my site where I explained the value equation in a different format. I'm mentioning it here now. I have it on whiteboards. I've applied it to services, education, physical products, software. You can take the same idea and think: how can I display it in new formats, new mediums, and new contexts?

**The Henry Ford story:** Henry Ford had his marketing person right next to him. He walked past the marketing department over and over again. Back in the day, they had mockups and big posters for their next ad campaign for a new Ford car. He walked past this guy's office for three months. At the end of the three months, he was like, "Hey John, when are we going to stop running this ad? I'm so sick of it." John, the marketing director, looked at him and said, "We haven't even started running it yet."

We get so much sicker of our own content before our audience even remembers our name. We assume that every person consumes every piece of content we ever put out. We put out 50-plus pieces of content a day — I'm grateful if somebody watches one a week.

We put out tons of volume with the hope that we catch one person on that one day. People need to be reminded more than they need to be taught.

## 14. Key Takeaways

1. **Start with Low Prices or Free** — Get flow through the system first, then raise prices incrementally by 20%.

2. **More Better New** — Scale by doing more of what works, then optimize (better), then try new channels.

3. **How to Do Better** — Optimize front to back: 80% of effort goes to headlines, hooks, first 5 seconds.

4. **LTV to CAC Is the Only Thing That Matters** — Aim for at least 3:1 ratio; the bigger the ratio, the more room to scale.

5. **My Ad Creation Process** — Write 50 hooks (40 proven + 10 new), 3-5 angles, 1-3 CTAs; squeeze winners with the Kaleidoscope process.

6. **The Four Ways to Advertise** — Warm outreach, cold outreach, content (1-to-many warm), ads (1-to-many cold).

7. **State the Facts and Tell the Truth** — Track results, measure everything, then promote real stats as clickbait that's backed by truth.

8. **Say What Only You Can Say** — Do epic stuff, talk about what you did; deliver value per second, not seconds of value.

9. **How to Expand Your Market** — Go up, down, adjacent, narrower, or broader — but most businesses should get narrower first.

10. **Provide Value (The Value Equation)** — Dream outcome x perceived likelihood / time delay / effort = value.

11. **Give Away Secrets, Sell Implementation** — Make free stuff better than competitors' paid stuff; deep niche content converts best.

12. **All Advertising Works** — It's about efficiency, not the channel; optimize back-to-front (LTV) to fund front-to-back (ads).

13. **We Need to Be Reminded More Than Taught** — Repeat content in new formats, stories, and contexts; your audience hasn't seen it all.
