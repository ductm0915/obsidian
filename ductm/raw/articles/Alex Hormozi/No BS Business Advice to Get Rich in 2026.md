# No BS Business Advice to Get Rich in 2026

**Source:** Alex Hormozi **Source:** Alex Hormozi **Type:** Yearly Business Recap & Lessons Learned **Context:** Hormozi shares his key business lessons, frameworks, and strategies from running a portfolio of companies, three of which have reached $100M+ valuation over 13 years in business.

## 1. Set Fewer Goals & Do Less Long-Term Planning

I've been in business for 13 years and since 2020 three of the companies I started have reached $100 million plus valuation. My first piece of advice is set fewer goals and do less long-term planning.

This may sound contradictory to some of my content about being patient. I think long-term goals are very important but the obsession over the minutia of how you're going to get there is an act of mental masturbation. You try and create these massive projections that are based on hundreds of variables that in reality you just don't know what's going to happen.

Over time I've consistently trimmed and trimmed our planning processes such that at this point I pretty much only care what we're going to do in the next 12 weeks. Beyond that we might have one target or two targets by the end of the year but we're not going to dedicate a lot of resources to this.

This has become a little bit more popularized by some of the CEOs like Jensen Huang who despite having Nvidia over 30 plus years has recently talked about how he basically doesn't do a lot of long-term planning because fundamentally you have things that you can do today and those are the things you begin to allocate.

### Growth Is Not Linear

We think that you're going to have this really neat line of how the growth is going to happen but reality is very messy. What's likely going to happen is that you will flatline and then figure something out, and then you'll flatline and then figure something out. You have stagnation and then improvements that occur and then increase revenue.

### Focus on the One Thing — That's Called Leverage

Before, I would try and delegate things A, B, C, D, E to person one, person two, person three, etc. But that appears to me like I have not done my job because I need to do a better job prioritizing. One of these things is the most important and if you can't decide what that is then you are not doing your job.

If I could only pick one — which of these would get us closer to our vision? Which ones would move us the furthest forward? Which of these is the biggest domino?

Once you solve A with all those resources because everyone's aligned, then B happens faster, C happens faster, D happens faster than trying to do them all at once.

**Example — Agency doing Meta ads for small businesses:** A good strategist says: given the resources I currently have, what one thing could I do that would make all of these problems disappear? If I had a brand and I invested in my brand — would that accomplish my leads problem? Yes. Would show up rates be higher with a warm audience? Yes. Would my close rate be okay because everyone is warmer? Probably.

That's called **leverage**. You're doing the one thing that would solve all your problems rather than trying to solve each problem individually.

## 2. Supply and Demand — The Strongest Force in Business

I believe in this so thoroughly that there are only two concepts embedded within the acquisition.com logo. The first is the supply and demand curve that exists in any market. The second is the triangle for leverage.

If you are the only person who sells this one thing and the entire world wants it, you're going to make a lot of money pretty much no matter what. Doesn't matter how good you are at marketing, sales, or pricing.

### Supply-Constrained Businesses

A supply-constrained business is one like cleaning — cleaners often don't have a hard time finding customers, the issue is finding staff who can do the cleaning. This happens a lot with nuisance services — services that people might know how to do but just don't want to do.

**Examples:** Accounting firms, legal firms, high-end consulting.

If that is the constraint, then focus on gaining leverage over it: - Build a community to recruit from - Create environments that attract talent - Build referral systems internally **The math on talent investment:** If a plumber brings in $400,000 in gross profit per year, what would you be willing to pay one time to unlock $400,000 every year? If you think about it like an investment — a stock that yields $400,000 per year — you'd be willing to pay at least $400,000 for it (100% return). Apply a risk discount, but the principle holds.

**Key insight:** Too many business owners give a $1,000 referral bonus on something that makes $400,000 a year in gross profit. That's dumb. Give $25,000 or $50,000 and solve the problem in a month.

### Demand-Constrained Businesses

**Examples:** E-commerce, B2B software, weight loss, fitness, music lessons.

Weight loss sounds like it should have huge demand, but there are even more people who supply it with many different methods. Fitness — getting customers is typically the hardest part, finding trainers is not that hard.

**The question:** which side of the curve are you on? Then ask how you can gain leverage over that problem so you can solve it at scale once and for all.

## 3. Talent Is Undervalued — The Highest Leverage Move

If your business had people who could do everything that currently exists and you could simply own it, you could work zero and make almost the same amount. You'd only have to find someone who could do what you do.

The idea of getting really good people is still undervalued in the marketplace. The most expensive companies in the world are at war for talent. They know that a 10x engineer or a 100x marketer is always worth more than what they're going to pay the individual.

**My emphasis:** Fewer, better people — and incentivizing them. Some semiconductor companies are offering 3x salaries to poach people. If they're making more money than you, look at what they're doing.

## 4. Work Ethic — The 100-Day Streak

Of the first 100 days this year I took zero days off. The entrepreneurs who are obsessed with their work do better than people who aren't. Their discretionary time basically all flows into this one thing.

**Key principles:** - You make more money when you work more because you have less time to spend it - The periods of tremendous financial growth have almost always had very long periods of just doing reps - Working seven days a week for 8-10 hours a day is far more productive for me than working five and taking two off - It's like the summer gap in learning — kids take 3 months off and then need another quarter to catch up **On vacations:** Maybe vacations aren't for me right now and that's totally okay. Who made the rule that vacations must exist? Humans existed for a very long time before vacations were a thing.

**On friends:** Better friends are busy friends. Better friends are doing things with their lives. I would rather see someone less frequently who's actually pursuing great things than someone who's always available with zero material updates.

## 5. Growth Trends — Portfolio Lessons

### Trend 1: Leadership Talent & Key Player Recruiting

**The biggest shift this year:** understanding talent is probably the single largest shift I've had in terms of understanding business. The companies doing the best are the ones where we've been able to place key players.

Key players are not the type of people who respond to traditional recruiters. They are the type of people that are giving YOU the opportunity of helping build your business — bringing their entire Rolodex, teams they can bring with them, and a huge stack of skills.

**Fundamentally as a CEO:** Attract the absolute best people, align incentives ruthlessly for them to work, and then get out of their way.

### Trend 2: Focus & Top-Down Prioritization

If you can't focus on one objective that is most important that makes the rest of your objectives less important, then you are not doing your job as CEO. The important part is **deleting everything that is not this**.

**Great razor:** When I want to fix a system or go into a department, the first thing I do is eliminate as much as humanly possible. You can always add it back.

**On departments:** What is the output of Finance? Information to make decisions. That's it. If the information doesn't change your decisions, then the function is inadequate.

**Question of the year:** *"What would it take?"* — This assumes success and lets you work backwards from the reality you want to create rather than forwards trying to figure it out.

### Trend 3: CRO (Conversion Rate Optimization)

**The companies that made the biggest gains had discipline around conversion rate optimization:** running 3 to 10 split tests at any given point on key parts of the revenue pipeline.

**Case study:** Our largest portfolio company got a +20% CRO test improvement, then a 50% lift, then a 28% lift. Probably took ~100 split tests, but it was basically one guy and a couple devs running sprints.

**Hack:** Many businesses integrate third-party tools. What none of those tools typically advertise is their conversion rate. Look at 3-4 other tools within the same use case and see which has a higher conversion rate on the UX.

## 6. The Detractors — What Hurts Businesses

### Personal Complacency

Entrepreneurs took their foot off the gas after making more money than they'd been accustomed to. You have to find something that's going to keep driving you — for me it's the love of the game.

### Spouses & Partners

One of the biggest detractors. A retroactive analysis showed: the stars who crushed had absolute spouse support. The ones who fizzled typically got into relationships where the partner had a disproportionate effect.

**The worst type of spouse:** One who actively decreases the likelihood of your goals — constantly reminding you of negatives, trying to get you to stop working, and incentivizing you to do something else instead. A double whammy.

**Advice for hiring:** Consider talking to the spouse of a high-level hire. Gauge their support level. Neutral becomes negative over time. You need someone active.

## 7. The SCALE Framework — How to Scale Anything

A micro-cycle that must exist within every scaling process: 1. **S — Start:** Whatever you're doing, you have to start 2. **C — Compound:** Do more of it 3. **A — Augment:** Do it better 4. **L — Leverage:** Make it consistent, automate it, reduce volatility 5. **E — Expand:** Go to the next territory, channel, product, and repeat **Content example:** Start posting → Do 3 posts/day instead of 1 → Improve quality of posts → Use AI, pre-prep, get consistent results → Expand to TikTok, Snapchat, etc.

## 8. The Optimization Framework — How to Fix What's Broken

When you come in to fix something that already exists (framework from Elon Musk): 1. **Question the requirements:** Why are we doing everything? How does this relate to output?

2. **Delete:** Cancel everything unnecessary — all meetings, all non-essential processes 3. **Optimize:** What gives us the most results for the least resources? Prioritize accordingly 4. **Pull up timelines:** How can we do this in 1/10th the time? Ask "how many hours does that take?" not *"what day will you finish?"* 5. **Automate:** Make the remaining processes consistent and repeatable **Example — Taking over the media team:** - Deleted all meetings - Replaced team meetings and 1:1s with a daily huddle - Deep workers just want to get into flow — meetings prevent flow **On pulling up timelines:** Instead of end-of-week deadlines, make it end-of-day. You 7x the speed. Use up all available renewable resources consistently to get the highest return.

**Argument for urgency:** The future won't be exactly like today. There will be things you didn't expect. When that day comes, you want to have accomplished everything possible behind you and have maximum resources available. The most adaptable player survives.

## 9. The Six Horsemen of Business Stagnation

**Six reasons businesses get stuck:** each involves choosing between pain today or pain forever:

### 1. Being Underpriced

**Presentation:** Close to full capacity but not making money.

**Solution:** Raise prices. Use the "Every 5, +20%" method — sell 5 customers, if they all say yes, bump price by 20%. Repeat until you net less money overall.

**Key insight:** If you sell 3 people at twice the price, it's better than selling 5 at half the price. You make more money AND have fewer people to service — creating a disproportionate increase in profit.

### 2. Overcompensation

**Presentation:** Revenue share given early on that you can't out-earn.

**Solution:** Shore up infrastructure first (hire someone else who can do the same thing at the new comp), then have the conversation. Offer a delay period for the transition.

### 3. Serving Too Many Avatars

**Presentation:** All different customers with different demands — can't get messaging or offers right.

**Solution:** 80/20 analysis — who are the top 20% of customers? What do they have in common? Make that your message and offer. Price it for the value you provide to them specifically.

### 4. Overextension

**Presentation:** Too many locations/projects without the people to run them.

**Solution:** Either hire impressive people (giving up income to bring them on) or cut your losses, reconcentrate, then scale back out. Run a 6-month test: the business must maintain or grow for 6 months without your direct involvement.

### 5. More Than One Business

**Presentation:** Multiple businesses because you don't know how to pick.

**Solution:** Zuckerberg didn't have a side hustle. The opportunity is in the problems you need to solve, not missed opportunities. Pick one.

### 6. No Data (No Data Daddy)

**Presentation:** Don't know your problems because you don't collect data.

**Solution:** Start collecting data. Test for value: when data changes (goes up or down), does it change your behavior? If not, you don't need to track it.

## 10. Personal Growth — Rush Is Imaginary

**My biggest personal growth lesson:** Rush is imaginary.

My biggest mistakes have been shiny object syndrome and overextension. I have arbitrary goals that I set for myself that I never say out loud, that mean nothing to no one, and then I'm upset when people fall short of the speed I wanted.

### Missed Opportunities vs. Problems

**Leila's frame:** The opportunities you're looking for are in the problems that you need to solve.

- **Problems:** Things that threaten the livelihood of the business (finite, listable) - **Missed opportunities:** Things that would be nice to do (infinite, unlimited) The better you get at business, the more things you see you should be doing. This creates stress. But problems are the real money on the table.

**The half-built bridges analogy:** You start building a bridge to reach a dollar, find problems, start a second bridge, find problems again, start a third... You never actually get all the way across. You have to stitch through the problems and get the dollar across.

**Practical solution:** Keep an "Alex's Big Money Ideas" list. Write ideas down, let them marinate. Don't tell anybody. Like Abraham Lincoln's unsent letters — get it out of your head, deal with it later. Many ideas you'll look at a month later wondering why you cared.

### Rush Is Linked to Competition Reference Points

**Rush is entirely linked to who I choose to compare myself to:** and that's entirely made up. Ask: how does this comparison change what I do? If it decreases the likelihood of achieving my goals, it hurts me.

**Options:** 1. Stop comparing (difficult) 2. Change who you compare yourself to 3. Compare yourself to your heroes at the same stage of their life ---

## 11. Branding — The B2B Playbook

### The New Balance Story

Their CMO flipped spending from 70% bottom-of-funnel / 30% top-of-funnel to 30% bottom / 70% top. For 18 months they made less money. On month 19 it started going up dramatically.

A massive rebirth.

### The 3:1 Ratio

Television, radio, and social media all follow a ~3:1 content-to-ads ratio. This is what's required to keep people coming back. If you shift to 2:1, people leave.

**Application:** Make sure someone gets at least 3-4 positive impressions before any ask.

**Products reinforce brand:** If your product is exceptional, the ask is not an ask because they're excited to buy. Brand and product are interwoven long-term.

### B2B Branding Strategies

Three categories for building a B2B brand: 1. **Help them accomplish their goals for free** — Content, advice, direct help 2. **Help someone like them** — Show proof with similar avatars ("someone just like me got exactly what I want") 3. **Do the aspirational outcome yourself** — Extreme versions that regress to "if it can do that, it'll probably work for me" **Aspirational examples:** - North Face → Everest → "it'll keep me warm from my car to the door" - Range Rover → Ultimate off-roader → "it'll help me get over a curb" - Red Bull → Jump out of space → *"it'll get me motivated on 5 hours of sleep"* **Brand validation:** Third-party edification is powerful. The amount of kudos for selling gym launch was far more than for owning it — even though owning was worth more.

## 12. The Flywheel — Know Your Loop

**Jim Collins' flywheel concept:** Can you draw a circle within your business where each step naturally leads to the next?

**Acquisition.com's flywheel:** 1. Make business media → 2. Attract interested business owners → 3. Do deals with them → 4. Get outcomes to make media about → 5. Repeat **Amazon's flywheel:** Cheapest products with highest review fidelity → more customers → more suppliers → more competition/selection → even more customers.

**Key question:** What are the friction points that prevent the wheel from spinning? Smooth them out.

## 13. Sawdust — Businesses from Waste

My favorite businesses are started off waste from something else. The sawdust analogy comes from lumber mills — someone looked at the saw dust on the ground and said *"could we mix this with glue and make plywood?"* **Personal example — Acquisition.com workshops:** - We had extra building space (paid for monthly anyway) - Portfolio team wasn't fully allocated - Solution: Invite businesses to fly out for 2-day workshops - Checks multiple boxes: deal flow, cash flow, content, talent flow - All using existing cost basis **Application:** Write down everything you do on a regular basis. Is there a way to recombine these into something valuable at no extra cost? Turn SOPs and checklists into lead magnets — they're already created, and they generate demand.

## 14. IRL vs. AI — Doubling Down on Both

I believe IRL and AI exist on polar opposite sides of a continuum. Doubling down on both is a good decision.

**IRL push:** Customers with in-person experiences have tremendously different brand affinity — more loyal, refer more, rate higher, buy more often. If you have strong culture in your marketing, you attract like-minded people who provide value to each other.

**The party host effect:** If everyone at the party was great, I get a disproportionate amount of reward from the associations. The network provides value to each other — I just organize it.

**On "unscalable":** I care much more about whether something is valuable. If it's valuable enough, it's worth scaling. Just because it's harder doesn't mean it's not worth doing.

## 15. School Lessons

### CRO: Split Tests Usually Fail

The more optimized something is, the more likely a change from optimal results in a decrease. Our first School Games page launch did so well that we've done 16 major split tests and only 2 have won.

### Customers Above Everything

**Paul Graham:** "You can solve almost every problem in a business by talking to your customers more."

### The Value of Deletion & Simplicity

The number one reason people cancel is overwhelm. Overwhelm translates into zero value because they stop. Product discipline around keeping simplicity and speed as the primary goal is incredibly difficult but essential.

**Steve Jobs approach:** Look at all the things that suck about a phone, remove all of them. What was left was the iPhone.

**Value equation focus:** Maximize value per second, not seconds of value. If you give customers every single thing they ask for, they will cause their own demise and then leave.

## 16. Operations — Culture, Criticism & Performance

### Culture = Rules of Reinforcement

Culture is the rules that govern reinforcement in a business. Keep it to three values max — nobody remembers more than three. Those three have hundreds of behaviors underneath them.

**Kind Not Nice:** Being kind means giving clear feedback and opportunity to improve. Being nice means seeking contentment and approval. We need to be truth-seeking.

### Insults vs. Criticism

- **Insult:** "You are lazy" — assigns a negative label - **Criticism:** "The expectation is 9:00 AM. You arrived at 9:05. There's a discrepancy." — observes reality **Formula:** Criticism + Steps (tell them what to do instead, not just what to stop doing).

### Reed Hastings Frame

"We are a professional sports team trying to win the World Championships. We are not a family." You're not going to fire your kids, but if you're on a professional sports team, everyone is there to win.

## 17. Operations — What, Who, When Framework

When you have a meeting about a task: 1. **What** — What is the task?

2. **Who** — Who's going to own it?

3. **When** — How many HOURS will this take? (Not days) **Why hours, not days:** If someone says "20 hours" you know. If they say "Friday" — why 3 days? Asking hours reveals the real timeline.

**Stack and recap:** Repeat each item throughout the meeting. By the fourth item, the first has been repeated seven times. The most recent is freshest. If needed, ask them to repeat it back — one out of three times they'll get it completely wrong.

**More deadlines = more opportunities to say "nice job."** Best performers want to work. They hate meetings, gossip, and office politics. Create an environment for those people.

## 18. Technical vs. Management Tracks

Don't force the only path for advancement to be management. Create tracks for both: - Technical experts who just want to cook - Managers who want to lead teams **Perverse incentives of management-only tracks:** 1. People keep secrets (if someone gets better, they move ahead of you) 2. Everyone asks to hire more people (more headcount = higher rank) 3. Promotion gatekeeper becomes "Chief person whose ass we must kiss" 4. People show how much they're working rather than doing work **Result of switching to pay-for-performance:** Team more than doubled output.

### Discretionary Effort

Everything above "not getting fired" is discretionary effort. Most companies have massive unlockable effort above the minimum. People can triple or quadruple output — only if incentivized.

### Pay for Performance — Implementation

1. For existing employees: keep current salaries, add performance bonuses on top 2. For new hires: build performance into the structure from day one 3. Back-test your compensation model against the previous month 4. Even simple flat bonuses by tier can work **Fire when things are good, not when things are bad.** Good times = most bloat. Better to let low performers go when it feels disciplined rather than when the company is struggling.

## 19. Problems and Solutions Have Delays

You are living with problems you created 6 months ago. When you implement a solution, the problem won't go away immediately.

**Key practice:** Before implementing a solution, decide how long to wait before appraising whether it worked. Stick to that timeline.

**Gym analogy:** If you're 50 lbs overweight (problem created years ago), you start working out (solution) but are still overweight AND now have the cost of the solution. After 7 days you might think "this isn't working." Give time proportional to the size of the problem.

**When you see a decline:** Don't look at last week. Ask: when did we stop growing? What changed at THAT point? Often it's a who — and usually you ADDED someone terrible, not lost someone key.

## 20. Content Strategy

### Back to Business

We did a 16-week experiment with wide content (meals, workouts, vlogs) expecting more absolute business owners even if the relative percentage dropped. That hypothesis was wrong. When we shifted back to business content, we got more business owners.

Crazy.

**Lesson:** Be clear about why you're making content. If you're a business owner making content to make more money, stay on point and serve your avatar.

### Education vs. Entertainment

- **Education:** Effort goes into pre-production - **Entertainment:** Effort goes into post-production Top education videos are often just a dude with a whiteboard. Make language as accessible as possible. The politician who spoke at the lowest grade level won every election.

### The 3 P's — Proof, Promise, Plan

**Every video introduction should have:** 1. **Proof** — Why should you believe me?

2. **Promise** — What are you going to get from this?

3. **Plan** — How are we going to do it?

### Platform Insights

- **Email:** MVP/Most Improved of the year - **Facebook:** Highest leverage per follower for book sales - **IG & YouTube:** Perennial champs for percentage of traffic - **Books/Amazon:** Responsible for ~15% of traffic (indirect but massive) - **TikTok:** Initially deprioritized due to skill gap, now up 300% after incentive changes ---
