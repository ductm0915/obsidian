## 6. Environment and Feedback Loops

and then we've got the **environment** which affects us too. humans are really influenced by our **environment** — one of the largest influences of our happiness and things is the **weather**.

if it's sunny we're statistically more likely to be happy — just if it's sunny. and if you don't believe me on this one, then look at the most depressed countries in the world — it'll be those ones over like Greenland, Iceland, where they don't have any daylight for basically most of the year. and then if you look at some of the happiest places, you'll notice that it's places with really good weather.

and there's even a trader called **Paul Tudor Jones** who has developed a stock trading algorithm that basically looks at weather patterns and human reactions to weather patterns, and trades stocks based on that. because humans are more likely to feel good and make optimistic bets when it's sunny — we think we're logical but we're affected by the Sun. that changes sales decisions.

so we've got **inputs**, **outputs**, **processes**, we've got our **environment**, and then we have **feedback**. and what **feedback** is — **outputs** actually affect **inputs**. all of this stuff is interconnected.

**Example —** a crowd forming: if there's like some sort of dance party or music event, and let's say no one is dancing — it's just an empty field. then one person goes in and starts dancing. that one person is going to be by himself for a while. but then as another person comes in, people slowly start coming in faster and faster because the crowd is now forming. and then as soon as a lot of people start running in, a lot more people start running in — and it grows **exponentially**.

this is a classic example of how **outputs** come back and provide **feedback** to **inputs**.

and this happens the same way on the way back down — if a lot of people start leaving a dance circle, then a lot more people start leaving. and it decreases just as fast as it increases.

this is what we call a **feedback loop** — when the **output** of something directly affects the **input** of something.

**Example —** the **stock market**: the biggest influence on the price of a stock is the price of a stock itself. so if a stock price is going up quite fast, that's going to influence more people to think *"oh this is good"* — and the people who put their money in are going to be like *"oh this wasn't the right decision"*, so they might put more in and tell more people. and then you get your bubble — you get your **Bitcoin** sort of event because of the **feedback**.

and so that's basically how **systems** work.

- Is **feedback** present? If so, what is it?
- Is it **positive** or **negative**?
- What is the **amplification** of this feedback?
- In the **environment** — what things or forces are influencing this, and in what way?

Understand this, and it makes everything so damn easy. I mean, it doesn't make things *really* easy — but compared to doing it without this framework, yeah. It's like putting on glasses after being blind and then learning to see. If you do this, it'll make you learn to see.
