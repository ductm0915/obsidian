### Separating What Really Matters

So, we're separating what's important from what's not so important. But much more importantly, we're separating what's really important from what's also important. Because the truth is, there are a lot of important things we'd like to do. It's easy to set a goal, to write a goal down, to put a goal in your system. But when you do this, there's always a cost to anything you want to pursue. And so when you set a goal, ask yourself, are you willing to make the necessary sacrifices to achieve this?

Now, I want to touch on a few best practices because as we get into goals and prioritization and applying all this, now I should I mentioned in our alignment zone, there are different levels of goals for different horizons. ### Breaking Down SMARTER

**S is Specific.** Clearly defined and unambiguous goals. I think it's obvious why that's valuable. The more clear it is, the more you can commit to and move towards that destination.

**M is Measurable.** You want to be able to quantify progress to the extent possible. Some are easier than others to quantify, but at the very least you can give a rating at the end of the day or a rating at the end of the week. It can be subjective, but it gives it a tangible anchor that you can track progress. Even if it has an error, the error will be consistent throughout the subjective rating that you give it. And so it'll be directionally accurate. So measurable is helpful. And knowing when you're done is part of being measurable.

**A is Attainable.** You want goals that are realistic and achievable. If you don't believe you can do it, you're not going to put effort into doing it. You have to believe it is doable and that if you put the effort in, you will get the result of achieving the goal.

**R is Reach.** Now, typically relevant comes down here. I've moved it to the end. So attainable and reach can be together. You want your goals to be attainable, but you also want them to be a reach. A bit of a stretch so that it's just a bit beyond your comfort zone so that you're putting effort in to learn and grow and go a little bit further than is immediately comfortable. I tend to say about a 15% stretch, whatever that means to you, but just to give you a sense. So attainable and a reach.

**T is Time Bound.** Gets goals done because goals will take up the time that they have and if you give them infinite time, well, a lot of them are going to just continue and continue and never get done. Give a deadline. It can change, but it's nice if the deadline has some consequences, some stakes attached to it. Then you're even more likely to get things done. But you want to put some kind of clear objective so that you can map out the progress to it with the expectation that it'll be done on this date or close to it. And if you need to move it, you move it, but there's a clarity and when you intend to finish it.

**E is Exciting.** You want engaging and motivating goals, things that you're fired up about, things that connect deeply with you and that get your heart speeding up and you can have that flow state and pursue them with enthusiasm. So exciting.

**R is Relevant.** And then finally, relevant. This is the alignment zone. So relevant means it aligns with your high level values and what makes life meaningful to you. It aligns with your life aspirations. That's what we're doing here today.

Just wanted to set that framework and perhaps expand it for those of you who have done the traditional smart goals.

## Never Punish Yourself for Failing

But most of all, never punish yourself for failing. If you don't reach reach a goal, if you're don't get down on yourself and beat yourself up, there is no value in that. What you do is you resolve to do it better on the next iteration. You recommit. That will be infinitely more effective.

If you beat yourself up and put yourself down and criticize yourself, you have this negative self-voice that just demoralizes you. It you lose faith in yourself. It's a negative downward spiral that just builds and builds at tearing you down.

### The Power of Iteration

On the contrary, if you recognize you didn't live up to either your commitment to something or something you were trying to achieve, okay, what worked? What didn't work? What can I do better on the next iteration?

That simple question will change everything. It's all about what I can do this afternoon or tomorrow or this week that I didn't do previously. If you make that change, then the shortcoming of the past iteration will actually be incredibly valuable. It will have taught you something and changed you in a way that you're now going to apply that learning and succeed better on this iteration.

And if you do that with every iteration, you're going to become an unstoppable force. I mean, if every iteration you assess what didn't work, what worked, tweak things or recommit and start doing it based on what you learned from the previous one.
