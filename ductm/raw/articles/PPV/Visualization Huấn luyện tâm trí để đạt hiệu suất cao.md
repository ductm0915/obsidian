## Tại sao nên Visualize

I approach visualization the way that athletes do, to train my mind to (1) create a designed memory of a challenging set of actions and (2) to build a belief in a desired path or performance, a belief and confidence that I am capable of doing it.

So this is not some law of attraction, vibrating energies kind of thing. This is conditioning my mind for peak performance, and building a belief system and training for maximum effectiveness. So it's more psychology than anything else. If done well, creating a default path in the mind.

On this channel, we explore creating systems in our lives, to perform better and achieve what matters most to each of us. And visualization is a standard part of my morning startup routine.

## Mental Representations

In the book peak secrets from the science of expertise. Anders Ericsson details the concept of deliberate practice to acquire new skills. At a high level, he describes the foundation of learning to perform better as our brain forming mental representations. I spoke in depth with Anders about this, I'll link to that conversation below.

These mental representations are what separates an expert from a novice, the expert has built a full range of deeply embedded mental representations for any scenario. And when a scenario comes up, the expert just loads the relevant, pre constructed mental representation in the mind and executes based on it. The expert seemingly instinctively knows how to handle the situation. Because the pre existing mental representations he can draw from these mental representations are built up by practice by encountering similar situations and trying various approaches, then evaluating the outcomes and learning which approach serves the situation best.

These real world experiences, shape and create the mental representations, which are then stored in the mind and available as needed. Over time they fade, but regular practice keeps them fresh and ready to go.

The mental representations are partially constructed by the physical movement and activity when the skill is physical, and partly through psychology, how we perceive and process the thoughts and experiences. In our mind.

## Cách Visualization hoạt động

When an athlete performs visualization before a challenging event, their mind is going through the psychology of the performance, the physical part is absent, but to the extent the athlete can reconstruct the thoughts and feelings of the performance, they can create a mental representation of the experience for sure, this will be a weaker mental representation than actually physically doing it. But in the absence of the opportunity, or in order to practice mentally when traveling or not in a place where physical practice is possible. This is a form of mental practice, and it does form a degree of mental representations. And these visualized mental representations can be drawn upon to perform and address real world physical activities. Whether it's an Olympic high dive, or a presentation at work, or any other type of challenging endeavor.

Of course, a visualization will be missing essential data that a real world experience would have. So it could be misinformed and leave you with surprises in the real activity. That's why it's important to work to construct as visceral and accurately informed visualization as possible.

Visualization does not tend to work for an activity you know nothing about and have never experienced, you need to know it well enough to shape a tactile, visceral mental version. But the more you know an activity, the more you can train in it through visualization. In addition to the physical practice, when done effectively, you are essentially creating a default path or expectation in your mind. This creates clarity in what you expect, as you take on The Real World Challenge. It can relax you and it can increase performance in the activity.

## Cách Visualize cho các sự kiện lớn

Here's how I apply it in my life regularly. I'll give you two instances. First for big challenging event, then for mundane, everyday routine.

Number one, let's say a big challenge is coming up, say a speaking event in front of a large audience. I will collect all the details I can about the event, ideally visiting the space ahead of time, if possible, perhaps watching other speakers at the event, then the day before or the day of I will close my eyes and mentally go through each stage of arriving at the venue going into the designated meeting area, waiting to be introduced, walking on stage, greeting the audience and flowing into my presentation. I'll go through the conclusion and walking on Stage. in completing the session, I will make each step as visceral and tactile as possible.

Think about room temperature, smells, sounds, energy levels, anything else you would expect, visualize delays or computer problems or things going wrong, what kind of joke Can I pull out then I would draw on previous similar events that I've experienced, and what the nervousness felt like and how I suppressed it, and how I can do better this time, make it as real feeling as possible. And go through each little step along the way, feel it, live it in your mind, eyes closed, reconstruct the sights and sounds, and environment as fully as possible.

Make things up. If you need to, to bring it to life, you can sporadically improvise as you're walking through it, because that's what the real event feels like when things pop up. It doesn't have to be a one to one representation of what's going to happen. That, of course, is impossible, but make it feel real, so that you can experience it and form this mental representation of overcoming any challenge and succeeding and making it happen. This will form a reasonably strong mental representation that's fresh from recent construction. If time permits, do it a few times.

## Cách Visualize để thành công mỗi ngày

Or if there's no big challenge coming up soon, I will often visualize a successful day as I plan my day to come each morning. This is particularly helpful if I've lately been getting distracted and pulled off track and not really executing on my day as I've intended.

So I'll walk through my morning routine, each step of the way, planning my day, starting the first task, what's that first task going to be? I mean, I'm being very specific. So I'm thinking this is a specific day, I've already planned out my priorities for the day, I think about getting started on that first task are getting a drink of water first, then getting started opening the computer, I'll visualize myself actually opening the application that's necessary, creating the side by side views or whatever I'm going to do. Actually setting up I'm closing my eyes and walking through this step by step on vision, a short break when I intend to take a break. If I'm calling someone, dialing the number, calling, arranging the Zoom windows, whatever I'm doing every step that I can anticipate, I'm envisioning, walking through it, creating this mental representation of this path that I want to take, executing my day flawlessly, doing the things that I've scheduled the things I've told myself, I'm going to do, I've visualized myself walking through them.

So the default path, this mental representation is the day that I've planned the day that I've designed for myself. Throughout the day, I'll check messages on vision of myself taking a break to check messages, respond briefly, then back to my priority tasks. Go back to my second priority. Once I finish the first priority, I will walk myself through the day in my mind, for maybe four minutes, three to four minutes, I can get a pretty visceral sense of especially the transitions between one end of a task, one end of an action item, and then starting and beginning the next it's usually the transitions where I know I go wrong, where my distractions pop up, you may be similar or if not the areas where you tend to succumb to the distractions most easily visualize those a little bit slower in more detail.

So you can establish the mental representation of executing, transitioning going on to the next thing that you planned flawlessly, and then ultimately finish your day and close your notebook. You can really do this in a few minutes. Obviously, you're not going through every moment of the day, but you're hitting the highlights. You're envisioning starting the priorities, finishing them, transitioning to the next one, taking breaks, taking lunch, coming back to your next priority, and implementing the day that you've designed for yourself. In your mind, it creates a default path.

## Visualization để xây dựng niềm tin tuyệt đối

And then the other way I use visualization is to create belief and reduce self doubt. To achieve anything ambitious, you have to believe it's possible, you have to believe that you can do it. Doubt is a killer, you will not stick through the challenge long enough. If you don't believe you have a good chance of succeeding. Visualization helps you see yourself succeeding, you become a witness to your future success, and it builds belief and confidence.

This will provide you with reserves when the going gets tough. It will be the beacon of light that visualization of success that enables you to persevere. In the previous two videos we explored creating your self identity visualization is a valuable tool for doing this.
