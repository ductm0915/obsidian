# Ledi Sayadaw

**Ledi Sayadaw** (1846–1923) — Myanmar Buddhist monk, prolific Pali scholar, and the pivotal figure who democratised Vipassana meditation by opening it to laypeople.

---

## Background

- Born 1846 in Burma; died 1923
- Fully ordained Theravāda monk and renowned scholar of Abhidhamma and Pali literature
- Wrote numerous treatises that became foundational texts of modern Burmese Buddhism

## The Key Innovation: Democratising Vipassana

Before Ledi Sayadaw, intensive Vipassana practice was effectively confined to monasteries and advanced monastics. Ledi Sayadaw's decisive contribution was teaching Vipassana **to laypeople**:

- Recognised that Burma was on the verge of losing political independence to British colonialism
- Understood that if Vipassana remained only in monasteries, and the monastic network was disrupted, the technique could be lost entirely
- Responded by making Vipassana accessible to farmers, merchants, and ordinary householders
- This single shift made **modern mass Vipassana** possible — every subsequent lay teacher in this lineage traces back to this decision

## Place in the Lineage

```
Ledi Sayadaw → [[saya-thetgyi]] → [[sayagyi-u-ba-khin]] → [[sn-goenka]]
```

Ledi Sayadaw trained [[saya-thetgyi]], a lay farmer who became a renowned meditator and teacher. Through Saya Thetgyi, the teaching reached [[sayagyi-u-ba-khin]], and then [[sn-goenka]], who brought it to the world.

## Significance

- The opening of Vipassana to laypersons was not a relaxation of standards but a strategic preservation move
- Ledi Sayadaw's students were expected to attain genuine meditative results — [[saya-thetgyi]] himself reportedly reached the stage of anāgāmī (non-returner)
- His model — rigorous practice, no sectarian restriction, lay accessibility — defines the character of the Goenka tradition to this day
