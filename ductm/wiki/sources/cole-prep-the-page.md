# 1 Simple Exercise To Defeat Writer's Block Forever

**Author:** [[nicholas-cole]]  
**Format:** YouTube video transcript  
**Topic:** Prepping the Page — a structural technique to eliminate writer's block by organizing a document into sections before writing

---

## Summary

Nicholas Cole presents **[[prep-the-page]]**, his core writing framework: instead of staring at a blank page, you first segment the document into labeled sections (title, intro, main points, conclusion) before writing a single word. This "skeleton" approach reduces cognitive load by separating the structural decision-making from the actual writing.

Key insight: writer's block comes from holding too many things in mind simultaneously — headline, intro, main points, conclusion. Prepping the page isolates each decision so you can focus on one at a time.

---

## Core Framework: Prep the Page

### Step 1 — Working Title (Frame First)
- Don't dive into writing without a headline. The headline is a **frame** — it tells the reader and yourself which direction you're going.
- The headline often contains the outline: "3 Reasons Why…" → the piece has 3 sections. "5 Mistakes…" → 5 sections.
- Spend most time here and on main points — these are the highest-leverage decisions.

### Step 2 — Introduction Checklist (4 elements)
1. What is it about?
2. Who is this for?
3. What's the promise to the reader?
4. What's the credibility / why should they listen?

### Step 3 — Main Points (The Bulk of Value)
- Each main point is what the headline promised: reasons, tips, mistakes, ideas.
- This is where the reader evaluates whether your writing is valuable. Weak main points = low-value piece.
- Lists are the easiest format to start with; remove numbers when comfortable.

### Step 4 — Conclusion (Optional)
- Only include if you have something **fundamentally new** to say — not a recap.
- If not: use **[[the-cliff]]** — end on the last main point and push the reader off.

---

## The Cliff Ending

> "End on the last main point, push the reader off the cliff. This forces a reaction: 'this was so valuable, you didn't waste my time, I'm ready for the next thing.'"

The cliff ending drives content binges: readers click to the next piece because each one leaves them slightly hanging — satisfied but wanting more. Cole attributes his "rabbit hole" readership to this technique.

---

## Key Principles

**Readability = Skimmability**  
Readers decide within 5 seconds whether to stay. They compare: headline → intro → main point headers. If the structure isn't scannable, they leave. Prepping the page makes the piece skimmable by design.

**The Flywheel**  
Structural clarity → faster production → more output → more practice → better writing → more readers → more output. The blank-page "purist" approach breaks this flywheel.

**Filling the Skeleton vs. Producing Magic**  
Cole spent 4+ years writing his first book (a gaming memoir) by streaming consciousness onto the page, then endlessly reorganizing. Prepping the page eliminates the reorganization phase entirely.
