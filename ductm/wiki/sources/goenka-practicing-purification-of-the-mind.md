# Practicing Purification of the Mind

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Public discourse at Hyderabad, India, 1993 (second evening of the three-day talk series)
- **Audience:** General Indian public
- **Series:** Discourses on Paññā (Wisdom)
- **Key Theme:** A detailed, step-by-step account of how Vipassana purifies the mind — from breath observation to mind-matter interaction to full liberation

---

## Core Argument

Dharma cannot be understood through listening to discourses, reading scriptures, or intellectualization. The only way is direct experience. The talk walks through the complete Vipassana method: starting with bare observation of natural breath, developing concentration with purity, then moving to sensations, to the dissolution of solidity, to full understanding of mind-matter interaction — and liberation.

> "The only way to understand Dharma, to understand the law of nature, is to experience it."

---

## Sīla as True Self-Interest

Morality (sīla) is not about obliging others — it is the deepest self-interest:

> "Those who walk on the path of Dharma should keep understanding that every time you break any *sīla*, any moral precept... you have started harming yourself. Actually, you harm yourself *first* and then you harm others."

Why? Because breaking sīla requires first generating a defilement (anger, greed, lust, ego) — and that defilement punishes the generator *immediately*, before any external harm occurs.

---

## The Danger of Concentration Without Purity

A concentrated mind is a powerful mind — and power can be misused if the base is impure:

Examples of "wrong" concentration:
- A crane standing on one leg, concentrated on finding a fish to devour
- A cat at a mouse hole, waiting to kill
- A pickpocket concentrating to pick pockets
- A hunter concentrating to shoot prey

> "Concentration is not the aim. Concentration with purity is the aim."

**Right concentration** (*sammā samādhi*) = free from craving, free from aversion, always based on truth. No imagination, no speculation, no auto-suggestions — just the truth as it is.

---

## The Breath Observation Method (Step by Step)

1. Sit down, close eyes, close mouth — no physical/vocal action
2. Observe the natural breath: coming in and going out — *just observe*
3. No breathing exercise (no *prāṇāyāma*) — do not control the breath
4. No verbalization — no mantra, name, or word alongside the breath
5. If shallow: observe it as shallow; if deep: observe it as deep; left or right nostril — just observe
6. *Taṭastha* — like someone sitting on the bank of a river, just watching the flow

> "It is an effortless observation of the truth as it is, from moment to moment."

**The challenge:** The mind wanders constantly. The instruction: smilingly notice the wandering, return to the breath. Do not generate anger at oneself for the wandering.

---

## Two Fields Where the Mind Wanders

Observation reveals: the mind wanders only in two fields:
1. **Past** — memories, rolling in "this happened, that happened"
2. **Future** — "I want this," "I don't want this"

The mind never wants to live in the present. This is one root of agitation. Training in breath observation = training to live in the present moment.

---

## Craving and Aversion as the Root

> "*Rāga, dveśha, rāga, dveśha.* The mind is constantly rolling in *rāga* or in *dveśha*."

Every pleasant thought → craving. Every unpleasant thought → aversion. Breath observation creates moments of neither-craving-nor-aversion — training the mind toward equanimity.

---

## Sensations and the Dissolution of Solidity

After 2–3 days of practice:
- Sensations begin appearing at the nostrils: heat, throbbing, pulsing, tingling, numbness
- After several more days: sensations spread throughout the body
- At advanced stage: the body's apparent solidity *dissolves* — experienced as a mass of vibrations (*kalāpas* — tiniest particles, constantly arising and passing)

> "A modern scientist also says the same thing: 'The entire material world is nothing but vibrations.' He says this because he has used his apparatus. But the scientists of our country understood by experience."

---

## Four Parts of the Mind (from Bhagavad Gītā Connection)

The verse from the Gītā (*Utkrāmantam sthitam vā pi bhuñjānam vā guṇānvitam...*) describes the four parts of mind:

| Part | Function |
|------|----------|
| *Utkrāmantam* | Perceives that something has happened at a sense door |
| *Sthitam* | Recognizes and evaluates what happened (pleasant/unpleasant) |
| *Bhuñjānam* | Feels the resulting sensation (pleasant or unpleasant vibrations) |
| *Guṇānvitam* | Reacts — craving for pleasant, aversion to unpleasant — multiplying bondage |

Vipassana breaks the chain at step 4: observe the sensation without reacting. As the reaction pattern weakens, layers of defilements are peeled off.

---

## The Biochemical Vicious Circle

> "Someone has abused me, and I have generated anger... a biochemical flow (*āśrava*) starts in the body. This biochemical flow is the result of anger — very unpleasant. Then, because this flow is very unpleasant, I get very unpleasant sensations. And when I get very unpleasant sensations, I again react with anger."

The cycle: anger → biochemical flow → unpleasant sensation → react with more anger → more biochemical flow → etc. For hours.

Vipassana breaks this: observe the biochemical flow/sensation without reacting → the multiplication stops → the reaction gradually dissolves.

---

## Six Sense Doors

The universe is experienced only through six sense doors:
1. Eyes (sight)
2. Ears (sound)
3. Nose (smell)
4. Tongue (taste)
5. Body skin (touch)
6. Mind (thoughts — sixth sense door)

When any stimulus contacts a sense door: neutral vibration → recognition → evaluation (pleasant/unpleasant) → sensation changes accordingly → reaction arises. Vipassana observes this entire chain.

---

## Conversion Without Religion Change

> "There is a very wrong impression that by practicing Vipassana, one will become a Buddhist... Vipassana has nothing to do with that."

Like Galileo's discovery that the earth is round — accepting it doesn't convert you to a new religion. Vipassana is a discovery about how mind and matter work:

> "Conversion is involved, but the conversion is from misery to happiness, from bondage to liberation, from ignorance to enlightenment — not from one organized religion to another."

---

## Notable Quotes

> "Dharma is a pure science of mind and matter, the interaction of mind and matter."

> "Don't use a breathing exercise. Don't control the breath... Just observe. Mere observation, bare observation, silent observation."

> "Sabbo pajjalito loko, Sabbo loko pakampito, pakampito." — The entire universe is nothing but vibration, combustion and vibration.

> "The entire field of mind and matter becomes clear, so clear."
