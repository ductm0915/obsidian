# Discourse to Prisoners in Nasik Jail, India — February 16, 1996

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Talk to prisoners and prison officials at Nasik Jail (Nasik Road), India, February 16, 1996. Translated from original Hindi.
- **Audience:** Prisoners, prison officials, chief guest
- **Format:** Main discourse + lengthy Q&A session (20+ questions from prisoners)
- **Key theme:** The "double jail" — outer walls and inner mental prison of defilements; Vipassana as the path to freedom from both

---

## Summary

Goenka opens by acknowledging the genuine suffering of imprisonment — separation from family, uncertain futures, repentance, anger at those who testified against them. He validates this suffering without dismissing it. But then pivots: there is a second jail worse than these walls — the mental prison of one's own anger, aversion, and ill will.

The key insight: prisoners (and everyone outside) are already prisoners of their own habit patterns. When they generate anger about perceived injustice, they are not punishing the enemy — they are punishing themselves. Understanding this through direct observation, not intellectual acceptance, is the beginning of liberation.

The practical method is observation of breath and body sensations. When anger arises, a burning sensation appears in the body. When the anger passes, the burning stops. This direct experience of the law of nature — defile the mind, suffer immediately; purify the mind, find peace immediately — changes behavior from within, not through willpower or external authority.

Goenka uses the historical example of Angulimala (who killed 999 people but became fully liberated through practice) and a Burmese prisoner who had committed 17 murders but became a Dhamma teacher. These are not fables — they demonstrate that transformation is possible regardless of past actions.

---

## Key Claims

1. **The double jail:** Physical imprisonment compounds the mental prison of defilements that everyone, inside and outside jail walls, suffers.
2. **Self-knowledge is liberation.** "The man who knows himself, he is free from all the sufferings of this world. Know thyself." (Attributed to Indian saints)
3. **Law of nature vs. government law.** Government law may be delayed or escaped; the law of nature punishes immediately and without exception.
4. **Understanding alone is insufficient.** An alcoholic understands alcohol is bad but cannot stop. Intellectual understanding of Dhamma is similarly powerless without experiential practice.
5. **Vipassana is non-sectarian.** Breath observation is universal — there is no Hindu breath, Muslim breath, or Jain breath.
6. **Fate is self-made.** "Who makes our fate? We ourselves by our actions and past deeds." Goenka rejects fatalism while acknowledging karma.
7. **Transformation is possible for everyone.** The murderer Angulimala and the 17-murder Burmese prisoner demonstrate that no past is too dark for Vipassana to transform.

## Notable Quotes

> "There may or may not be freedom from these four walls someday, but this other jail of the mind where one keeps generating defilements... there is a way out of this jail."

> "You yourself have to learn to observe this and experience this... Nobody else can do it for you. Someone can, at the most, show you the way: 'Look this is the path on which I walked and became free, but you have to walk yourself.'"

> "Where can I find pacification? — Within oneself." (Q&A)

> "About 20 years ago, I had gone to Switzerland... The bus stopped in front of a large building... It was the local jail. The guide said there had been no residents for the last 10 years. Someday this will happen in India also. When Vipassana arises in this country, all these jails will become empty."

> "Every man is his own god, his own master. But you have to win this mastery over yourself."

## Q&A Highlights

The extended Q&A covers 20+ questions from prisoners, including:
- Whether the present suffering is God's will or self-caused (self-caused through past actions)
- Whether chanting Ram's name helps (only surface benefit, ~5%; 95% of the mind remains untouched)
- Whether one innocent person imprisoned unjustly is a criminal (accept past karma with equanimity; calm mind can better prove innocence)
- How harsh words can be spoken from compassion rather than anger
- Whether fate can be changed (yes, through purifying one's mind and actions)
- Whether jails will ever become empty (yes — points to Swiss example)
