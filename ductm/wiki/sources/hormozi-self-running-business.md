# Hormozi — If I Wanted to Create a Business That Runs Itself

## Metadata
- **Author:** [[alex-hormozi]]
- **Type:** Video transcript
- **Topic:** Building a self-running, owner-independent business

## Core Argument

A business that *requires* you isn't a business — it's a job you cannot quit. The goal is transitioning from an Iron Man suit that *enhances* you (but requires you to operate) into one that runs autonomously. Once a business goes from "valuable to you" to "valuable to anyone," it has sellable equity and generates compounding wealth via valuation multiples.

## The Valuation Math: Frustrated Fred vs. Wealthy William

See [[owner-independence-valuation]].

Both owners: $10M top-line, $2M profit, $8M cost.

- **Fred (required):** $2M → 50% tax → $1M → living expenses → **$500K/year net worth accumulation**
- **William (optional):** Business trades at 6× profit = **$12M asset today.** Extra $500K in profit × 6× = **$3M added net worth** in one year.

The wealth difference is ~20×. Income is taxed once; business equity is multiplied. This is the math of why building a self-running business beats being a high-earning operator.

## 5-Step Framework

### Step 1: Self-Inventory

Do a **time study** — track every 15-minute block for a week. No fancy tech needed.

Then list everything you do and assign each item one of three resolution types:
- **Person** — someone on the team can take this now
- **Process** — a one-time project to install a system
- **Project** — a onetime build that creates a repeatable process

Color-code: **Green** (delegate now), **Yellow** (build process/project first), **Red** (need a hire you don't have). Solve Green → Yellow → Red.

See [[self-inventory-method]].

### Step 2: Trade Doing for Managing

The leverage math: 200 hrs/month doing → 20 hrs managing = 10× leverage. Repeat once = 4 hrs → 1 hr = additional 4× leverage.

Key belief shift:
- *"If I can do it right with part of my time, someone else can do it better with all of their time."*
- *"You can outwork anyone in your business. You will not be able to outwork everyone."*

Measure employees by **rate of improvement**, not starting skill level. See [[learning-intelligence-confidence]].

Three-phase handoff: **Shadow Train → Supervise → Support Independence.** See [[shadow-train-method]].

### Step 3: Build Decision Infrastructure

Every role needs:
- **If-then decision trees** for common scenarios (change card, change billing, refund, etc.)
- A **spending authorization threshold** (e.g., under $500 they decide independently)
- A **KPI scorecard:** "How does your role make the company money?" — most employees can't answer this, which is a problem

### Step 4: Remove Yourself from Marketing

A founder-face acquisition model is [[key-man-risk]] in disguise. Seven systems to generate marketing without the founder:
1. Screenshot community testimonials weekly → run as ads
2. Incentivized testimonials (trials of higher service tier = double whammy: ascension + marketing)
3. **Lifecycle ads** — record sales call + onboarding + touchpoints → compress into a timeline ad showing the journey. See [[lifecycle-ads]].
4. Document key client milestones (reveal, ribbon cutting, scale moment, revenue milestone)
5. Pay customer support $5/mini-testimonial captured in chat
6. Convert Google/Yelp/Amazon reviews into marketing assets
7. One-star review inversion (Liquid Death model — cast out the wrong customer, attract the right one)

### Step 5: Stress Test — The LMA Test

Take 90 days off. Something will break. Fix it. Repeat until nothing breaks.

The litmus test: **Does it burn down without me?** Baseline = no. Goal = when you return, it's in a *better* position than when you left. That's what investors pay a premium for. See [[lma-test]].

## A-Player Identification

Measure candidate quality by **quality + quantity of metrics** they track for their own role. Great hires ask diagnostic questions unprompted — e.g., a strong HR hire asked: "What's your cost of acquiring talent? Time to fill? Two-way 90-day fit rate?"

Pattern recognition is a competitive moat: interview many people (Sam Ovens interviewed 600 developers for the [[skool-platform]] CTO role) to calibrate your "picker."

At leadership level and above: if you're not *learning* from the candidate in the interview, pass. They should be teaching you about their function.
