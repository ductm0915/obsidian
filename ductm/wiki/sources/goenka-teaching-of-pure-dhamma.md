# The Teaching of Pure Dhamma

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Discourse on how the Buddha's Dhamma spread without opposition
- **Series:** Discourses on Dhamma, the Law of Nature
- **Key Theme:** Why calling it "Dhamma" (not "Buddhist Dhamma") was essential; how practical Dhamma gives immediate results

---

## Core Argument

The Buddha's 60 Dhamma ambassadors spread the teaching without difficulty because:
1. They taught universal Dhamma (not a sect), so there was no sectarian conflict
2. They taught people to *practise*, not just to hear sermons
3. The technique gave *immediate, verifiable results*

> "Had he called his teaching 'Buddhism' people might not have accepted it as it would have been just one of the many sectarian views. Dhamma actually belongs to all."

---

## Dhamma as Law of Nature

> "The laws of nature, which govern the universe are called Dhamma. These laws apply to all without any exception. They work without fear or favour."

Concrete examples:
- The Dhamma of fire is to burn and cause others to burn
- The Dhamma of the sun is to provide light and warmth
- The Dhamma of the moon is to provide light and coolness

These are equally available to everyone — no discrimination by religion, caste, or nationality.

---

## Why Practical Teaching Succeeded

The Dhamma ambassadors highlighted the *practical aspect* — not just sermonizing but showing how to live Dhamma so people got immediate benefit:

> "Whereas other sectarian teachers did not know how to teach Dhamma so that people could live it, those ambassadors did so."

They taught:
1. **Sīla** — morality already accepted by all traditions (mind precedes all actions)
2. **Samādhi** — self-reliant concentration on natural breath (universal, no deity required)
3. **Paññā** — experiential wisdom (*bhāvanāmayī paññā*) arising through direct experience

Self-reliance principle: *"Atta hi attano natho, ko hi natho paro siya"* — one is one's own master, who else can be one's master? *"Atta hi attano gati"* — one makes one's own destiny.

---

## The Spread via Merchant Networks

Among the first 60 ambassadors, 55 were from the merchant class. Their trade journeys to distant lands became Dhamma journeys. Through them, the teaching reached Takshshila (capital of Gandhar) and spread throughout northern India.

The Dhamma *wheel* (*Dhammacakka*) spread its light far and wide.

---

## Three Kinds of Wisdom (Paññā)

| Type | Pali Name | Source | Value |
|------|-----------|--------|-------|
| Heard wisdom | *Srutmayī paññā* | Received from others | Limited — borrowed |
| Intellectual wisdom | *Cintanamayī paññā* | Reasoning over what was heard | Limited — indirect |
| Experiential wisdom | *Bhāvanāmayī paññā* | Direct personal experience | Right wisdom |

> "The right wisdom is that knowledge which arises through one's own direct experience. It is not indirect knowledge, but it is one's own direct knowledge."

## Notable Quotes

> "Someone may explain how to go on the path, but one has to walk on it on his own. One cannot depend upon other's support and help for none can proxy for others."

> "People were attracted towards it as they were benefited by it."

> "Let us also be inspired by this instance and make efforts for our welfare. In this lie happiness and welfare of all."
