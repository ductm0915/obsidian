# Morality, Concentration and Wisdom

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Discourse on the spread of early Dhamma teaching
- **Series:** Discourses on Dhamma, the Law of Nature
- **Key Theme:** Why the Buddha's teaching spread without opposition — because it was universal, practical, and gave immediate results

---

## Core Argument

The Buddha's teaching spread universally because he called it "Dhamma" (not "Buddhist Dhamma"), and because his 60 first Dhamma ambassadors did not merely preach — they taught people how to *practise*. The three pillars of Dhamma — sīla, samādhi, paññā — are universally acceptable because they transcend sectarianism.

> "Had he called his teachings 'Buddhist Dhamma' instead of just 'Dhamma,' it would have turned into a sect."

---

## The Three Pillars of Dhamma

### 1. Morality (Sīla)

Avoid any vocal or physical action that harms others and disturbs their peace and harmony. This was accepted by all traditions, because:

- *"Mano-pubbangama dhamma, mano-settha, manomaya"* — Mind precedes all phenomena; mind matters most; everything is mind-made.
- An action with an impure mind is a misdeed; an action with a pure mind is virtuous.
- Quote from the Dhammapada: *"Manasa ce padutthena, bhasati va karoti va; Tato nam dukkhamanveti, cakkam'va vahato padam."* — If with an impure mind one speaks or acts, suffering follows as the cartwheel follows the foot.

### 2. Concentration of Mind (Samādhi)

The universal technique taught by the Dhamma ambassadors:
- Observe the natural breath coming in and going out
- No mantra, no deity, no imaginary belief — just bare observation of breath
- This is self-reliance: *"Atta hi attano natho"* — one is one's own master

Different from sectarian concentration methods (repeating names of deities), which are not universally acceptable.

### 3. Wisdom (Paññā)

Three kinds of wisdom:
1. **Suta-mayā paññā** (heard wisdom) — received from others
2. **Cintanamayī paññā** (intellectual wisdom) — gained by reflecting on what was heard
3. **Bhāvanāmayī paññā** (experiential wisdom) — gained through direct personal experience

Only the third is "right wisdom." The first two are borrowed and do not produce lasting benefit.

> "The right wisdom is that knowledge which arises through one's own direct experience."

## The Story of King Pukkusāti

Merchants reached Takshshila and told King Pukkusāti about the Buddha's teachings. The king, moved by deep past merits (*paramī*), abandoned his kingdom and walked alone to Rajgir to meet the Buddha. Arriving at night, he stayed in a potter's inn — by chance, the Buddha was also there. The king received teachings and instantly attained the stage of *Anagami* (Non-Returner). Going to the town the next morning to get robes for ordination, he was gored to death by a bull. An example of how the teaching spread far — and how even accidental death does not stop progress on the path.

## Key Claims

1. **Universal = unopposed.** Because the Dhamma was universal, the 60 ambassadors faced little opposition. Had it been sectarian, it would have clashed with existing sects.

2. **Practical results overcome resistance.** Even those attached to old traditions were eventually won over when they saw others benefiting from the technique.

3. **Merchants as Dhamma carriers.** Among the first 60 ambassadors, 55 were merchants. Their long-distance trade routes became channels for spreading the Dhamma.

4. **Emperor Asoka's role.** 400 years after the Buddha, Asoka spread the teaching not just in India but to many neighbouring countries. Then it was completely lost in India for 2,000 years before returning.

## Notable Quotes

> "Like a true scientist, he [the Buddha] experimented to know the truth of mind and matter by dividing and analysing them as also by experiencing how the two work."

> "So long as defilements are not completely destroyed, one is not liberated from them. However, one is free from defilements to the extent one has destroyed them."

> "I hope the essence of the Buddha's teaching, Vipassana, lasts for the next two thousand years for the benefit of innumerable beings."
