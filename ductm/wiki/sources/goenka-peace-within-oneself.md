# Peace within Oneself, for Peace in the World — Sarnath, 1998

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Keynote address at the International Bauddha Mahotsav (Buddha Conference) "World Unity in Buddha's Tri-Ratna," Sarnath, India, November 6, 1998
- **Audience:** Bhikkhu Sangha, Buddhist scholars, and Dhamma practitioners from India and internationally
- **Key theme:** Buddha never used the word "Buddhism"; Dhamma is universal; Vipassana as quintessence of the Buddha's teaching; Triple Gems as qualities, not sectarian affiliation

---

## Summary

This is one of Goenka's most comprehensive formal addresses — delivered at a major international conference in the sacred city of Sarnath. It covers: the linguistic history of "Bauddha" vs. "Dhamma," the return of Vipassana to India via Myanmar, the technical explanation of Vipassana at the level of vedanā and anusaya, Vipassana's role in prison reform, the correct understanding of the Triple Gems, and the mechanism of social integration through Dhamma practice.

Key historical claim: Research at Igatpuri found that the word "Bauddha" (Buddhist) does not appear anywhere in ancient spiritual literature — Buddha's teaching, Mahavira's tradition, or Vedic tradition — for approximately 500 years after the Buddha. The Buddha called his teaching Dhamma ("appamāṇo Dhammo" — Dhamma is infinite).

Technical contribution: Goenka introduces the concept of **anusaya** (guhasaya) — the deep latent impurities at the root level of the mind that continuously generate and multiply defilements. Cutting the anusaya is the unique work of Vipassana; all other methods address only the surface.

On the Triple Gems: Taking refuge in Buddha, Dhamma, and Sangha is not conversion — it is taking refuge in *qualities*: the quality of enlightenment (bodhi), the quality of universal Dhamma (sīla-samādhi-paññā), and the quality of purity found in any saintly person regardless of tradition.

---

## Key Claims

1. **"Bauddha/Buddhism" not found in Pali literature for 500 years after the Buddha.** The Buddha never established a sect. He taught Dhamma — universal law.
2. **Dhamma returned to India through Myanmar.** Sayagyi U Ba Khin preserved it; Goenka brought it back in 1969, as prophesied 2500 years earlier.
3. **Anusaya — root impurities.** The impurities at the depth of the mind (anusaya) continuously generate suffering. Surface methods don't reach them. Only Vipassana does.
4. **Vedanā paccayā taṇhā at the root level.** One generates taṇhā (rāga and dvesha) in response to vedanā. This is the deepest mechanism. Vipassana observes it objectively.
5. **"Vedanā samosaraṇā sabbe dhammā"** — All mental phenomena, including the most fleeting thought, are accompanied by sensation in the body.
6. **Mettā Bhavana requires a purified mind to be effective.** Mettā generated from an impure mind is superficial. Vipassana purifies the mind so mettā can flow genuinely.
7. **Social integration at the actual level.** ~2,000 Christian priests/nuns, 500 Jain munis/sadhvis, hundreds of Hindu and Buddhist monastics have taken Vipassana courses.
8. **Triple Gems = qualities, not persons.** "Buddha Ratna" = the quality of enlightenment. "Dhamma Ratna" = sīla-samādhi-paññā. "Sangha Ratna" = the quality of purity in any saintly person.

## Notable Quotes

> "Dhamma is infinite — 'appamāṇo Dhammo'. It is not for one particular community or religion. It is for all."

> "The Buddha made another invaluable discovery. He found that anything arising in the mind, starts flowing with the feeling of sensation in the body: Vedanā Samosaraṇā Sabbe Dhammā."

> "In Buddha's teaching, man matters most; when we talk of man, mind matters most; when we talk of mind, purity matters most so that it can generate pure love and compassion."

> "If this root habit pattern is not changed; and if the impure mind is not changed; and if the individual is not changed; the society, the world is not changed."

> "'Atta hi attano nātho' — one is one's own master. One has to work out one's own salvation. The Enlightened Ones only show the true Path."

> "History has proved that whenever the teachings of sīla, samādhi, paññā have gone from India to neighbouring countries, the teachings have never clashed with that nation's traditional culture. On the other hand, like sugar dissolving in milk, the teachings have been gently assimilated to sweeten and enhance that society."
