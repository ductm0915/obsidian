# Discourse at Presentation of First Volumes of Tipiṭaka in Devanagari Script — S.N. Goenka

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Closing address at the seminar "Vipassana: Its Relevance to the Present World" and at the presentation of the first volumes of the Tipiṭaka in Devanagari script
- **Series:** Discourses on Tipiṭaka — Pāli
- **Significance:** Explains Goenka's personal journey, the discovery of vedanā as the missing link, and the Tipiṭaka publication project as a joint India-Myanmar Dhamma offering

---

## Core Argument

Dhamma is a universal law of nature — not Hindu, Buddhist, or Jain — and it works through the mechanism of vedanā (bodily sensations) that arise at the six sense doors. The publication of the Tipiṭaka in Devanagari script is the culmination of bringing back to India both the practice (patipatti) and the scriptural knowledge (pariyatti) that India lost 2,500 years ago. Individual research within oneself — not mere intellectual study — is the real benefit.

---

## Key Claims and Arguments

### Dhamma as Universal Law

Sectarian interpretations ("Hindu dharma," "Buddhist dharma") are limited. Pure Dhamma governs the entire universe regardless of religious label:

> "_Sabbo pajjalito loko, sabbo loko pakampito, pakampito_"  
> — The entire world is burning, the entire world is vibrating.

The Buddha discovered that the entire physical and mental structure is nothing but vibration.

### The Missing Link: Vedanā

The classical chain of suffering:
> "Vedanā-paccaya taṇhā" — With the base of sensation, craving and aversion arise.

People believe they react to external objects. The Buddha's discovery: we actually react to *sensations in the body* triggered by the external contact. The external event is only the apparent cause; the sensation is the actual trigger point. Without awareness of vedanā, all efforts go toward rectifying external conditions and miss the internal root.

### The Six Sense Doors Model

Contact at any of the six sense doors (eye/form, ear/sound, nose/smell, tongue/taste, body/touch, mind/thought) immediately produces a sensation. If pleasant → craving; if unpleasant → aversion. This is mechanical and universal. Vipassana trains meditators to observe the sensation without reacting.

### Goenka's Personal Story

Born in a conservative Hindu family, drawn to the Gita and Upanishads, Goenka could understand ideals (vita-rāga, vita-dvesha — freedom from craving and aversion) intellectually but had no practical method:

> "There seemed to be no way. I could only contemplate: 'Oh, raga is very bad! I must become vita-raga.'"

After encountering Sayagyi U Ba Khin and taking his first Vipassana course:
> "After practising for four days, I realized that Vipassana is applied Gita."

### The Tipiṭaka Publication Project

Historical context:
- **Chattha Sangayana (Sixth Council)**, Myanmar, 1954-56: scholars from all neighboring countries recited the entire Tipiṭaka; minimal discrepancies found across thousands of pages
- 15,000 pages of Tipiṭaka + 50,000 pages of commentaries preserved in Myanmar
- Vipassana Research Institute undertook transcription into Devanagari script
- Joint India-Myanmar project; Myanmar scholars verify accuracy
- Ambassador from Myanmar, U Winn Lwin, co-presented the first volumes

Sayagyi U Ba Khin's motivation: "The teaching came from India. Myanmar has a debt to India."

### Individual Research as the Real Purpose

> "Every individual has to do one's own research on the law of nature within, the Dhamma within. Without this research, we cannot benefit from Vipassana."

Publishing the Tipiṭaka serves intellectual understanding; the actual benefit comes from personal practice. Siddhattha Gotama's research liberated one person — himself. The path he found liberates anyone who walks it personally.

## Notable Quotes

> "This N.R.I. brought an invaluable jewel!"

> "People who come to Vipassana courses are converted, but not from one organized religion to another organized religion. They are converted from misery to happiness! From bondage to liberation. From ignorance to wisdom."

> "Five centuries after the time of the Buddha, India had lost the scientific method of Vipassana. But fortunately, the words of the Enlightened One, and the practice, were maintained in the neighbouring country."

## Key Historical Facts

- **Chattha Sangayana**: 1954-56, Rangoon, Myanmar — the sixth great council of Theravada Buddhism
- **Loss of Vipassana from India**: approximately 500 CE (five centuries after the Buddha)
- **Myanmar preservation**: technique preserved in an unbroken teacher-student lineage from Buddha → Sayagyi U Ba Khin → S.N. Goenka
- **First return to India**: Goenka began conducting courses in India approximately 25 years before this discourse
- **Tipiṭaka in Devanagari**: first volumes presented at this event; subsequently published in Roman script
