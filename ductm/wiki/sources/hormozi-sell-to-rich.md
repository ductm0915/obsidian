# Why You Aren't Making As Much Money As You Want — Alex Hormozi

## Metadata
- **Author:** Alex Hormozi
- **Source type:** Video transcript (YouTube)
- **Raw file:** `raw/articles/Alex Hormozi/Why you aren't making as much money as you want.md`
- **Date ingested:** 2026-04-13

## Summary
Hormozi's central argument: most entrepreneurs are poor because they sell to poor people. The video walks through US wealth distribution data, applies the 80/20 principle recursively to customer profit, and gives a concrete pricing ladder framework for tiered offers. Ends with tactical advice on pricing psychology and lead qualification.

## Key Claims & Arguments

### 1. Wealth is radically concentrated — go where the money is
If US household wealth ($163T) were $100 distributed to 100 people:
- Bottom 50%: $2.50
- Next 40%: ~$28
- Next 9%: ~$38
- Top 1%: **$32** (more than the bottom 90% combined)

Implication: competing for the bottom 50% means fighting over $2. Go where the money is.

### 2. 80/20 applies recursively to customer profit ([[power-law-wealth]])
- 20% of customers → 80% of profit
- 4% of customers → 64% of aggregate profit
- 1% of customers → 51% of aggregate profit

### 3. The tiered pricing model ([[tiered-pricing-model]])
Rule: for each new tier, **5–10× the price and expect 20% uptake**.
- Adding a second tier at 5–10× can double or more total revenue
- Top tiers contribute disproportionate profit (overhead is already covered by base tiers)
- Example: $10/mo (800 customers) + $50/mo (200 customers) → doubles revenue

**Hormozi's own ladder at Acquisition.com:**
- Skool hobby: $99/mo
- Skool pro: $299/mo
- L1 advisory: $5,000
- L2 advisory: $35,000
- L3 advisory: $135,000

### 4. Top-down brand positioning ([[top-down-brand-positioning]])
Start with premium, work down (Tesla: Roadster → Model S → Model 3). Reverse doesn't work: budget brand trying to sell luxury = no credibility. Anchoring high also makes lower tiers look like deals.

### 5. Absolute vs. relative profit
1 person buying a $10,000 thing that costs $2,000 = **same profit** as 400 people buying a $50 thing at $25. Don't underestimate large prices in small quantities.

### 6. Selling from your own wallet ([[selling-from-wallet]])
Projecting your own price sensitivity onto prospects is the #1 psychological barrier to high-ticket pricing. Hormozi's story: said $6K hoping the guy would say no → guy said yes → escalated through 8 calls ending at $10K/call → $60K in a morning.

### 7. Rich vs. poor buyer mindset ([[rich-vs-poor-buyer-mindset]])
- Poor buyers: think in terms of **cost**
- Rich buyers: think in terms of **ratio** (ROI, value-to-price)
- Rich people shop high-to-low; they want fast, easy, guaranteed — not cheap
- Top 10% of Americans have $1M+ net worth. 1 in 10 people. They're not rare.

### 8. "It's super expensive" pre-frame
Before quoting price, say "Hey, it's super expensive." Rich prospects immediately anchor to their own reference of expensive (usually high); poor prospects brace themselves — either way you get a better outcome than cold-quoting.

### 9. Price as quality signal & virtuous cycle (reinforces [[close-rate-pricing-ladder]])
High price → high gross margins → hire better talent → better service → better reputation → more demand → higher price. Pricing is two-way communication.

### 10. Lead scoring / lead qualification
Only speak with people who fit your ideal avatar (those with money). Pitching expensive things to the wrong people creates false negatives. If you're only talking to million-dollar business owners, your close rate on high-ticket goes up dramatically.

## Notable Quotes

> "If you sell to rich people long enough, they will make you one of them."

> "The only thing worse than offering a $1,000 thing to a $100 buyer is offering a $100 thing to a $1,000 buyer. In the first, you lose $100. In the second, you lose $900."

> "You can tell how advanced a service business owner is by how expensive their product is."

> "They don't want to save money anymore. They want better value. They want to skip the line."
