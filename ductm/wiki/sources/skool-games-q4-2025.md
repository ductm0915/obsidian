# Skool Games Q4 2025 — Sam Ovens + Alex Hormozi + Skool CEO

Live event recording for Q4 2025 Skool Games winners. [[sam-ovens]] (Skool co-founder) and [[alex-hormozi]] in conversation, hosted at Skool HQ. Audience: top Skool community builders from across niches (golden ratio frequencies, dating, wholesaling real estate, AI education, guitar, etc.).

---

## Summary

A free-flowing Q&A and value session covering: what has value in an AI world, how to differentiate as a creator, what's working in content right now, how to filter information overload, building cult-like audiences, scaling communities, and the philosophy of the Skool platform.

---

## Key Claims

### 1. What Has Value When AI Makes Information Free?

- Information has always been "free" (the internet existed before AI)
- Value = **selection + curation + compression of time** — people pay for someone to sift through infinite information and show them what matters
- The consumable portion: identify what in your product is monthly-recurring vs. one-time. Examples:
  - 3D printing community: trending products weekly
  - Wholesale real estate: sourced deals weekly
  - Financial: stock picks
- **Community itself is consumable** — last month's community doesn't substitute for this month's
- Real people going through real struggles = the AI-proof asset; authenticity rises in value as everything becomes fake

### 2. The Talent + Personality Formula

- **[[talent-personality-formula]]**: Talent + Personality = differentiation. In an AI world where content that looks like content doesn't work, these are the two levers
- Getting better at the core skill is the 80/20 of everything else — not algorithm hacks, not content formats
- Sam's thesis: Hormozi's prominence comes from real-world business results, not understanding of social algorithms
- **[[wisby-principle]]**: "Why Should I Believe You?" — the authority question every creator must answer. Start with extreme proof (jimmied 1200, world #1), then your instruction is credible
- B2B branding: "if that guy vibe-coded a $10M app, he can probably help me automate my email" (regress-down authority)

### 3. Apparent Contradictions = Interesting Personalities

- **[[apparent-contradiction]]**: Interesting personalities emerge when someone doesn't match the expected pattern
  - "A jacked dude who likes philosophy" — not common
  - "Guy in wife-beater who makes a lot of money and talks business at high level" — pattern mismatch
- Off-the-shelf personalities (I'm 30, I like IPAs, I watch these 6 shows) = boring. Aftermarket modifications = interesting
- How brands are built: Apple consistency + Steve Jobs' "I wear this so I don't think about clothes" — not trying to create character; just optimizing for what matters most
- Skool cat: impulsive decision to create a cat → huge personality boost. Not logical, but on-brand with "fun first"

### 4. Saves > Shares — The Metric That Predicts Purchase

- **[[saves-over-shares]]**: TikTok Shop meta-analysis — saves are the #1 metric correlated with purchase intent, not shares, not likes
  - A shared meme → viewers laugh, share onward; doesn't build authority
  - A saved "11 steps to fix X" → the saver doesn't know the steps yet (non-obvious insight), trusts the creator, likely to buy
- Content that converts = content that removes friction and gives non-obvious insight. Content that goes viral ≠ content that sells
- **Key heuristic**: Look at your organic content sorted by saves. Those are your ads.

### 5. Content-Ads Convergence (The Singularity)

- **[[content-ads-convergence]]**: Paid and organic have merged — "we have reached the singularity." Hormozi's paid team and media team are now one
- Workflow: post 450 pieces per week → algorithm tells you the winners → run winners as ads with a banner overlay ("click to get the thing")
  - No added CTA narration needed anymore
  - Same content can lead to any relevant offer via overlay
- Ads that look like content = platform nirvana (platform wants no ads, you want reach = aligned objective)
- Tried reverse (best ads posted as organic) → didn't work. Best short-form ads came from short-form creators, not ad teams
- "If you're putting out 20 pieces, run all 20. You'll be amazed."

### 6. First-Party Data Principle

- **[[first-party-data-principle]]**: Your own results from doing the work > any third-party advice about what works
  - Early stage: you don't have first-party data → consume books, courses, figure out the approach
  - Later stage: you have data → your data is more accurate than anyone else's opinion
  - The signal gets stronger, the noise gets smaller
- "Don't change things when a solution is on the way" (Leila's principle) — don't pivot midway through a test
- Traffic sources feature: French guy spending all time on Instagram, YouTube was delivering more money → 2 seconds of first-party data changed his whole strategy
- Caveat: don't cut top-of-funnel (discovery) in favor of bottom-of-funnel (email) — email converts high because list is warm; you need inputs to keep it growing

### 7. Repeat Successful Actions

- **[[repeat-successful-actions]]**: The most underrated operating rule. When something works, keep doing it until it stops working — not until you fear it might stop
  - $100M book launch: Day 2, same exact presentation, same slides → $25M in sales. Same people re-bought. "We watch reruns."
  - "Did it work? Yes. Do it again. But we did it yesterday. Do it again."
- Most people stop doing what works because it feels boring or they worry it'll stop resonating — this is the failure mode

### 8. How to Filter Information Overload

- Trust that if something is significant enough to affect your business, you will change. Until then, keep doing what works
- Hormone analogy: "I will gather attention → point it in a direction → make a compelling offer → deliver on the promise"
- Sam's operating rule: 1 piece of content/week (YouTube), 1 email/week — everything else ignored
- Time blocking: "First 4 hours promote, next 4 hours build, last 4 hours deliver" — know what you do every day
- The ultimate productivity hack: **No**. Anything not the thing = making you less focused

### 9. Skool Native Phenomenon

- **[[skool-native]]**: A new class of creator whose primary platform is Skool itself, not Instagram/YouTube/TikTok
  - "Goose" — won school games multiple times, no ads, no off-platform social media
  - The platform has its own engagement mechanics (member relations, discussion culture) that reward native creators
- School has ~22M users vs. ~9 billion people — enormous runway still ahead
- 30% of all memberships come from Skool's own discovery features

### 10. Building Cult-Like Following (Authority Conditioning)

- Hormozi's 4-step formula for becoming a "reinforcer" (the mechanism behind cult-like followings):
  1. Visibly demonstrate control over the things your audience wants to control
  2. Give behavioral instructions that yield good outcomes (non-obvious)
  3. Have third-party credibility confirming what you say is true
  4. Be like your audience in some meaningful way
- When you do this at scale + over time → people attribute portion of their success to you → Martha Stewart effect
- **No "going to war" needed**: The enemy doesn't have to be a person. Skool's enemy = complexity and joylessness.
- Elon, Tesla, Steve Jobs — never tried to build cults; just built the best thing, community formed around it

### 11. Product Depth Over Feature Parity

- Competitors copy Skool features at surface level — "feature parity" without depth
- Example: the green dot (online status indicator) — what does "online" mean? Tab open but on another tab? Phone + desktop simultaneous? Active in a DM?
- Sam's philosophy: "When I look at Skool I see nothing but problems" — the highest standard is the driver
- The doingness of care: taking features to their hypothetical ideal, not stopping at "I have the feature"
- **Cowboy testing** (low-traffic friendly): change one thing, watch the metric, see if it goes up or down. AB tests for sensitive/high-traffic decisions only
- 14 of 16 Skool Games AB tests lost — the control (the existing thing) usually wins; creative on the front end has more leverage than page conversion

### 12. Scaling Content Without Burning Out

- 70/20/10 framework for creative sustainability:
  - 70%: repeat successful actions / existing format
  - 20%: adjacent experiments (one degree off core)
  - 10%: moonshot — wild stuff with no expected return
- "You'll do more total volume over 5 years because you didn't want to stop" — sustainable pace beats Mr. Beast-mode burnout
- Content extraction: start from the longest format (live stream / podcast) → clips → shorts → email. Never start from shorts.
- Family Feud / UFC Slap = formats designed for clipping. Optimize long-form so it generates clippable moments.
