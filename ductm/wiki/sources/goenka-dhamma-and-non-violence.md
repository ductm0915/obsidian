# Dhamma and Non-Violence — S.N. Goenka (Jain Celebration, Mumbai 2005)

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Public discourse at a Jain Celebration, Mumbai, 2005
- **Audience:** Jain community and general public
- **Series:** Discourses on Sīla (Morality)
- **Significance:** Bridges Jain teaching on non-violence (ahimsa) with Vipassana; presents sīla-samādhi-paññā as equivalent to Mahavira's teaching

---

## Core Argument

Dhamma is not merely belief or intellectual understanding — it must be lived and experienced. Lord Mahavira's teaching of non-violence, self-control, and diligence is equivalent to the three trainings of sīla, samādhi, and paññā. The key insight that Vipassana adds is that before harming anyone else, one always first harms oneself — and this truth can only be understood through direct experience, not scripture.

---

## Key Claims and Arguments

### Dhamma Must Be Lived, Not Just Known

Opening with a Hindi verse:
> *"Dhārana kare so Dharma hai, varanā korī bāta..."*  
> — "Dhamma not experienced, not lived in life is useless."

Like a sick person who recites the dosage on a prescription without taking the medicine, merely praising Dhamma produces no benefit. Dhamma must be practiced; only then does it give results.

### Jain and Buddhist Dhamma Are the Same at the Core

Lord Mahavira's teaching: non-violence (ahimsa), self-control (restraint), diligence (hard work). Another great teacher described the same thing as sīla, samādhi, and paññā. These are not two different teachings — they are the same universal truth expressed for different audiences.

### Redefining Non-Violence Through the Five Precepts

Non-violence is not merely refraining from killing. Any action that deprives another of peace and happiness is violence:
1. **First precept** (no killing) — taking life deprives of peace
2. **Second precept** (no stealing) — taking property deprives of peace
3. **Third precept** (no sexual misconduct) — causing suffering to family members
4. **Fourth precept** (no lying/harsh speech) — robbing others of mental peace
5. **Fifth precept** (no intoxicants) — leads to inadvertent violations of the first four

### Samādhi and Tapa

"Tapa" (austerity) is not external mortification; it is the inner experience of what happens at the depth of the mind where defilements arise, multiply, and blind us. This is also what paññā means — not book-learning but direct inner experience.

> "Not the paññā that consists in book learning and using logical acumen, but the paññā which develops from directly experiencing what happens in the depth of one's mind."

### The Great Discovery: You Harm Yourself First

> "It is the immutable law of nature that a person cannot make others unhappy without first making himself unhappy."

Before killing anyone, one must generate massive anger — and suffer from that anger first. Before stealing, one generates greed and suffers from it. This was experienced firsthand by the great men of India and is not merely a theoretical claim.

### The Small Burning Coal Child Analogy

A mother forbids a small child from playing with burning coals. One day, unsupervised, the child grabs a burning coal and cries in pain. Now the child knows, from direct experience, that fire burns. Similarly, once a meditator experiences what anger does to the body from within, they stop generating it — not from moral obligation, but from direct knowledge.

### Goenka's Personal Story

Goenka recounts going to his teacher Sayagyi U Ba Khin with hesitation, fearing conversion from Hinduism to Buddhism. After tasting Dhamma, he realized: "Far from sectarianism, Dhamma is eternal truth applicable to one and all."

## Notable Quotes

> "In ancient times to see within was called vipassana... vipassana means to experience. We have forgotten its ancient meaning."

> "Once we learn to see within, we too will know that the fires of anger and other defilements burn us."

> "Truth is indeed to be experienced and not merely believed in."

## Key Concepts

- **Cāturyama** — The four restraints of Jainism (equivalent to the first four precepts)
- **Tapa** as inner observation, not external austerity
- **Passanā** — to see/feel/experience; the root of "Vipassana" in everyday usage
- **Self-harm before other-harm** — the immutable law of nature
