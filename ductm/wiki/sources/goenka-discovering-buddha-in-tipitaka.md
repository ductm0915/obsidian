# Discovering the Buddha in the Tipiṭaka — S.N. Goenka

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker/Author:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Condensed extract from Goenkaji's foreword to the new edition of the Pāli Tipiṭaka published by the Vipassana Research Institute; published in VRI's International Vipassana Newsletter, 1994
- **Series:** Discourses on Tipiṭaka — Pāli
- **Scope:** Comprehensive overview of what the Tipiṭaka contains and why it matters

---

## Core Argument

The entire Tipiṭaka is saturated with the personality of the Buddha — not just his physical presence (rūpakāya) but his Dhamma-body (dhamma-kāya), which embodies complete enlightenment, perfect wisdom, and compassion. Reading the Tipiṭaka corrects three prevalent misconceptions about the Buddha's teaching and reveals it as universally applicable — to lay people and monastics alike, addressing worldly as well as supramundane concerns.

---

## Key Claims and Arguments

### The Two Bodies of the Buddha

**Rūpakāya** (material body): possessed the 32 signs of a great man; radiated peace and beauty.

**Dhamma-kāya** (Dhamma body): the real legacy — complete enlightenment, perfect wisdom, moral behavior, right understanding, and compassion. The Tipiṭaka is the Dhamma-kāya transmitted to the world.

### The Nine Qualities of the Tathāgata

Goenka enumerates the classical epithet qualities that explain what makes the Buddha different:
1. *Arahata* — destroyed all impurities
2. *Sammā-sambodhi* — perfectly enlightened
3. *Vijjā-caraṇa* — full of wisdom and well-established in practice
4. *Sugato* — "well-gone" due to pleasant physical/vocal/mental activities
5. *Lokavidu* — knower of the entire universe, mundane and supramundane
6. *Anuttaro* — peerless, without equal
7. *Purisa damma sārathi* — skilled charioteer who guides untrained people
8. *Satthā devamanussānaṃ* — Teacher of gods and humans
9. *Bhagavā* — having destroyed attachment, antipathy, and ignorance

### The Six Qualities of the Dhamma

The Tipiṭaka illuminates Dhamma that is:
1. *Svākkhāto* — clearly expounded
2. *Sandiṭṭhiko* — directly visible, taking one to the truth without illusion
3. *Akāliko* — giving concrete, visible results here and now
4. *Ehi passiko* — summoning one to come and realize
5. *Opanayiko* — leading to nibbāna step by step
6. *Paccattaṃ veditabbo viññuhīti* — worth personally experiencing by every intelligent person

### Correcting Three Misconceptions

**Misconception 1: The Buddha's teaching was only for recluses.**
Reality: He was hugely popular among lay people throughout his ministry. Lay devotees outnumbered monks. He spent rainy season retreats near densely populated cities specifically to reach more people.

**Misconception 2: The Buddha ignored everyday concerns.**
Reality: He gave extensive guidance on all aspects of lay life — mutual duties of parents/children, husbands/wives, masters/servants, teachers/students, friends, kings/subjects. The advice to the Licchavas on republican governance is described as "a model for any republican government of modern times." Asoka's reign was inspired directly by the Buddha's teachings.

**Misconception 3: The teaching is pessimistic/focused only on suffering.**
Reality: The Buddha did not deny suffering — he diagnosed it, found its cause, and prescribed a complete cure. This is the most hopeful possible approach:

> "Truly a patient is discouraged when told that his or her disease is incurable. But if someone makes the patient aware of the disease, discovers its primary cause and offers a way of removing the cause by pointing out a medicine which can totally eradicate the disease, this is a blessing for the patient."

### The Tipiṭaka's Historical Value

Beyond its spiritual significance, the Tipiṭaka is a complete historical document of life in northern India 2,500 years ago:
- Administrative, educational, commercial, and industrial customs
- Political structure (republicanism, monarchy)
- Social conditions, both urban and rural
- The lives of people from all classes — kings, sweepers, courtesans, merchants, beggars — transformed by contact with Dhamma

### The Sāvaka-Saṅgha (Community of Accomplished Disciples)

The Tipiṭaka also illumines the Saṅgha — accomplished disciples who include both lay people and renunciants. Their practical realizations described in the Tipiṭaka "hearten and thrill us, raising goose bumps and electrifying our practice."

The Saṅgha demonstrates that Dhamma has no place for blind faith, emotional devotion, or intellectual hair-splitting — only practical realization.

## Notable Quotes

> "All of the Tipiṭaka is saturated with the noble and sacred personality of the Buddha."

> "Such is the noble Dhamma: universally beneficial to all; free from sectarianism; acceptable to all people, from all countries, in any age."

> "What could be more relevant in this age of moral degradation, with its inevitable result of downtrodden people afflicted with terror?"

> "The stately grandeur of the Buddha's teaching is verdant forever. It is the perennial forerunner of the resurrection of fallen human values."

## Key Concepts Introduced or Clarified

- **Rūpakāya vs. Dhamma-kāya** — the two aspects of the Buddha's personality
- **Nine Tathāgata epithets** — the classical nine qualities of a fully enlightened being
- **Six qualities of the Dhamma** — svākkhāto, sandiṭṭhiko, akāliko, ehi passiko, opanayiko, paccattaṃ veditabbo
- **Kālāma Sutta** — described as "the first declaration of human rights and a beacon for all humanity, enunciating the principle of freedom of thought"
