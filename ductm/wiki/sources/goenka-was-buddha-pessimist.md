# Was the Buddha a Pessimist?

## Source Metadata

- **Author:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Extract from *Was the Buddha a Pessimist?* (VRI publication; translation of Hindi *Kyā Buddha Dukkhavādī The?*)
- **Significance:** Systematic refutation of the "pessimism" charge; establishes the linguistic and philosophical meaning of "Noble" (*ariya*) in the context of the Four Noble Truths; explains *nirodha* as complete uprooting, not suppression

---

## The Charge Being Refuted

A missionary in Mandalay during Goenka's youth argued: "The Buddha only taught four things — suffering, the cause of suffering, the eradication of suffering, and the path. All he talks about is suffering! There is not a trace of happiness in his teaching. He is a pessimist. How can suffering be 'noble'?"

Goenka as a teenager found this convincing. He became ashamed of this when he later understood the Four Noble Truths through Vipassana practice.

---

## The Answer: Suffering Points Toward Its Eradication

The four Noble Truths form a complete system:
- There is suffering → it arises from craving/aversion → it *can be eradicated* → there is a practical path to eradicate it

> "The final aim of the teaching of the Buddha is to eradicate all suffering."

The "pessimism" charge only holds if you look at the first truth in isolation, ignoring the third truth (cessation) and fourth truth (path).

---

## The Four Aspects of Eradication (Nirodha)

The Noble Truth of the Eradication of Suffering is explained in four aspects:

| Pali Term | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| *Nissaraṇaṭṭha* | To come out of all accumulated impurities |
| *Vivekaṭṭha* | To be free from the habit of developing new impurities |
| *Asaṅkhataṭṭha* | To experience the unborn state where nothing arises |
| *Amataṭṭha* | To experience the deathless state where nothing passes away |

---

## Nirodha = Complete Uprooting, Not Suppression

In contemporary Indian usage, *nirodha* means suppression. But the original meaning is complete uprooting, total eradication — that which cannot arise again.

> *"Pahīno, ucchinnamūlo, tālāvatthu-kato, anabhāvaṃkato, āyatiṃ anuppādadhammo"*
> "Destroyed, uprooted, like a palm tree whose head is cut off, extinguished, attaining the state of non-arising."

When a palm tree's top is cut off, it dies completely — no new leaves. This is nirodha.

---

## What Does "Noble" (Ariya) Mean?

The missionary also objected: "How can suffering be 'noble'?" Goenka traces the linguistic history:

- In the Buddha's time, *ariya* denoted qualities, not caste or race
- In Pali: *ariya* = one who is pure, supreme, far from the stains of passion
- Defined as: "one who does not follow the unwholesome way"; "one who is non-violent towards all beings"; "one who has attained nibbāna"
- The Noble Truths (*ariyasaccāni*) are "truths that are known to the ariya"

**Key definition:** Experiencing any one of the four Noble Truths makes one an ariya — a noble person. The truths are "noble" because they have the power to make the practitioner noble.

---

## The Four Stages of Liberation

Through continued Vipassana practice:

| Stage | Pali | Meaning |
|-------|------|---------|
| 1st | *Sotāpanna* | Stream-enterer; entered the stream of liberation; max 7 more lives |
| 2nd | *Sakadāgāmī* | Once-returner |
| 3rd | *Anāgāmī* | Non-returner |
| 4th | *Arahat* | Fully liberated being |

Within a week of the first discourse, all five brahmin ascetics became arahats. Later, thousands of monks/nuns became arahats in the Buddha's own lifetime; hundreds of thousands attained sotāpanna.

---

## Pali Language Note

The Buddha's mother tongue was Kosali (the spoken language of Kosala). This *Prakrit* (natural language) later called Pali. Sanskrit was an artificial language created ~2 centuries after the Buddha by the grammarian Panini. In Pali literature, *ariyā* never once denoted caste or race — only qualities.
