# Sam Ovens — How To Use Metrics

**Author:** [[sam-ovens]]  
**Type:** Podcast/conversation (Sam interviewing a guest, Paden)  
**Core theme:** Good metrics are simple, actionable, and customer-focused. Most companies measure the wrong things.

---

## Summary

A conversation-format discussion on metrics philosophy. Sam and his guest Paden explore what good measurement looks like through Amazon's approach, airport satisfaction measurement, and the specific challenge of measuring student/customer outcomes in an online education business.

**Key principles on metrics:**

1. **Fewer, better metrics.** "Good investors, when coming to an investment decision, if it's made on more than three critical assumptions and three figures, they will not do it." Simplicity = clarity = actionability. A labyrinth of KPIs obscures rather than reveals.

2. **Amazon example: measure what actually predicts customer happiness.** Amazon's primary customer satisfaction metric is "number of support tickets per order" — the closer to zero, the happier the customer. Counterintuitive but elegant: if the customer has to contact support, something already failed.

3. **Lagging vs. leading indicators.** Financial metrics are lagging. Engagement, tickets, community activity — these are leading. Sam watches community engagement (bell rings, posts, testimonials) as leading indicators of course health.

4. **The hardest metric: student success in an online course.** Sam acknowledges this is nearly impossible to measure accurately:
   - Self-reported outcomes are biased
   - Engagement proxies (video views, action items) correlate but don't guarantee results
   - Best proxy: when people in the community spontaneously recommend your course as the best
   - Second-best: refund rate, happiness score in support tickets, testimonials at moment of success

5. **"You should ask for a testimonial at the moment of success, not later."** Capture it when they ring the bell.

6. **Good companies listen to feedback and have already fixed the problem.** Bad companies implement feedback surveys but are indifferent to outcomes (AT&T example: 10-minute verification process before telling you they can't help).

---

## Key Claims

1. **Simplicity in metrics = better decisions.** Amazon's single satisfaction metric is more actionable than a dashboard of 50 KPIs.

2. **The right metric for customer success is often indirect.** Tickets per order, not "satisfaction score." Community recommendation rate, not "completion rate."

3. **Engagement is necessary but not sufficient.** People can watch all your videos and not succeed. Results are what matters; engagement is a proxy.

4. **"The best metric you have is: go to the Facebook groups your customers are in, sort by recent, and read what they say about you."** Qualitative signal is often more reliable than quantitative proxies.
