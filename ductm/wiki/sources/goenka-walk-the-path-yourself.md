# Walk the Path Yourself — S.N. Goenka (Zee TV Discourse #7)

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Seventh in a series of 44 Hindi discourses broadcast on Zee TV (translated and adapted)
- **Audience:** General public, Hindi-speaking audience
- **Series:** Discourses on Samādhi (Concentration) — Anapana Meditation

---

## Core Argument

No teacher, god, or savior can liberate another person. Liberation is a personal responsibility — each person must walk the path themselves. This is not egoism; it is the nature of the path. The technique of Vipassana leads toward the dissolution of ego (anattā/egolessness), not its inflation. Progress requires patience and persistence in taming the "monkey mind."

---

## Key Claims and Arguments

### No Words on the Breath

Goenka opens with a final reminder: no word should be added to the breath observation. Word-based meditation can calm the surface but leaves the volcanoes of deep defilements dormant — ready to erupt at any time.

### The Taming Metaphor

The untamed mind is compared to a wild buffalo or elephant — dangerous and destructive. But when tamed with patience and persistence, the same power becomes enormously constructive and helpful.

> "The mind is more powerful than a thousand elephants. When it is destructive, it does more harm than a thousand wild elephants. The same mind, when tamed, will be of much more service than a thousand tamed elephants."

Key requirement: patience. The wild animal (mind) does not become tame immediately. Continuous, patient work is required — ideally at a Vipassana center with a qualified guide.

### Taming the Monkey Mind

The mind is like a monkey that keeps jumping from branch to branch. It is agitated, disturbed, miserable. The meditator must not become further agitated by the wandering:

- Accept the present reality: "At this moment, my mind is full of craving/aversion"
- Don't add judgment or anger about the wandering — anger generates more agitation
- Accept and return to the breath, again and again

### Progress in Observation

As the practice deepens, a shift occurs:
1. Early stage: "I am observing" — there is an observer and an observed
2. Later stage: "It is being observed" — the observer-identity dissolves
3. Patanjali's formulation: *"Drastā drśīmātra"* — In seeing there should be only seeing
4. Final stage: bare awareness remains; this is sammā diṭṭhi

### Personal Responsibility: Not Egoism

Some critics misunderstand self-liberation practice as egoism. Goenka refutes:
- The goal is anattā — dissolution of "I," "mine," "my soul"
- Taking responsibility to clean the mind is no more egotistical than bathing daily to clean the body
- No external force generated these defilements; each person accumulated them through their own ignorance and must remove them through their own effort

### The Sāvatthi Parable: The Buddha and the Rajagaha Path

A person asks the Buddha why some meditators become fully liberated, others improve greatly, and others show no change at all — even though they all come to the same Buddha.

The Buddha's counter-question: "Do all people you explain directions to actually reach Rajagaha?"

Answer: "Of course not — only those who actually walk the path."

> "Tumhe hi kiccam ātappam, akkhātaro tathāgatā."  
> — "You have to do your own work; Enlightened Ones only show the path."

The Buddha knows the path perfectly because he walked it himself. He shares it out of compassion. But he cannot walk it for anyone else.

## Notable Quotes

> "The sooner a person gets rid of the delusion that someone else will carry him or her on the shoulders to the final goal, the more beneficial it is for such a person."

> "Someone full of compassion might show us the way... but one has to actually walk on this path oneself."

> "One has accumulated defilements oneself; one will have to remove the defilements oneself."

---

## Key Story

**The Sāvatthi Parable** — Buddha and the traveler from Rajagaha. One of Goenka's most powerful illustrations of why people get different results from the same teacher: only those who walk the path get results. Knowledge of the path and walking it are entirely different things.
