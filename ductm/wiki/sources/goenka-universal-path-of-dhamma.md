# The Universal Path of Dhamma

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Hindi discourse broadcast on Zee TV
- **Series:** Discourses on Dhamma, the Law of Nature
- **Key Theme:** Sīla, samādhi, paññā as the whole of Dhamma; detailed analysis of the Noble Eightfold Path's sīla division

---

## Core Argument

All of Dhamma is contained in sīla, samādhi, and paññā. These three are universal — any person of any tradition who practises them becomes a truly Dhammic person. The Noble Eightfold Path is the detailed map of this three-part journey. "Ariyo" (noble) was originally a *qualitative* word — not racial — referring to anyone who has purified the mind through this path.

> "The entire Dhamma is contained in *sīla*, *samādhi*, and *paññā*. The whole meaning of Dhamma is contained in them."

---

## What It Means to Be Ariya

In Pali, *ariya* meant "noble person" — someone who had totally purified their mind through sīla, samādhi, paññā. It was a qualitative term, not a racial one.

> "Any person who became a noble-hearted person by walking on the path, establishing himself in *sīla*, *samādhi*, *paññā*, and developing a totally pure mind, became a noble person."

The word *anariyo* (ignoble) = one who causes harm to themselves and others = foolish (*anārī* in modern Hindi). The Noble Eightfold Path (*Ariyo aṭṭhaṅgiko maggo*) makes everyone noble who walks it.

## The Sīla Division of the Eightfold Path

Three of the eight factors belong to sīla:

### Sammā-vācā (Right Speech)
Speech that is:
- Truthful and beneficial
- Applied in one's own life (not just recited from books)
- Not lying, not speaking bitter words, not backbiting, not carrying tales

> "Until it becomes a part of one's life, one does not gain from it."

### Sammā-kammanto (Right Action)
Physical actions that do not harm others or disturb their peace. Specifically:
- Abstaining from killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, and intoxicants

### Sammā-ājīvo (Right Livelihood)
One's occupation must not directly or indirectly encourage others to break precepts. Examples of *wrong* livelihood:
- Dealing in weapons (even if you personally don't shoot)
- Rearing animals for slaughter (even if you don't kill them yourself)
- Selling intoxicants (even if you don't consume them)
- Selling poison

The mental *volition* behind the occupation is crucial — even an inherently good profession becomes impure if performed with wrong volition.

## The Doctor Story

Before learning Vipassana, Goenka visited a doctor whose clinic was empty. The doctor said sadly: "Usually there is some epidemic in the city this season, this year for some reason there has been no outbreak." Goenka was shocked: "He is a doctor who is supposed to see that more and more people are healthy and yet he wishes that more and more people should fall sick."

This illustrates how even a noble profession can be perverted by wrong volition — not *sammā-ājīvo*.

## The Businessman's Lesson

Goenka himself came from a business family. He teaches businesspeople:
- Your income comes from your consumers — your consumer is your Lord who gives you your bread
- Do not cheat your consumers in quality, quantity, measurement, or weight
- Earning wealth is not wrong; *how* you earn it and what *volition* drives you is what matters
- Share a portion of excess wealth with society — this counters the ego that naturally grows with wealth

> "In the ancient language, it was known as the duty of sharing a portion of one's earnings with others."

## Key Claims

1. **Dhamma makes any person "good" in their own tradition.** A Hindu practising sīla/samādhi/paññā becomes a good Hindu; a Muslim, a good Muslim; a Buddhist, a good Buddhist.

2. **The Noble Eightfold Path described in great detail.** Sīla division = 3 factors (right speech, action, livelihood). The other two divisions (samādhi: right effort, mindfulness, concentration; paññā: right view, intention) are referenced but not detailed here.

3. **Right livelihood requires examining volition.** The surface-level occupation may appear proper, but the *mental intention* behind it determines whether it is actually sammā-ājīvo.
