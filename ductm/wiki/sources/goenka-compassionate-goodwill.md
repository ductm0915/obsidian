# Compassionate Goodwill

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Discourse on mettā (loving-kindness) and the brahmaviharas
- **Series:** Discourses on Mettā (Loving Kindness)
- **Key Theme:** Brahmavihāras (sublime states) arise only from a mind purified through sīla, samādhi, paññā — they cannot be cultivated through talk or intellectual acceptance alone

---

## Core Argument

The four brahmaviharas — *mettā* (selfless love), *karuṇā* (compassion), *muditā* (sympathetic joy), *upekkhā* (equanimity) — are not cultivable through discussion or intellectual appreciation alone. They arise *naturally and spontaneously* only when the mind has been completely purified through the practice of sīla, samādhi, and paññā.

> "The mind can overflow with compassion as well as *mettā*, *muditā* and *upekkhā* only when the mind is completely free from all defilements at the deepest level."

---

## What Is Brahmavihāra?

*Brahmavihāra* = the nature of a *brahma* (highest being in the order of beings). It is not:
- Merely talking about compassion
- Intellectually accepting compassion as an ideal
- Praising or discussing compassion

These are "far away from true brahmavihāra." True brahmavihāra = the mind actually *suffused and overflowing* with these qualities.

---

## The Prerequisite: Sīla, Samādhi, Paññā

**Why defilements must be eradicated first:**

1. **Ego as root of all defilements.** As long as the mind is ego-centred, *brahmavihāra* cannot arise. All defilements have ego as their root.

2. **Paññā erodes the habit pattern.** By developing experiential wisdom at the depths of the mind, the ingrained habit of generating and multiplying *saṅkhāras* of craving and aversion is progressively weakened.

3. **Progressive development.** The more the mind becomes free from defilements, the more the brahmaviharas develop. When a meditator is fully liberated, they dwell *continuously* in pure brahmavihāra.

4. **Universal, not sectarian.** No individual of any caste, colour, class, society, community, or religion has a monopoly on sīla, samādhi, paññā. Therefore, no one has a monopoly on the brahmaviharas either.

---

## Universal Dhamma as Unifier

> "All can become one in the practice of the *brahmavihāras* of love and compassion."

Different sects have different:
- Ways of worship and places of worship
- Rites, rituals, and festivals
- Philosophical beliefs
- External appearances

But the Dhamma of morality, concentration, wisdom, and love/compassion is **universal — the same for all**. This universal Dhamma and the resulting brahmaviharas *unite* all religious sects.

> "While continuing to maintain their distinct sectarian features, they can unite at the level of universal Dhamma."

## The Two-Article Comparison

Note: This article and "The Essence of Compassion" (see [[goenka-essence-of-compassion]]) are nearly identical in content — possibly two versions of the same discourse. Key differences:
- "Compassionate Goodwill" uses the term *saṅkhāras* (reactions) explicitly
- "The Essence of Compassion" uses *habit-pattern of blind reaction* phrasing

Both reach the same conclusion about the relationship between sīla/samādhi/paññā and the brahmaviharas.

---

## The Neutral Object of Meditation

A key technical point: for right samādhi, the object of meditation must be **neutral** — it should neither generate *rāga* (attachment) nor *dosa* (aversion). It must be based on direct experiential truth, free from ignorance.

This distinguishes Vipassana's approach (observing natural breath, then bodily sensations) from concentration practices that use deities, mantras, or imagined objects — which may generate attachment.

---

## Notable Quotes

> "Compassion (*karuṇā*) is a very noble state of the human mind. Like selfless love (*mettā*), sympathetic joy (*muditā*) and equanimity (*upekkhā*), compassion is also a *brahmavihāra* (sublime state of mind)."

> "The purity of mind and the resultant sublime states born out of it is the fruit of practice of Dhamma."

> "Just as the defilements of an impure mind cannot be labelled as Hindu, Muslim, Buddhist and Jain defilements, similarly love, compassion, goodwill and other wholesome qualities of a pure mind cannot be given any sectarian label."
