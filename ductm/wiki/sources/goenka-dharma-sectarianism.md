# Dharma & Sectarianism

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Public talk given at Hyderabad, India, 1993
- **Audience:** General Indian public
- **Series:** Discourses on Dhamma, the Law of Nature
- **Key Theme:** Reclaiming the original universal meaning of "Dharma" from sectarian corruption

---

## Core Argument

India lost the true meaning of "Dharma" over 1,500–2,000 years. People now equate Dharma with sect or religion (Hindu-dharma, Muslim-dharma, etc.). This corruption has led to communalism and violence. The ancient meaning was universal: Dharma = the law of nature, what the mind contains at any given moment.

> "It is a great pity that we have totally forgotten pure Dharma. How can Dharma be Hindu, Muslim, Christian, Jain, Parsi, or Sikh? This is impossible."

---

## Key Claims

1. **The ancient definition:** *"Dhāretī ti dharma"* — what one holds, what one's mind contains at this moment. Wholesome contents = *kuśala-dharma*; unwholesome = *akuśala-dharma*.

2. **Ārya and Anārya.** In ancient India, *ārya* meant a noble person with a pure mind — not a racial category. One whose mind contains impurity was called *anārya*. This has nothing to do with race or caste.

3. **Dharma as nature's characteristic.** "The dharma of fire is to burn." The dharma of ice is to cool. The dharma of a mind generating anger is to generate suffering — immediately, without exception.

4. **Punishment is immediate.** "The moment you defile your mind, the moment you generate any negativity, nature starts punishing you then and there." Similarly, purity is rewarded immediately with peace and happiness.

5. **Sectarianism divides; Dharma unites.** "Sectarianism is divisive. Dharma is universal: it unites."

6. **The real cause of misery.** The cause lies *inside* us — not in external circumstances. We remain ignorant of this because we have lost the practice of inner observation (Vipassana).

7. **Vipassana as the path.** The word "vipassanā" appears in the *Ṛg Veda*: *"Yo viśvābhih vipaśyati"* — one who practices Vipassana perfectly comes out of all aversion and anger.

---

## The Inner Mechanism: Sensation Chain

Goenka explains how misery is generated internally:

```
External event (insult, obstacle)
→ Sensation in body
→ Reaction: craving or aversion (rāga/dveśha)
→ Misery
```

> "The deepest level of your mind (the so-called 'unconscious') is never unconscious: it is always feeling sensations on the body. Whenever there is a pleasant sensation, it will react with craving — *rāga, rāga*. Whenever there is an unpleasant sensation, it will react with aversion — *dveśha, dveśha*."

This is the addiction cycle at the deepest level of mind — unbroken for lifetimes.

---

## Vipassana as India's Ancient Technique

Goenka references a historical traveller who visited India and found every household practicing Vipassana morning and evening. Over time, India replaced this practice with rites and rituals and lost both the technique and the real meaning of Dharma.

> "Dharma is nothing but a pure science, a super-science of mind and matter: the interaction of mind and matter, the cross-currents and the under-currents happening deep inside every moment."

---

## The Anger Observation Method

When anger arises:
1. At the gross level: breath becomes abnormal (faster, harder)
2. At the subtler level: biochemical/electromagnetic reaction; heat, palpitation, tension
3. The instruction: **just observe**. Do not suppress, do not express. Simply observe with equanimity.
4. Result: the anger becomes weaker and passes away on its own. Suppression drives it deeper.

---

## Practical Conclusion

No conversion required. A Hindu remains Hindu, a Muslim remains Muslim — but each *becomes a better human being* by purifying the mind. "Dharma teaches you how to become a good human being."

---

## Notable Quotes

> "If this understanding of Dharma becomes more and more prevalent in Indian society, as it was twenty-five centuries ago, then the country will be more peaceful because its people will be more peaceful."

> "There is a very wrong impression that by practicing Vipassana, one will become a Buddhist... Vipassana has nothing to do with that."

> "Dharma is the art of living. In ancient India, Dharma was nothing but an art of living, the art of how to live peacefully and harmoniously within, and how to generate nothing but peace and harmony for others."
