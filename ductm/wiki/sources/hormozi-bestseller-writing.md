# How I Wrote A #1 Bestseller with $0 and No Publisher

**Author:** [[alex-hormozi]]  
**Format:** YouTube video  
**Topic:** Full breakdown of Hormozi's book-writing process and storytelling craft

---

## Summary

Hormozi breaks down how $100M Offers sold 200,000+ copies with no publisher, no ad campaign, and before he had an audience — driven purely by word of mouth. The video is a deep dive into the craft of storytelling and book writing: how to find a big idea, structure it, write stories, edit for simplicity, and test frameworks to validity.

---

## Key Claims

### On Writing Books

- A good book = **complete solution to a narrowly defined problem**. 1.6M books published per year; most name hundreds of problems but solve none completely.
- Writing process: ~1,000 hours per book. Eight full drafts for $100M Leads (600 pages → 200 pages final).
- Goal: compress 5 years of knowledge into 3–4 hours for the reader.
- Mental model: *"Writing is walking uphill with a bag of rocks where you keep adding rocks the higher up the hill you go."* — Stephen King

### On Storytelling Craft

- **Most powerful influence tool**: Stories change brain waves, increase suggestibility, educate and entertain simultaneously.
- **Stakes and struggle** drive story interest — not just plot. See [[stakes-and-struggle]].
- **External + internal journey**: Every story has what you can see (external) and what the character feels (internal). Great stories weave both.
- **Show, don't tell**: Stephen King example — instead of "Cheryl was his ex-wife," write "he struck the spot on his finger still bleached from where his wedding ring used to be."
- **Selective detail**: Only call out details that change the story. If deer vs. buffalo on the wall doesn't change the meaning → skip it.
- **Five senses**: Smell, hear, see, touch, feel — set scenes by describing, not narrating.

### On Framework Validity

- A framework must be: **useful** AND **valid** (applicable across many circumstances and still works).
- Test by applying to new situations; if it fails, adjust the framework to incorporate both old and new, then retest.
- Most frameworks are just documented mental checklists people already use unconsciously.

### Seven Factors of Newsworthiness

See [[seven-factors-newsworthiness]]:
1. **Recency** — closer to now = more interesting
2. **Impact** — relevance to the reader; author's job to bridge "why this matters to you"
3. **Prominence** — Kim Kardashian sneezes → interesting; involve prominent characters
4. **Proximity** — Towson MD vs. Baltimore MD headline; the closer the more compelling
5. **Conflict** — tension and unresolved mystery; man with a gun walks in
6. **Unusualness** — pattern interruption; brain diverts resources to reconcile unexpected
7. **Updatability** — how many times can you give updates on the same story

## Editing Process

- Goal: 12th grade language → **3rd grade language with pictures**
- Tool: [hemingwayapp.com](https://hemingwayapp.com) — paste text, highlights complexity (adverbs, passive voice, long sentences)
- Rules: eliminate sentences if removing them doesn't change meaning; shorter > longer; shorter word > longer word; eliminate adverbs (find the right verb instead); simple tenses
- After self-edit → send to **4–8 ideal readers** (people who value him but will tell truth)
- Rule: if everyone gives same feedback → change it; if feedback is scattered → the writing is fine (Stephen King)
- Final pass: grammar editor
