# 1 Simple Exercise To Defeat Writer's Block Forever

## Source Metadata

- **Author:** Nicholas Cole (co-creator of Ship 30 for 30; co-creator of Category Pirates newsletter)
- **Format:** Video transcript (YouTube / course content)
- **Significance:** Introduces the "Prep the Page" technique — Cole's core anti-blank-page writing method, used for 10+ years and thousands of articles; practical framework for structured online writing

---

## Core Problem

Staring at a blank page is hard because you have to hold multiple things in your head simultaneously:
- Title / Headline
- Introduction
- Main points / structure
- Conclusion

Each element serves a different purpose. Holding all of them *at once* is cognitively overwhelming — that is the source of "writer's block."

---

## The Solution: Prepping the Page

> "Prepping the page means you're organizing what you're staring at by section before you even actually get into the writing."

**Analogy:** Filling in a skeleton vs. building a structure from scratch. When the skeleton (outline) is in place, the page looks nearly finished — reducing the psychological weight of the task.

---

## The Structure

Every piece of writing breaks into:

### 1. Working Headline (Write First)
> "Don't just dive into the writing — force yourself to create a frame."

A headline is a *frame* — it tells both you and the reader which direction you're going. The number/type embedded in the headline automatically becomes the outline:
- "Three reasons why X" → piece is organized into three reasons
- "Five mistakes that kept me from X" → five mistakes become the main points

### 2. Introduction (4 Elements)
1. What it's about
2. Who it's for
3. The promise (what the reader gets)
4. Credibility (why listen to you)

### 3. Main Points (The Core Value)
> "The most valuable thing about this piece is the main points. That is the bulk of the value you are giving the reader."

The reader clicks because of the headline promise; they evaluate based on how valuable the main points are. Spend disproportionate time here.

If the headline is "Three reasons why X" — the three reasons ARE the main points. Each is simply a labeled section with a few paragraphs of content.

### 4. Conclusion (Optional)
Two rules:
1. **Say something fundamentally different** from everything already covered — do not recap
2. **If you have nothing new to say, just end it** — Cole calls this "the cliff": end on the last main point, push the reader off. They are left wanting more → click the next piece

---

## The "Cliff" Technique

> "I gave you exactly what I promised you. Now we're done. If you want the next thing, you have to go and click the next one."

The cliff ending drives readers to consume more of your content — they go down the "rabbit hole" of your back catalog. This is how Cole has had readers tell him they read 100 articles in one sitting.

---

## Why This Works for Online Writing

Reader behavior on digital platforms:
1. Click headline → decision 1: "Is this for me?"
2. Skim intro → confirm they're in the right place
3. Skim main point headers → commit to reading

> "Readability is skim-ability. The more skimmable it is, the easier it is for the reader to decide whether to buy in."

The three goals:
1. Clarity for reader (what they're getting)
2. Minimal friction from click to understanding
3. Reduced time to produce high-quality work (output = practice = improvement = more reach)

---

## Cole's Origin Story

Cole spent 4+ years writing his first book (*Confessions of a Teenage Gamer* — a gaming memoir from his time as a pro gamer) by writing and rewriting start-to-finish multiple times, producing hundreds of pages without structure, then having to reorganize. This was "a monumental waste of time."

After starting to write extensively online and examining his own mental models for producing work, he developed the Prep the Page framework as a systematized alternative.
