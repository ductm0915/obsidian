# What is True Dhamma?

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Public talk at Ramabai Ambedkar Girls High School, Nasik, Maharashtra, 1998. Context: foundation stone laid for Global Vipassana Pagoda in Mumbai.
- **Audience:** Citizens of Nasik, Maharashtra
- **Series:** Discourses on Dhamma, the Law of Nature
- **Key Theme:** What Dharma truly is — its universal nature — and why it cannot be found through external practices alone; the two levels of mind

---

## Core Argument

Dharma = *dhāretīti dhammam* = that which is imbibed and lived. Its essence is the law of nature (*rit*) — immutable, applying to all without exception. Any defilement generates agitation; any purification generates peace — immediately, without delay. Sectarian crutches obscure this universal truth. The only yardstick for Dharma: are defilements decreasing?

---

## Dharma as Nature's Law (*Rit*)

> "In ancient India Dharma meant that which is imbibed, lived by — *dhāretīti dhammam.*"

Examples:
- Fire burns; ice cools — these are the "dharma" (nature) of fire and ice
- When anger arises, agitation inevitably follows — this is the dharma of anger
- When loving-kindness arises, coolness and peace follow — this is the dharma of a pure mind

**The Burning Coals Analogy:**
Put burning coals in a container → the container burns first, then heat spreads to the environment. Similarly, when anger arises, the person generating it *burns first* before affecting others.

**The Cool Ice Analogy:**
Put ice in a vessel → it first cools the vessel, then cools the environment. Loving-kindness, compassion, and joy first cool the person generating them, then spread to others.

---

## The True Yardstick

There is only one measure of whether one is living Dharma:

> "The only yardstick to check whether one is growing on the path; that is to see whether defilements are decreasing."

Rites, rituals, visits to temples/mosques/churches — these are not inherently wrong, but they are not Dharma. Dharma = actual reduction in anger, jealousy, arrogance.

---

## True Selfishness

> "Dharma teaches us to be selfish in its true sense."

A truly selfish person understands where their *best* welfare lies. Defilements harm oneself first. So avoiding defilements is the highest self-interest. This "selfishness" is completely different from the ordinary selfishness that leads to cheating and lying (which actually harms oneself).

## Two Levels of Mind

This article introduces a crucial psychological model:

| Level | Name | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| Surface level | *Parita citta* | Small part; conscious; receives intellectual understanding |
| Deep level | Subconscious | Large part; old conditioning; blind reactions of craving/aversion |

**The Problem:** Intellectual inputs, discourses, devotional practices — all touch only the *parita citta*. They give brief insight ("graveyard wisdom") that quickly fades when one re-enters ordinary life. The deep conditioning remains unchanged.

**"Graveyard Wisdom":** At a cremation ground, one has a flash of insight — "I too will die; why am I attached to 'I' and 'mine'?" But as soon as one steps outside, the world of attachments takes over again. *Surface wisdom — no lasting impact.*

## The Vinoba Bhave Meeting (1972)

Three years after returning to India from Burma, Goenka attended a Vipassana camp at Mahatma Gandhi's Sevagram Ashram in Wardha. He was encouraged to visit the saintly Vinoba Bhave in Pawnar Ashram.

Vinoba confirmed: "Yes, Vipassana is certainly an ancient teaching of India. The *Rigveda* is filled with words of praise for Vipassana."

Vinoba then recited the Rig Veda couplet:
*"Yo vishwabhi vipassati, bhuvana. Sam cha passati, sa naha parshadati dvishaha."*

— One who practices Vipassana observes with awareness whatever multiplies within (*vishwa* = that which keeps multiplying). Observing it objectively with equanimity: negativity cannot remain in such a person.

This couplet — thousands of years old — contains the full essence of Vipassana.

## Why the Buddha Said "Never Heard Before"

The Buddha said about the Dhamma he rediscovered: "Through this Dhamma which was never heard before by me my eyes of wisdom have opened." But he had studied all the scriptures of his time. Contradiction?

Goenka's explanation: Over centuries, the *practice* died out. The *words* remained (as in the Rig Veda couplet), but without the practice, the true experiential meaning of those words was lost. So even if he had read or heard the words, he had not experienced the truth they pointed to.

> "Without practice, when the true meaning of these words changed, then the way to live the life of Dhamma was lost. Only mantras remained."

## Vipassana in Prisons

True Dharma is for all — including prisoners. When prisoners practice Vipassana, they observe:

> "Whenever I generate negative and hate-filled thoughts I become so agitated! Already one avenue of agitation exists due to my imprisonment, and now I have created one more avenue of misery."

They realize the *court of nature* punishes them simultaneously with the court of the country — generating additional suffering on top of their prison sentence. Vipassana in prisons produces genuine transformation.

Reference to a "Dhamma emperor" (Asoka) who previously introduced Vipassana in prisons, producing changes in criminals.

---

## The Maharashtra Connection

Nasik and the surrounding region:
- 1,800 years ago: many sages practised tapas and deep meditation here
- After the Buddha: Vipassana blessed this country for 500 years; Maharashtra specifically for 700 years (the Buddha's general teaching for 500 years, plus an additional 200 years in this region)
- The numerous caves around Nasik where people meditated are testament to this
- Then rites and rituals took precedence, and Dhamma was again lost

> "As a citizen of a nation, whichever nation one lives in, one follows its laws. If any law is broken, the punishment that is likely to follow may take time due to legal delays... But the law of nature or Dharma has no exceptions and no delays ever."
