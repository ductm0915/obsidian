# Why Dukkha

## Source Metadata

- **Author:** S.N. Goenka
- **Format:** Essay with vivid Indian social vignettes illustrating the causes of suffering
- **Significance:** Makes the second Noble Truth (cause of suffering = craving) viscerally concrete through relatable everyday examples from Indian middle-class life; distinguishes *kāmataṇhā* and adds the amplifying role of envy

---

## The Root Cause

At the root of all suffering, there is always *attachment* — always *craving* (*taṇhā*).

> "We are forever experiencing an infinity of cravings. We see a form with our eyes which seems beautiful, and our desire is stimulated."

*Kāmataṇhā* (sense-desire) arises because sense objects make us restless. When the fire of desire combines with the petrol of envy (*irshya*), intensity multiplies many-fold.

---

## Four Vignettes Illustrating Craving + Envy

### 1. The Car Owner
A man proudly decorates his Indian-made car — then at a party sees his colleague Daboo Bhai driving a large imported Impala. All pleasure in his own car immediately vanishes. His plush seats "sting like a thousand scorpions." His mind fills with dreams of Cadillacs and Mercedes.

### 2. The Sari
Mohini wears her new expensive sari to a party, hoping to be the center of attention. Sohini arrives in a chiffon sari from Hong Kong — the latest design. Mohini becomes miserable, loses appetite, cannot sleep. Her wardrobe full of expensive saris now looks like rags.

### 3. The Government Officer
Neki Ram, an honest minor official managing on his salary, visits a colleague's lavish four-room air-conditioned flat (clearly acquired through corruption). Returns home doubting his own honesty. His wife berates him. The couple loses all peace.

### 4. The Dowry Competition
Lakhotraji spends lavishly on his daughter's wedding and feels proud. A few months later, Katorimal spends twice as much — flies in the best sweets from Delhi and Calcutta. Lakhotraji has a heart attack.

## The Core Pattern

> "For what we do not possess, there arises a strong craving. We get only dissatisfaction from what we have. Where there is clinging, there is dissatisfaction, and where there is dissatisfaction, there has to be clinging."

This is the dukkha of:
- Inferiority complex and envy: "Why am I inferior to others?"
- Mad ambition: "I must excel and be better than them"

---

## Competition as Infectious Disease

> "The disease of clinging and competition spreads like an infectious illness and constantly keeps on spreading throughout humanity."

Even one family member infected with this greed disrupts an otherwise contented family. Society-wide, this inordinate competition becomes the breeding ground for misery — not happiness.

---

## The Right Approach to Work and Wealth

Goenka is clear this is NOT an argument for poverty or inaction:

> "Definitely not! Most certainly not! People must work to eliminate their own poverty as well as that of others too. Let them really work hard, but let them maintain a balanced mind while they are engaging in work."

The goal is equanimity *during* the activity of earning and achieving — not withdrawal from activity. If wealth is achieved but equilibrium is lost, happiness has not been achieved.

---

## The Intellectual Knowledge Problem

> "Even if we realize the suffering of greed and clinging at an intellectual level, still we could not come out of this misery by means of such intellectual knowledge."

Intellectual understanding alone cannot break the habit pattern — formed from childhood (mother dressing child to be prettier than others, competition in school, etc.). This is why Vipassana practice is needed.
