# The Practical Teaching of the Buddha

## Source Metadata

- **Author:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Article on the first sixty Dhamma ambassadors; how the teaching spread through northern India
- **Significance:** Historical account of the early spread of the Dhamma; emphasizes the practical, results-oriented nature of the teaching as the key to its appeal; explores the three types of wisdom in relation to missionary activity

---

## The Sixty Dhamma Ambassadors

After establishing sixty arahats, the Buddha sent them in different directions — the first Dhamma ambassadors. They were:
- 5 Brahmins
- 55 from the merchant class (*vaishya*)

Because of their merchant connections, Dhamma spread faster among merchants — who then carried it on their long business journeys to distant cities.

---

## Why They Did Not Call It "Buddhism"

> "The Buddha never called his teaching 'Buddhism'. Had it been so, it would have become a sect. Instead he called it Dhamma."

Had they taught "Buddhism," it would have been just one more sectarian view among many. Instead, they taught the universal laws of nature — applicable to all:
- "The Dhamma of fire is to burn"
- "The Dhamma of the sun is to provide light and warmth"
- These laws apply equally to everyone

## The Three Components of Dhamma and Their Reception

### Sīla (Morality)
No opposition. Every tradition taught the importance of morality. The Dhamma ambassadors' contribution: showing that the thought of wholesome/unwholesome action arises in the mind first, then manifests in speech and body. Therefore, the mind must be purified.

> *"Mano-pubbangamā dhammā, mano-seṭṭhā manomayā"* — Mind precedes all phenomena; mind is chief; everything is mind-made.

### Samādhi (Concentration of Mind)
Traditional approach: concentrate mind on one's deity by repeating the name. The Dhamma ambassadors offered the universal alternative: observe the natural breath without combining any word, image, or sectarian belief with it.

> "Keep on observing the flow of the normal breath coming in and going out naturally. As soon as the mind wanders away, bring it back to the awareness of the breath. No word should be repeated with the breath; no imaginary belief is to be combined with it."

### Paññā (Wisdom)
As concentration strengthens, sensations begin to arise near the nostrils and then throughout the body. Direct experiential knowledge develops:

**Three types of wisdom:**
| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| *Srutamayī paññā* | Heard wisdom — accepted with reverence from others |
| *Cintānamayī paññā* | Intellectual wisdom — analyzed and found logical |
| *Bhāvanāmayī paññā* | Experiential wisdom — arises through direct experience; right wisdom |

## The Spread Through Results

Wickedness transformed to righteousness. Drunkards got out of addiction. Gamblers gave up gambling. People directly witnessed positive changes in practitioners. The technique attracted people because it gave *immediate, tangible results.*

> "One may explain how to go on the path, but one has to walk on it on one's own. One cannot depend upon other's support and help — none can proxy for others."

## The Story of King Pukkusāti of Gandhāra

A group of merchants reached Takṣaśilā (Taxila), the capital of Gandhāra, and met King Pukkusāti. Hearing of the Buddha's teaching, the king abandoned his kingdom and walked on foot to Rājagiri to meet the Buddha. He found the Buddha at an inn belonging to a potter, received the Dhamma, and immediately attained the stage of Anāgāmī (non-returner). Going to fetch monk's robes the next morning, he was gored to death by a bull — thus unable to receive full ordination. But he died having already attained a noble stage of liberation.
