# The Universal Path of Dhamma — Zee TV Broadcast

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Translation of a Hindi discourse broadcast on Zee TV (Indian television)
- **Audience:** General Indian television audience
- **Key theme:** Detailed practical explanation of sīla as the first division of the Noble Eightfold Path; right speech, right action, right livelihood for householders; business ethics; the original meaning of "Ariya"

---

## Summary

This discourse is unusual in Goenka's corpus for its sustained, detailed focus on the *sīla* (morality) division of the Noble Eightfold Path — specifically the three factors of right speech (sammā-vācā), right action (sammā-kammanto), and right livelihood (sammā-ājīvo). It is oriented toward householders, particularly those in business.

Goenka opens by stating that Dhamma consists entirely of sīla, samādhi, and paññā. These three must become lived — not just intellectually known. A person walking the path "establishing himself in sīla, samādhi, paññā so that the mind becomes totally pure" was called an *ariya* (noble) in the Buddha's time — not a racial category but a qualitative one. By this standard, someone with dark skin and curly hair who practices the path is noble; someone with the physical features of the "Aryan" who harms others is not.

On right livelihood, Goenka goes into remarkable detail for a TV broadcast: occupations that encourage others to break their precepts (weapon trading, animal fattening for slaughter, selling intoxicants, selling poison) are wrong livelihoods even if one does not personally kill. The *volition* behind the livelihood matters — a doctor who secretly wishes for epidemics to fill his clinic is not practicing right livelihood even though his profession is ostensibly healing.

The discourse includes personal testimony: a family doctor who told Goenka "usually there is some epidemic in the city in this season, this year for some reason there has been no outbreak" — with a sorrowful face. "What sort of a doctor is he?" And a broader meditation on how businessmen call a war or famine a "market improvement" because prices rise.

## Key Claims

1. **Dhamma = sīla + samādhi + paññā.** The entire Dhamma is contained in these three. "Any person following any tradition will accept that leading a life of good conduct is wholesome."
2. **"Ariya" is qualitative, not racial.** The word was corrupted over 2,500 years into a racial category. Its original meaning: one who has purified his mind and walks the Noble Path.
3. **Right speech = non-harmful, truthful, beneficial, actually practiced.** "The speech that is correct, truthful, beneficial, and has been applied by oneself."
4. **Right action = no killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, or intoxicants.** Physical actions that do not disturb the peace and harmony of others.
5. **Right livelihood = occupation whose volition is wholesome, and which does not encourage others to break precepts.** Weapon trade, animal fattening for slaughter, selling intoxicants, selling poison — all wrong regardless of personal non-participation.
6. **Volition is the true yardstick.** The doctor wishing for epidemics fails right livelihood even in a healing profession. The businessperson can fail despite technically legal trade.
7. **Earning wealth is not wrong for a householder.** "A householder should not beg money from others. By his effort, he should earn wealth honestly." But excess wealth should be returned to society to prevent ego inflation.

## The Sammā-Ājīvo Framework for Business Ethics

| Test | Question |
|------|----------|
| Harm test | Does this occupation harm others or disturb their peace? |
| Facilitation test | Does it encourage others to break their precepts (kill, steal, intoxicate, harm)? |
| Volition test | Is my mental intention genuinely to serve, or do I secretly wish for others' misfortune? |
| Honest quality | Am I cheating my consumer in quality, quantity, weight, or measure? |
| Excess sharing | Am I returning a portion of excess profit to society? |

## Notable Quotes

> "Dhamma is universal. Let a person call himself or herself by any name, it makes no difference. Let the person at least follow sīla, practice samādhi, develop paññā. Then only is such a person a truly Dhammic person."

> "By practicing Dhamma, a Hindu becomes a good Hindu; a Buddhist becomes a good Buddhist; a Jain becomes a good Jain... One becomes a good human being."

> "Today, the word ariya has become a general term denoting the race of a person. In those days it was not a word denoting the race of a person, it was a qualitative word."

> "Someone sells weapons, guns and bullets and yet thinks that one is very saintly. Oh no! That livelihood is a wrong one."

> "Even in the highest profession, it is very important to know the volition of the person who is engaged in it. If his mental volition is not good, however good the occupation may be, it will not be the right one."

> "Your consumer is your Lord who gives you your bread and butter. Therefore, even by mistake, do not cheat your Lord."
