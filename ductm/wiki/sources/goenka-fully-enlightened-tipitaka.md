# The Fully Enlightened One as Depicted in the Tipitaka

## Source Metadata

- **Author:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Original preface to *Tipitaka men Samyak Sambuddha* (first published in Hindi, 1995; re-issued in six volumes). The ~850-page work is described as a long introduction to the Tipitaka.
- **Significance:** Personal account of Goenka's journey from misconception about the Buddha to deep appreciation; argument for the Tipitaka as the authentic source of the Buddha's teaching

---

## Personal Narrative Arc

Goenka was born in Myanmar into a community that considered the Buddha the ninth incarnation of God — worthy of veneration but whose teachings were "not worthy of acceptance." His first 10-day Vipassana course (September 1955, under Sayagyi U Ba Khin) dissolved these misconceptions entirely.

> "From my very childhood in Myanmar, I had heard and believed that the Buddha was the ninth incarnation of God… his teachings are not worthy of our acceptance. How untrue did this belief turn out to be!"

His experience at the first course: overwhelming peace and tranquility after years of inner fires of lust, anger, and egotism. Discovery that the path was completely stainless, irreproachable, and universally acceptable.

## Three Types of Wisdom (Paññā)

A key conceptual contribution of this article:

| Type | Pali Term | Meaning |
|------|-----------|---------|
| Heard wisdom | *Suta-mayā paññā* | Accepted on faith/devotion from listening or reading |
| Intellectual wisdom | *Cintā-mayā paññā* | Logically analyzed and found rational |
| Experiential wisdom | *Bhāvanā-mayā paññā* | Arises from direct personal experience; this is real paññā |

> "Both of these [suta-mayā and cintā-mayā] are essentially the wisdom of others. The real wisdom is that which arises from our own direct and personal experience."

This framework is central to understanding why intellectual study of the Tipitaka must be combined with actual Vipassana practice.

## The Confluence of Pariyatti and Patipatti

- *Pariyatti* — Study of the Buddha's words (theory)
- *Patipatti* — Actual practice of Vipassana
- *Pativedha* — Direct realization/penetration of truth

> "The auspicious confluence of pariyatti and patipatti brought forth with greater clarity the pristine meaning of Dhamma."

Goenka describes diving into the Pali texts between 1962-1969, finding great joy and "a thrilling sensation throughout body and mind."

---

## Why India Lost the Dhamma

India lost both the technique of Vipassana and the Tipitaka literature, leading to millennia of misconceptions about the Buddha and his teaching. The result: great personalities going unrecognized in their own land, and India losing its role as world teacher.

> "India had forgotten that great historical personality who was absolutely stainless, free from any kind of deception, craft or guile, an embodiment of unbounded friendliness, love and compassion — a Great Man unparalleled not only in India but unique, peerless and un-excelled in the entire world."

## On the Pali Language

Pali (originally called Kosalī or Māgadhī) was the mother tongue of the Buddha — the lingua franca of the kingdom of Kosala and spoken throughout central India. It was the natural language of the people, not artificially created like Sanskrit. The word "Pali" literally means "that which protects" — this language protected and preserved the Buddha's teachings.

## The Tipitaka as a Garden

> "The Tipitaka is like a vast, captivating garden containing beautiful flowers of different hues and fragrances."

Goenka describes his book as a garland of flowers picked from this garden — an accessible introduction for those who cannot read the full Pali literature.
