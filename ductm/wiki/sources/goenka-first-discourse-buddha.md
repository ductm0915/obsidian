# The First Discourse of the Buddha

## Source Metadata

- **Author:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Written for Guru Pūrṇimā (full moon day of Āsādha, July 28, 2007) — the day commemorating the Buddha's first discourse at Sarnath
- **Significance:** Most detailed and systematic account of the Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta; explains the two extremes and Middle Path; provides the full three-aspect structure of all four Noble Truths; closes with the critical point that only direct experience liberates

---

## Background: The Five Companions

At the time of the Buddha, some held strongly that liberation requires strict asceticism. The Buddha's five former companions (Kondañña, Bhaddiya, Vappa, Assaji, and Mahānāma) abandoned him when he adopted the middle way and stopped self-mortification.

After enlightenment, the Buddha decided to teach them first. When they saw him approaching, they decided to show only basic courtesy — but as he came nearer, "his infinite love and compassion and the glow on his face attracted them, and they paid full respect to him."

---

## The Two Extremes to Avoid

> "These two extremes should be avoided by recluses:
> 1. Indulgence in sensual pleasures — base, vulgar, worldly, ignoble and not beneficial
> 2. Practice of self-mortification — painful, ignoble and not beneficial"

Abandoning both, the Buddha discovered the middle path.

## The Noble Eightfold Path

1. Right understanding (*sammā diṭṭhi*)
2. Right thoughts (*sammā saṅkappa*)
3. Right speech (*sammā vācā*)
4. Right action (*sammā kammanta*)
5. Right livelihood (*sammā ājīva*)
6. Right effort (*sammā vāyāma*)
7. Right awareness (*sammā sati*)
8. Right concentration (*sammā samādhi*)

## The Four Noble Truths

### 1. Noble Truth of Suffering
Birth, old age, disease, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, distress — all suffering. Disassociation from the pleasant is suffering. Not getting what one desires is suffering. In short: **attachment to the five aggregates is suffering.**

### 2. Noble Truth of the Cause of Suffering
Craving that causes rebirth, bound up with pleasure and lust:
- Craving for sensual pleasures
- Craving for repeated rebirth (existence)
- Craving for annihilation (non-existence)

### 3. Noble Truth of the Cessation of Suffering
Complete fading away and destruction of this craving; its forsaking, renunciation, liberation from it, leaving no place for it.

### 4. Noble Truth of the Path Leading to Cessation
The Noble Eightfold Path.

---

## The Three-Aspect Structure of Each Noble Truth

For each Noble Truth, the Buddha realized it in three ways:
1. *Sacca ñāna* — Acceptance of the nature of the truth
2. *Kicca ñāna* — Intellectual understanding of the effort required
3. *Kata ñāna* — Actual accomplishment

For the First Noble Truth: recognize dukkha (*pariññeyya*) → explore entire field of dukkha (*pariññātaṃ*)
For the Second: recognize craving as cause (*pahātabbaṃ*) → eradicate it (*pahīnaṃ*)
For the Third: recognize cessation (*sacchikātabbaṃ*) → directly experience it (*sacchikataṃ*)
For the Fourth: recognize the path (*bhāvetabbaṃ*) → develop it fully (*bhāvitaṃ*)

> *"Imesu catūsu ariyasaccesu evaṃ tiparivattaṃ dvādasākāraṃ yathā-bhūtaṃ ñāna-dassanaṃ suvisuddhaṃ ahosi"*

---

## Kondañña's Attainment

At the end of the discourse, the eldest companion Kondañña attained the first stage of saintliness (*sotāppana*) and realized:

> *"Yaṃ kiñci samudayadhammaṃ, sabbaṃ taṃ nirodhaddhamman"ti* — "Whatever is subject to arising is subject to cessation."

The Buddha exclaimed: "Friends, Kondañña has indeed understood!" (*Aññāsi vata bho Kondañño!*)

---

## The Closing Teaching

> "Accepting the truth merely at the devotional level because we have belief in the words of the Buddha, or at the intellectual level, will not liberate us. We have to realize the truth at the actual level, at the experiential level. This alone will liberate us."

The practice described: *yathā-bhūta-ñāna-dassana* — observing truth as you experience it from moment to moment. **This is Vipassana.**
