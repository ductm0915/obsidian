# The Gem Set in Gold: Pariyatti with Patipatti — S.N. Goenka (1985)

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker/Author:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Article in Hindi language Vipashyanaa Patrika, January-February 1985; also appeared in International Vipassana Newsletter, April 1986
- **Series:** Discourses on Tipiṭaka — Pāli
- **Tone:** Personal, reflective; written shortly after Sayagyi U Ba Khin's death anniversary (January 19, 1971)
- **Significance:** Articulates the proper relationship between study (pariyatti) and practice (patipatti); announces the intention to establish Pāli study alongside meditation centers

---

## Core Argument

Pariyatti (scriptural study) and patipatti (meditation practice) are like a gem set in gold — each enhances the other. Study without practice is sterile intellectualism. Practice without study loses depth and inspiration. The proper order is always: patipatti is the main aim; pariyatti supports it. The article announces a plan to establish Pāli study facilities near Vipassana centers, with the crucial condition that all participants must be regular meditators.

---

## Key Claims and Arguments

### The Golden Age of Goenka's Life (1964-1969)

When the Burmese government nationalized all businesses in 1964, Goenka lost his livelihood — but gained unlimited time:

> "The result was that my practice took wing. In meditation I could go more deeply than ever before, and when I read the words of the Buddha I would feel a thrill of delight throughout my body."

The daily routine: read a sutta, discuss it with Sayagyi U Ba Khin, then meditate on its content. Sayagyi explained texts according to the *experience of meditation* — not just grammatical analysis — penetrating to the most profound meaning.

> "As someone might card cotton or wool, combing out snarls and tangles, Sayagyi would remove all confusions, all ambiguities."

### The Metaphor: Gem Set in Gold

*Pariyatti* (study, words of the Buddha) = the gem  
*Patipatti* (practice, meditation) = the gold setting

A gem in a beautiful setting is enhanced by it — the beauty of each is amplified by the other. Study without practice produces intellectual confusion; practice with study produces deep, inspired progress.

> "Pariyatti and patipatti — study accompanied by practice — these two seemed to me like a gem, the beauty of which is enhanced by its golden setting."

### The Problem: Meditators Cut Off from the Words

At the time of writing:
- Tipiṭaka in Devanagari had gone out of print in India
- Hindi translations of portions were also unavailable
- Western students had English translations, but passages relating to meditation were often mistranslated in ways that confused meditators

Students who did Satipaṭṭhāna courses (studying the sutta while practicing) experienced deep inspiration — they wanted more access to the Buddha's words.

### The Danger: Study Replacing Practice

Critical warning:
> "Doing so, however, must never be at the expense of meditation practice. Otherwise, Vipassana centers might degenerate into places where people merely read, write or talk about the Dhamma, and the real purpose might be lost."

The aim is always liberation. Study is a means; practice is the vehicle. This warning is emphasized in bold in the original.

### The Proposal: Pāli Study at Dhammagiri

The article proposes establishing a Pāli study facility near Dhammagiri (the main Vipassana center in India), with these conditions:
1. All participants (students and teachers) must be regular meditators
2. Meditation must be an important part of the curriculum
3. Study of Sanskrit and Prakrit texts containing Vipassana references should also be included

Goal: enable meditators to study not only the Tipiṭaka but commentaries and subcommentaries — and in their own Indian languages.

## Notable Quotes

> "I felt sometimes as if the Buddha was speaking directly to me, as if every word was aimed specifically at me."

> "This was a veritable shower of nectar, the nectar of the Dhamma."

> "At home, I would read a sutta and then go to my Teacher, who would take up certain points from it and expound deeply on them."

> "Our aim is always to experience the Dhamma within ourselves in order to emerge from all suffering. And the means to do so is the practice of Vipassana meditation. Reading, writing and study are merely to find guidance and inspiration in order to go more deeply in the practice."

## Key Historical Facts

- **Sayagyi U Ba Khin died**: January 19, 1971
- **Goenka returned to India**: approximately 1969 (15 years before article, counting from 1985 — or the article mentions 25 years before his death, which was 1971 — 1971-25 = 1946; likely his return to India was ~1969)
- **50 assistant teachers** already conducting courses at time of writing
- **Centers in India and abroad** already established
- **Chattha Sangayana (Sixth Council)**: 1954-56, key to preserving Tipiṭaka purity
- **Dhammagiri**: the main Vipassana center in India (Igatpuri)
