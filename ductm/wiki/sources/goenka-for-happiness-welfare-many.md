# For the Happiness and Welfare of Many

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Extract from "Was the Buddha a Pessimist?" (translation of VRI Hindi publication *Kyā Buddha Dukkhavādī The?*)
- **Significance:** Refutes the criticism that the Buddha left home only for personal liberation or selfish curiosity; establishes his universal compassionate motivation

---

## Central Thesis

Siddhattha Gotama did not renounce household life for self-satisfaction or curiosity. He did so out of infinite compassion for all sentient beings trapped in the ocean of saṃsāra — and with the explicit intention to help all beings, not only himself.

> *"Kiṃ me ekena tiṇṇena, purisena thāmadassinā; Sabbaññutaṃ pāpuṇitvā, santāressaṃ sadevakaṃ."*
> "What is the use of I alone gaining liberation? After attaining perfect enlightenment, I should become helpful to men and gods in their liberation."

---

## Key Arguments

### 1. Compassion as Primary Motivation
When Prince Siddhattha witnessed old age, disease, and death, he was troubled not merely for himself but developed *infinite compassion* for all beings who suffer these miseries. The question in his mind was: "Is there a way out of this misery for *all beings*?"

### 2. Why Vipassana Required Leaving Home
The technique of Vipassana — the path to experience ultimate reality within oneself — had been lost at the time. Only various jhānas (absorption techniques up to the 8th jhāna) remained. Leaving home was necessary to seek out teachers of the Samaṇa tradition and eventually rediscover the technique. Had Vipassana been available, the going forth would not have been required.

### 3. The Bodhisatta Journey
The Bodhisatta fulfilled all ten pāramis across countless lives, specifically because he refused to take the shortcut of becoming an arahant (possible as far back as the time of Dīpankara Buddha four asankheya and 100,000 eons ago). He chose the harder path — full sammāsambuddhahood — so he could teach the way to countless others.

### 4. The Historic Exhortation to the Sixty Arahats
After teaching and establishing 60 arahats, the Buddha declared:
> *"Caratha, bhikkhave, cārikaṃ, Bahujanahitāya bahujanasukhāya lokānukampāya"*
> "Go your ways, O monks! for the welfare of many, for the benefit of many, out of compassion for the world."

He instructed them not to travel in pairs — so more people could benefit.

### 5. The Three Benefits of the Path
- Practice only **sīla** → happiness in this life and divine happiness after death
- Practice **samādhi** → bliss of absorption; brahmic happiness after death
- Practice **paññā** → eradication of all kammas → infinite happiness of nibbāna; the eternal, steadfast, deathless state

## Refutation of Criticisms

**Criticism 1:** "He left home only for curiosity / self-benefit, not to help others."
**Response:** The Buddhavaṃsa clearly records his compassionate intention. He could have become an arahant long ago if self-liberation were the only goal.

**Criticism 2:** "Truth is within; no need to leave home to find it."
**Response:** True — but the technique to experience it had been lost. The jhānas available were not sufficient and were actually misleading (mistaking the bliss of absorption for ultimate liberation). The Buddha had to rediscover the lost technique.

**Criticism 3:** "He abandoned his wife, child, and parents."
**Response:** By attaining enlightenment and teaching the Dhamma, he gave them the gift of *liberation* — far superior to worldly comfort. His entire family ultimately attained full liberation.

## Notable Quotes

1. *"How can a blind man show the way to another blind man? How can a handicapped person help another handicapped person?"* — On why self-liberation had to come first.

2. *"Teach the Dhamma that is beneficial in the beginning, beneficial in the middle and beneficial in the end; absolutely complete and totally pure."* — The Buddha's instruction to the 60 arahats.
