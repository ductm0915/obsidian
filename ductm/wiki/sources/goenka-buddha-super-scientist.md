# Buddha the Super-Scientist of Peace — Address at the United Nations, 2002

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Address at the United Nations on the occasion of Vesakha (full moon of May), 2002 — during Goenka's 35-city North America tour
- **Audience:** UN gathering; Myanmar and Sri Lankan delegations
- **Significance:** Most comprehensive single-lecture argument for the Buddha as empirical scientist rather than religious founder

---

## Central Thesis

The Buddha's greatest discovery: **craving and aversion arise in response to bodily sensations (vedanā), not directly in response to sense objects.** This discovery — that *vedanā-paccayā taṇhā* (sensation gives rise to craving) is the actual link in the chain of suffering — was what made him a Buddha.

> "This is the bold declaration of a supreme scientist. Just as whether there is a Newton or no Newton, the law of gravity remains true. Newton discovered it and explained it to the world."

---

## The Kalama Sutta: First Charter of Freedom of Thought

The text that drew Goenka into the Dhamma at age 31: do not accept anything because of tradition, scripture, logic, or teacher's personality. Accept only what you have verified through direct experience and found to be beneficial to all.

> "This was perhaps the first charter of freedom of thought in human history. There is no scope for blind belief or blind faith in the teaching of the Enlightened One."

## The Critical Discovery: Sensation as the Junction

> "The feeling of sensation is the crucial junction from where one can take two paths going in opposite directions."

- **Path 1:** React to sensations (pleasant → craving; unpleasant → aversion) → multiplies misery
- **Path 2:** Maintain equanimity toward sensations → changes deepest habit pattern → liberation

The pre-Buddha teachers taught "don't react to sense objects" — but that is the wrong level. The Buddha's insight (black bull / white bull / rope analogy): the *rope* (taṇhā) is the bondage. And taṇhā arises in response to vedanā, not directly to sense objects.

> *"Vedanā-samosaraṇā sabbe dhammā"* — All phenomena are rooted in sensation.

---

## The Root Metaphor

> *"Yathāpi mūle anupaddave daḷhe, chinnopi rukkho punareva rūhati"*
> Just as a tree with roots intact, though cut down, sprouts again — even so, while latent craving is not rooted out, misery springs up again and again.

---

## Dhamma vs. "Buddhism"

The words "Buddhism" and "Buddhist" do not appear in the vast Pali literature. The Buddha called his teaching *Dhamma* — the Law, the Truth. Buddhism as a label limits the teaching; *Dhamma* is limitless (*appamāṇo Dhammo*).
