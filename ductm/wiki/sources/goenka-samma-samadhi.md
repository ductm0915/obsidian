# Sammā Samādhi — S.N. Goenka

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Formal essay/discourse on the nature of right concentration
- **Series:** Discourses on Samādhi (Concentration) — Anapana Meditation
- **Tone:** Philosophical and technical; probably addressed to Vipassana meditators

---

## Core Argument

Mere concentration of the mind — regardless of the object used — is not sammā samādhi (right concentration). Sammā samādhi requires a wholesome (*kusala*), untainted mind established in equanimity. Only concentration free from craving, aversion, and ignorance qualifies as sammā samādhi, and only natural breath meets all the criteria for a universal, equanimity-preserving object.

---

## Key Claims and Arguments

### Defining Sammā Samādhi

> "Samādhi means that the mind is established in equanimity."

*Kusalacitt-ekaggatā* — one-pointedness of a wholesome mind — is the technical definition. A mind filled with craving, aversion, or ignorance that becomes concentrated is not sammā samādhi; it is merely concentrated impurity.

### Counterexamples of Wrong Concentration

Goenka offers vivid examples of high concentration that are NOT sammā samādhi:
- **The cat at the mouse-hole**: fully concentrated, but on craving for prey
- **The soldier in ambush**: fully concentrated, but on aversion and violence toward an enemy
- **The LSD user**: completely absorbed in hallucinations — concentration with ignorance
- **Emotional/devotional objects**: create craving for spiritual experiences, subtle bondage

All these produce concentration without equanimity — concentration on an *unbalanced* mind.

### The Problem with Supra-Sensory Experiences

Even pleasant visions, lights, sounds, or bodily ecstasies during meditation — though subtle — are forms of craving. They bind rather than liberate. A meditator who clings to such experiences as objects of concentration cannot reach full liberation.

> "If we regard them as objects of concentration, we shall again become entangled in craving. We shall not be able to reach the ultimate stage of full liberation of the mind."

### Why Natural Breath Is the Correct Object

Natural incoming and outgoing breath meets all necessary criteria:
1. **Neither pleasant nor unpleasant** — no craving or aversion is produced
2. **Keeps the mind alert** — prevents hypnotism, sleep, and delusion
3. **Universal** — requires no religious symbol, name, or form; acceptable to people of any tradition
4. **Natural** — any interference (pranayama, speed regulation) introduces artificiality; naturalness preserves direct contact with reality
5. **Bridge from gross to subtle** — breath becomes progressively subtler as concentration deepens, eventually reaching the level of subatomic vibrations
6. **Voluntary and involuntary** — allows transition from known (voluntary) to unknown (involuntary) aspects of the body

### The Breath as Bridge to Liberation

> "Breath can act as a bridge. For this reason too, it is useful as the object of concentration."

The ultimate truth is the truth of *this moment* — neither past nor future can be experienced. Breath observation trains the mind to live in the present moment, progressively unveiling subtler truths until the ultimate truth is realized.

The practice sequence: gross breath observation → increasingly subtle breath → cessation (*kumbhaka*) → ocean of inner vibrations → anicca at the atomic level → nibbāna.

## Notable Quotes

> "A cat with a fully concentrated mind has its full attention on a mouse-hole... This is the concentration of a mind filled with craving for the mouse or fish, it is not sammā samādhi."

> "We have to experience the ocean of infinite waves surging within, the river of inner sensations flowing within, the eternal dance of the countless vibrations within every atom of the body."

> "Without strong sammā samādhi, we cannot enter the depth of this moment; we cannot set foot in the field of paññā."

> "Pure samādhi developed in this way gives happiness. Come, let us develop samādhi by practicing awareness of the incoming and outgoing breath."

## Key Technical Terms

- **Sammā samādhi** — right concentration; one-pointedness of a wholesome mind
- **Kusalacitt-ekaggatā** — technical Pāli for sammā samādhi
- **Kumbhaka** — state where the breath stops completely during deep concentration
- **Nimitta** — a meditation sign or internal image (mentioned as a problematic object)
