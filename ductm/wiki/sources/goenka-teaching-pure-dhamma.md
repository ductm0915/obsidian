# The Teaching of Pure Dhamma

## Source Metadata

- **Author:** S.N. Goenka
- **Format:** Historical narrative of how the first Dhamma ambassadors spread the teaching through northern India
- **Significance:** Documents the sociological mechanics of how pure Dhamma spread; emphasizes self-reliance over guru-dependence; provides the story of King Pukkusāti as a case study in far-reaching impact

---

## The First Dhamma Ambassadors

The Buddha sent his sixty Dhamma sons in various directions as the first Dhamma ambassadors. Key features of their approach:
- They called it "Dhamma," not "Buddhism" (avoiding sectarian connotation)
- They emphasized the *practical* aspect, not just sermons
- People gained immediate tangible benefits, drawing others toward the practice

> "Had they taught Buddhism people might not have accepted it as it would have been just one of the many sectarian views. Dhamma actually belongs to all. It is universal, everlasting, crossing all boundaries of different sectarian views."

---

## Three Constituents of Dhamma

### Morality (Sīla)
Accepted readily because all traditions taught the importance of morality. The Dhamma ambassadors' insight: mind precedes all phenomena. Therefore, purifying the mind is the foundation of moral life.

> *"Manasa ce padutthena, bhasati vā karoti vā; Tato naṃ dukkhamanveti, cakkam'va vahato padaṃ."*
> "If with an impure mind one performs any action of speech or body, then suffering follows that person as the cartwheel follows the foot of the draught animal."

Conversely, with a pure mind, happiness follows as a shadow that never departs.

### Concentration of Mind (Samādhi)
The traditional approach was to concentrate through recitation of a deity's name — but this is sectarian. The universal method taught by the Dhamma ambassadors: observe the natural breath, without any word or imaginary belief combined with it.

Key principle: *"Atta hi attano nātho, ko hi nātho paro siyā"* — "One is one's own master; who else can be one's master?"

Individual liberation is in one's own hands. Therefore, one must make effort at one's own level — not depend on any other's grace.

### Wisdom (Paññā)
As concentration deepens, sensations arise near the nostrils and throughout the body. Three types of wisdom arise:
1. Heard wisdom (*srutamayī paññā*) — from teacher or text
2. Intellectual wisdom (*cintānamayī paññā*) — through reflection
3. Experiential wisdom (*bhāvanāmayī paññā*) — direct first-person knowledge; the only right wisdom

## Social Transformation through Practice

Practitioners directly witnessed changes:
- Wicked persons became righteous
- Drunkards got out of addiction
- Gamblers gave up gambling
- Adulterers gave up misconduct

This generated organic interest in others: "When they saw others reaping benefits from it they must have been attracted towards it."

## The Story of King Pukkusāti

Merchants from the Buddha's teaching area reached Takṣaśilā (Taxila, capital of Gandhāra). They told King Pukkusāti about the Buddha. The king abandoned his kingdom and walked on foot to Rājagiri to meet the Buddha.

The doors of the city were closed when he arrived. He stayed in a potter's inn — where, by chance, the Buddha was also staying. He received the Dhamma directly and attained the stage of Anāgāmī (Non-Returner). Going to fetch robes the next morning to receive ordination, he was gored to death by a bull.

**Significance:** Despite dying without formal ordination, he died as a noble one (*ariya*) — liberated from lower births. This story shows how the teaching reached as far as Gandhāra (Afghanistan/Pakistan border area) within the Buddha's lifetime.

## Self-Reliance as Core Principle

> "Someone may explain how to go on the path, but one has to walk on it on one's own. One cannot depend upon other's support and help — none can proxy for others."
