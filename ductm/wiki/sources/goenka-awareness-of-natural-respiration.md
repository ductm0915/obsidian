# Awareness of Natural Respiration — S.N. Goenka (Zee TV Discourse #6)

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Sixth in a series of 44 Hindi discourses broadcast on Zee TV (translated and adapted)
- **Audience:** General public, Hindi-speaking audience
- **Series:** Discourses on Samādhi (Concentration) — Anapana Meditation

## Core Argument

The mind wanders only between past and future — never in the present. By observing natural breath as a present-moment reality, the meditator begins to understand the nature of the mind and to train it to remain in the present. This present-moment awareness is the first step toward purifying the deep layers of the mind.

---

## Key Claims and Arguments

### The Mind's Two Wandering Zones

The mind only wanders in two areas: the past or the future. When past/future thoughts are agreeable, craving (rāga) arises; when disagreeable, aversion (dosa) arises. This continuous generation of rāga and dosa — along with moha (ignorance) — is the root of all suffering.

### Why Natural Breath as Object

- Breath is always in the present moment — it cannot be a past or future object
- No regulation, mantra, or visualisation should be added — only bare observation
- The moment the mind returns to breath, it is free of craving and aversion: a moment of purity

### The Purification Explosion

One moment of purity at the conscious level contacts the accumulated impurities at the deep level, producing an eruption — like cold water on burning coals, making a "chung" sound. Physical and mental discomforts during meditation (pain, fear, agitation) are signs of success, not failure — the "pus coming out of the wound."

> "The burning coals of craving, aversion and ignorance that one has accumulated within are smouldering... The contact of this internal burning with the coolness of this one moment of purity is like the contact of positive and negative forces."

### Present-Moment Living

Living in the present does not mean forgetting the past or not planning for the future. It means training the mind so that it can recall the past when needed and plan for the future more effectively — from a stable, concentrated base.

## Notable Quotes

> "One has not learned how to live; one has not learned the art of living."

> "_Bhasmācchanno va pāvako_ — The Buddha said that it is like burning coals covered by ash."

> "What seems to be a problem is actually a sign of success in the meditation. When one cuts open an abscess, pus is bound to come to the surface."

---

## Key Concepts Introduced

- **Rāga (craving)** and **dosa (aversion)** as the fundamental reaction pattern: "wanting, wanting" vs. "not wanting, not wanting"
- **Moha (ignorance)** — the mad-person example: mind jumps between delusions without sequence
- **Present-moment awareness** as the antidote to the wandering mind
- **Paritta citta** — the small surface part of the mind; the deep layers are where real work happens
