# The Essence of the Buddha's Teaching

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Talk given at Yangon University, Myanmar, September 6, 1991 (first of three). Edited and condensed. Published in International Vipassana Newsletter, Vol. 42 (2015), No. 2.
- **Audience:** Yangon University audience — mostly Burmese people deeply familiar with Buddhism
- **Significance:** Argues that the essence of the teaching — sīla, samādhi, paññā — is what matters, not the label "Buddhist"; uses Einstein/Newton analogy for Dependent Origination; vivid account of how Goenka himself experienced the practice

---

## Central Argument

> "This is the beauty of the Buddha's teaching: It is so complete that nothing has to be added to it. If you just practice sīla, samādhi and paññā, that is enough. And it is so pure that nothing has to be taken out."

The teaching becomes problematic only when made into a sect with philosophical dogmas, rites, rituals, and blind faith. The essence — sīla, samādhi, paññā — everyone is bound to accept because it is scientific.

---

## A Buddha's Relationship to Sects

> "A Buddha teaches Dhamma. A Buddha does not establish a particular religion. A Buddha is not interested in establishing a sect."

When the Buddha explained Dhamma to people: "I am not interested to make you my disciples. I am not interested to snatch you away from your old gurus... I am here to help you fulfill your aim — to come out of misery. I have a technique for doing that. Give it a trial."

---

## The "Buddhist" Paradox

> "If so many people in the world start calling themselves Buddhists, do you think Buddha would be very happy about that? Not at all."

The test is practice, not label:
- One who calls oneself Buddhist but does not practice sīla/samādhi/paññā: no benefit
- One who does not call oneself Buddhist but practices sīla/samādhi/paññā: gets the benefit, comes out of misery

---

## What the Buddha Actually Contributed

Other spiritual teachers said "live a moral life, develop control of the mind, purify your mind, remain detached." No one before the Buddha could explain *how*.

> "The biggest contribution of Buddha was that he explained how."

The Dependent Origination formula: *phassa-paccayā vedanā* (contact gives rise to sensation) → *vedanā-paccayā taṇhā* (sensation gives rise to craving). Most teachers saw craving arise in contact with sense objects. The Buddha saw it arise in response to *vedanā* (sensation) — the crucial missing link.

> "Just as Newton explained gravity, and Einstein explained relativity, the Buddha explained *paṭicca-samuppāda*, Dependent Origination. This is the law of nature."

---

## The Vibration Discovery

> *"Sabbo pajjalito loko, sabbo loko pakampito"* — The entire universe is nothing but combustion and vibration.

At the apparent level, things look solid. At the deeper level experienced through Vipassana: "It becomes divided, dissected, disintegrated, and you see that it is mere vibration, vibration, vibration."

The significance for *anattā* (no-self): When the ego dissolves through this experiential discovery, one understands by experience — not by philosophy — that "there is no soul; only mind and matter, and the interaction of the two."

---

## Goenka's Personal Transformation

> "I had been such an angry person, full of ego, but when I started applying the technique I found that these mental impurities started to dissolve. And not merely at the surface but at the deepest level of the mind."

Born in a Hindu family where scriptures taught "come out of craving and aversion" — but without explaining how. Sayagyi U Ba Khin gave him the technique and the experience: "This is where craving and aversion arise — at the depth of your mind, in the interaction of mind and matter within the framework of the body."

## Notable Quotes

> "The law of nature is applicable to everyone. It makes no difference whether you are a Christian or a Muslim or a Hindu or a Jain. It makes no difference whether you are Burmese or Indian or American or Russian or Chinese."

> "If the Buddha had simply given sermons, he would have been just another intellectual or philosopher, one of many in India. No, no. The Buddha was a practical person."
