# The Importance of Self-Observation — S.N. Goenka (Zee TV Discourse #4)

## Core Argument

The work of Vipassana is self-observation — observing the truth of the body and mind as it actually is (yathābhūta), without imposing any belief, word, or image. Adding any element to the breath observation — even neutral words like "coming" and "going" — converts the practice into a mantra meditation, blocking direct observation and preventing progress toward liberation.

---

## Key Claims and Arguments

### Starting with the Grossest Truths

The meditator begins with the grossest experiential truth — the incoming and outgoing breath — and progresses to progressively subtler truths until reaching the ultimate truth beyond body and mind. This is not belief; it is direct experience at every stage.

The path: gross body truth → subtle body truth → gross mind states → subtle mind states → truth beyond mind and matter.

### Why No Word or Image Should Be Added

Goenka acknowledges from personal experience that adding a word to the breath dramatically accelerates concentration:

> "I know from my own experience and from the experience of many others that if one adds any word to the incoming and outgoing breath and recites it repeatedly, the mind will quickly become concentrated."

But this ease comes at a cost: the word eventually dominates and the breath disappears. The mind becomes immersed in the word (or image), not in the breath and body. Once this happens, the work of understanding the body-mind truth stops — and with it, the eradication of defilements.

### The Sectarian Problem

Any word or image added to breath practice comes from a specific religious tradition and excludes others. The entire path of Dhamma must be universal:

> "Everyone can observe breath. Whether a person is Hindu or Buddhist or Jain or Muslim or Sikh or Parsi or Jewish, he can easily observe breath without any hesitation."

Even neutral words like "coming" and "going" eventually become a mantra and block the work.

### The Kabir Confirmation

The saint Kabir independently reached the same conclusion about the obstacle of internal chanting:
> *"Tāgā tūtā, nabha mem vinasagā, sabad ju kahām samāyī re."*
> — "The string has broken; it cannot remain in the universe within. How can the word that is so gross remain!"

The subtle states within cannot be reached while a gross verbal construct occupies the mind.

### Patanjali's Confirmation

Influenced by Vipassana, Patanjali said: *"Drastā drśīmātra"* — In seeing there should be only seeing. This objective observation ultimately dissolves the "I am observing" until only bare awareness remains — sammā diṭṭhi (right understanding).

### The Breath-Negativity Link

During observation, when a negativity arises and the meditator notices the breath becoming fast and heavy — and then returning to normal as the negativity passes — the direct experiential understanding emerges: negativities and breath are intimately linked. This understanding cannot arise if words or images have replaced attention to the breath.

## Notable Quotes

> "_Thāpiā na jāi, kītā na hoi, āpe āpi niranjanu soi._" — Do not impose any belief, word, or shape. Whatever is happening, just observe it.

> "Observe the breath, as it is, _yathābhūta_ — as it is, not as you would like it to be."

> "Along with concentration of mind, one should purify the mind. If the mind is becoming free from negativities, it will bring happiness, it will bring peace, it will bring liberation."

---

## Key Technical Terms

- **Yathābhūta** — "as it is"; observing reality without projection or imposition
- **Ajapā jāpa** — un-chanted chant; the internal echo that spontaneously arises from long mantra practice and becomes an obstacle
- **Nimitta** — internal meditation sign; an object of visualization that can concentrate but also derail
- **Sammā diṭṭhi** — right understanding/right view; the outcome of bare, objective awareness
