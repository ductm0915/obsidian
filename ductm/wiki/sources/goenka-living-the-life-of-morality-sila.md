# Living the Life of Morality (Sīla Discourse) — S.N. Goenka (Hyderabad 1993)

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Public discourse at Hyderabad, India, 1993 — second in a two-evening series
- **Audience:** General Indian public
- **Series:** Discourses on Sīla (Morality)
- **Note:** This is the Sīla series version; an identical text also appears in the Samādhi series. See also [[goenka-living-the-life-of-morality-samadhi]] for the parallel version with cross-reference context.

## Core Argument

The word "Dharma" has been corrupted in India to mean sectarian religion, losing its original meaning as the universal law of nature. The first prerequisite of Dhamma is morality (sīla) — not sectarian obligation, but because generating impurity in the mind in order to break sīla immediately punishes oneself. Morality is therefore in one's own self-interest. Knowing this intellectually is not enough: the second prerequisite is mastery of the mind through meditation, since even the wisest person (like Duryodhan from the Mahābhārata) cannot follow what they know to be right without mind-training.

## Key Claims and Arguments

### Dhamma vs. Sect (Universal Law vs. Religion)

Dharma as "sanātana" (eternal) is the universal law of nature — not a sectarian label. The word has been corrupted so that "Hindu-dharma," "Buddhist-dharma," etc., are now treated as different Dharmas. The sooner the distinction between Dhamma (natural law) and sampradāya (sect) is restored, the easier it will be for people to practice.

### The Five Precepts as the Yardstick

Sīla = abstaining from actions that disturb others' peace and harmony:
1. Not killing
2. Not stealing
3. No sexual misconduct
4. No lying/deception
5. No intoxicants

These are not merely religious rules but a "yardstick" of Dhamma. The concept of *samvibhāga* (sharing wealth back to society) was an ancient add-on that addressed the intoxication of wealth accumulation — distinct from *dāna* (charity) which can inflate the ego.

### Three Levels of Explanation for Sīla

1. **Childlike (carrot-and-stick)**: Heaven if you follow; hell if you don't
2. **Rational (golden rule)**: Would you want this done to you? Then don't do it to others
3. **Social**: You are a social being; disturbing others disturbs yourself
4. **Deepest (mature)**: Breaking sīla requires generating impurity first — you harm yourself before others

### Immediate Natural Punishment

> "As soon as a negativity arises in the mind, simultaneously misery starts arising in the mind. There is no time gap."

Unlike legal punishment, which can be evaded through lawyers and courts and time, Dhamma's punishment is instantaneous and unavoidable. Pure mind generates peace immediately — no waiting needed.

### The Duryodhan Problem

From the Mahābhārata:
> *"Jānāmi dharmaṃ na ca me pravrittī, jānāmi adharmaṃ na ca me nivrittī."*

Everyone is like Duryodhan — knowing the right thing but unable to do it because they have no mastery over the mind. The solution is not more preaching; it is a practical technique for mind-mastery that the ancient sages provided alongside their ethical teachings.

### Survey of Ancient Concentration Techniques

To solve the Duryodhan problem, ancient India developed many mind-training techniques:
- Word/mantra repetition (like lullaby)
- Visual contemplation (nimitta — clay disk, idol)
- Chakra visualization
- Auto-suggestion / philosophical auto-affirmation ("I am the immortal soul...")

All work to concentrate the mind, but the truly enlightened teachers realized concentration alone is not enough — the base must be *purity*, not merely power.

### Purity as the Essential Base for Concentration

A concentrated but impure mind is dangerous — its power can harm others. The correct technique is one where:
1. One remains with the experienced reality, moment to moment
2. The reality is personal and experiential, not borrowed from scripture or teacher
3. The truth of the body is explored directly

> "The reality experienced by Gotama made Gotama a fully enlightened one, a buddha. It cannot make you a buddha unless you realize it yourself."

## Notable Quotes

> "Dharma is Dharma. If we take Dharma out of this mire of sectarianism, then it will become very pure."

> "At the deeper level of Dharma, you are not obliging anybody by practicing sīla, morality. You are obliging yourself."

> "Dhamma teaches us not to oblige others, but to oblige themselves. If I live a life of morality, I am obliging myself."

## Key Concepts

- **Sanātana Dharma** — the eternal law of nature (not sectarian)
- **Samvibhāga** — sharing of wealth back to society; contrasted with ego-inflating dāna
- **Duryodhan problem** — the universal gap between knowing what's right and doing it
- **Mastery of mind** as the second prerequisite of Dhamma after sīla
