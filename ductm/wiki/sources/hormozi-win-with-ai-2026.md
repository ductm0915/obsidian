## Core Thesis

AI will never be worse than it is right now. The question is not whether to adopt it, but whether *you* will be the one who controls the automation — or someone else will do it to you first. Humans plus superior technology always beat humans with inferior technology, across every age of history.

---

## Key Arguments

### 1. AI Adoption Urgency
- "AI will never be worse than it is right now" — any rate of improvement makes adoption the #1 priority.
- It takes ~20 hours to become proficient at any new skill. But people delay the first hour for *decades*.
- Practical shortcut: take a weekend, build something small, your understanding exceeds any fear-mongering article.
- Jerome Powell data point: effectively zero net private sector job creation — people are automating jobs away, not a weak economy.

### 2. Workflow-Based Thinking (not role-based)
- Old paradigm: "I need to hire an editor."
- New paradigm: "What are the 5 tasks an editor does? Each one should be a workflow."
- For every hire, write 4–10 tasks that person does with their hands/eyes/mouth. Ask: can each live inside a workflow instead of headcount?
- Org charts organize *humans*. Workflows organize *inputs and outputs*. A manufacturing business already thinks in I/O — service businesses should too.
- See: [[workflow-vs-role-thinking]]

### 3. BYOS / BYOA — The New Employee Value Proposition
- Medium-term future: BYOS (Bring Your Own Software) and BYOA (Bring Your Own Agent/Agents).
- One person can walk into a company and say "I am your entire marketing department" — with agents backing them.
- Example: Anthropic reportedly had 1 person in their marketing department. Possible because that person deployed agents.
- This creates enormous earning power: sell entire department output, share in equity, or get outsized cash.
- See: [[byos-byoa]]

### 4. Train AI Like an Employee
- AI is trained via reinforcement just like humans — pattern recognition + reward signal.
- Bad output = bad training, not bad AI. Don't fire the employee after the first imperfect deliverable.
- Concrete example: "Write email copy" → AI slop. vs. "Here are 12 rules you can never break and 16 writing samples of mine" → 5× better output.
- 100 feedback cycles = 100 minutes with AI vs. 1.5 years with a human.
- See: [[ai-training-method]]

### 5. Revenue Per Employee
- Hormozi's AI-native companies: millions per employee per year — possible because "day one we started that way."
- Existing large-org incumbents face a structural disadvantage: sunk headcount, emotional resistance to change, reluctance to have hard conversations.
- A startup with no org chart simply doesn't have those conversations to have — they just automate from birth.

### 6. Practical Action Plan
1. Write every task you do daily at the most granular level (not "run ads" — but: make campaigns, set budgets, analyze results, make creatives, write copy, test headlines...).
2. Take the first task. Put it into AI: "Help me automate this. What steps would you take?"
3. Take the first step on the list. Do it. When stuck: screenshot the screen → "What do I do now?"
4. Everyone has an AI tutor at their fingertips — they're just not using it.

### 7. Barbell Strategy for the Future
- **High-risk / high-reward end:** Go fully AI-native. Have hard conversations. Automate ruthlessly. Raise the bar; people who meet it stay, people who don't leave.
- **Low-risk / timeless end** (Jeff Bezos frame — bet on what won't change):
  - Humans will still have bodies → health, fitness, food, supplements.
  - More automation → more leisure → more entertainment demand.
  - Housing / shelter needs remain.
- See: [[barbell-strategy-ai]]

### 8. Phase Shift Metaphor (Brian Johnson / Blueprint)
- "You've been training your whole life to swim. You go from lap pool → lake → open ocean. But the change coming is a *phase shift*: the water boils, evaporates, and now you're flapping your arms in gas. It doesn't matter how good of a swimmer you are."
- Past skill mastery doesn't transfer when the *fundamental physics of the environment* changes.
- See: [[phase-shift-metaphor]]

### 9. Operational Leverage
- Cost of labor → near zero with AI. Coordination costs (the biggest cost of scaling) → near zero.
- Humans are slow to update price sensitivity. If you can still charge $2,000/month for a service that now costs you $5/month — the margin explosion is transformational.
- One person + agents = millions in revenue. No coordination overhead. See: [[horizontal-leverage]]

### 10. The Risk Premium
- In a world where AI labor and intelligence approach zero cost, the last thing humans get paid for is **taking risk** — which only a human can truly bear.
- Money will still exist. Labor value will not (for most work).
