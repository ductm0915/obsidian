# A Dhamma Decision

A personal account by [[sn-goenka]] describing how he refused to travel to Nepal illegally or deceptively to conduct Vipassana courses, choosing instead to uphold moral integrity ([[sila-samadhi-panna]]) even when doing so meant delaying the spread of Dhamma.

## Summary

After successfully conducting courses at the Indian side of the Indo-Nepal border (Raxaul, September 1972), Goenka received persistent requests from Nepali students to conduct courses inside Nepal. As a Myanmar citizen, he was legally restricted to travel only within India. Multiple influential Nepali contacts offered to help him cross the border without detection — either by dressing as an Indian, or by entering in the car of a wealthy industrialist — but Goenka declined every offer, citing the same standard held by his own teacher Sayagyi U Ba Khin.

Goenka recounts that U Ba Khin himself had been unable to travel to India because the Myanmar government's policy demanded one either emigrate permanently or hold foreign employment to receive a passport endorsement — and he refused to obtain a fraudulent employment letter. This precedent was Goenka's lodestar: how could one teach pure Dhamma if the first step was taken against sīla?

The article concludes with retrospective satisfaction: because Goenka waited and eventually obtained the right to travel legally, the purity of his Dhamma work was never tainted.

## Key Claims and Arguments

- **Sīla is the prerequisite for effective Dhamma teaching**: Any compromise of ethical conduct at the outset poisons the entire Dhamma mission. "How could he teach pure Dhamma when the first step itself was taken against sīla?"
- **Moral responsibility outweighs political and legal obstacles**: The issue was not only legal but a matter of personal moral responsibility. Even a "safe" or "noble-cause" deception is still deception.
- **U Ba Khin as ethical exemplar**: Goenka explicitly invokes his teacher's refusal to use a fictitious employment letter as the model for his own decision — showing continuity in the teacher-disciple moral standard.
- **Patience vindicated by results**: "When the time ripened, I could visit Nepal for Dhamma work without deception. And Dhamma spread as my teacher predicted."
- **The Dhamma journey cannot begin on an impure foundation**: This theme — that means must be as pure as ends — recurs throughout Goenka's teachings.

## Notable Quotes

> "How could he teach pure Dhamma when the first step itself was taken against sīla? How could the Dhamma journey be really successful?"

> "I felt strongly that being a citizen of Myanmar, unless the Myanmar Government gave me permission I should not go to any other country beyond India, however noble the cause may be, however safe the journey may be."

> "Today when I review all this past, I feel a sense of great satisfaction in Dhamma: my decision was really correct. If I had broken rules, ignored the moral issue and gone to Nepal, then the purity of serving Dhamma would have become tainted. And this would have been piercing me like an arrow throughout my life."

- **U Win Pe** — Cabinet Secretary of Myanmar Government; student of Sayagyi U Ba Khin.
- **Col. Kyaw Soe** — Home Minister of Myanmar Government.
- **Col. Maung Maung Kha** — Eventually Prime Minister of Myanmar; a close friend of Goenka who was nonetheless unable to change passport policy.
- **U Thi Han** — Former Foreign Minister of Myanmar; facilitated Goenka's original passport to India.
