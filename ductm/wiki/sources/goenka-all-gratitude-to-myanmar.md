# All Gratitude to Myanmar

A historical essay by [[sn-goenka]] tracing the deep roots of the India–Myanmar relationship through ancient trade, the earliest contacts between Myanmar and the Buddha, and the unbroken preservation of Vipassana in Myanmar over 2,500 years — culminating in the technique's return to India in 1969.

## Summary

Goenka begins with pre-Buddhist history: Indian merchants had established settlements in Myanmar centuries before the Buddha, giving local towns Indian names still in use today. He then narrates three key transmission moments of Dhamma to Myanmar:

1. **Tapassu and Bhallika** (the Buddha's lifetime): Two merchant brothers who were first to offer the Buddha food after his enlightenment, received his hair relics, and enshrined them in the Shwedagon, Sule, and Botathoung pagodas. They later returned to learn Dhamma from the Buddha personally. This was the first transmission.

2. **Arahant Gavampati** (eight years post-parinibbāna): Taught Dhamma to King Siharaja of Suvannabhūmi (southern Myanmar/Thailand/Malaya peninsula), the second transmission.

3. **Arahants Sona and Uttara** (post-Third Council, under Emperor Asoka): Sent by Venerable Moggaliputta Tissa to Suvannabhūmi; first taught the Brahmajāla Sutta to purify wrong philosophical views and establish pure patipatti (meditation practice).

Goenka traces the subsequent centuries of preservation: the Aris monks' corruption in northern Myanmar, King Anuruddha's (Anawratha's) conquest of Thaton to retrieve the Tipitaka, the arahant Dhammadassi's role in re-establishing pure Dhamma, and the Sagaing Hills as a continuous meditation center.

The essay culminates with the figure of **Ledi Sayadaw** (~150 years ago), who democratized Vipassana by training lay teachers (previously restricted to monks), and **Saya Thetgyi**, the lay farmer he trained. Saya Thetgyi in turn trained **Sayagyi U Ba Khin**, through whose compassion Goenka received the technique and brought it back to India in 1969.

## Key Claims and Arguments

- **Vipassana is the life-transforming core of the Buddha's teaching**: Sīla alone is incomplete; the full Noble Eightfold Path requires all three trainings. Any arahant establishing Dhamma abroad would necessarily have taught Vipassana, not merely morality.
- **Myanmar as the custodian of pristine patipatti**: While Vipassana was lost in India ~500 years after the Buddha, it survived in southern Myanmar in an unbroken teacher-student tradition through centuries, and was eventually revived across all of Myanmar by Dhammadassi/Ledi Sayadaw.
- **Ledi Sayadaw's democratic breakthrough**: By opening Vipassana to laypeople (not just bhikkhus), Ledi Sayadaw fulfilled the prophetic preparation for the 2,500-year second sāsana cycle, enabling the global spread of Vipassana.
- **Debt of gratitude as ongoing Dhamma practice**: "To be grateful is strong evidence of progress in Dhamma. Gratitude is abundantly beneficial." The debt to Myanmar is owed by every Vipassana meditator.
- **Dhamma is complete at every stage**: "Every step taken on the path of Dhamma gives healthy, wholesome results here and now; no step, no effort taken on the path is wasted."

## Notable Quotes

> "Not only I but all Vipassana meditators who have benefited from this technique are grateful to Myanmar, and will always remain grateful."

> "How can any Vipassana meditator ever forget the debt to Myanmar? To be grateful is strong evidence of progress in Dhamma."

> "It is true that the pure Dhamma, which the arahants Bhallika and Gavampati had taught to the people in the form of Vipassana, became contaminated within 275 years after the time of the Buddha."

> "Ledi Sayadaw was a great saint with considerable foresight. He had complete faith in the prediction that 2500 years after the Buddha, the Vipassana technique of pure Dhamma would arise again, return to India, and spread to the whole world."

> "Dhamma is munificent in the beginning, Dhamma is munificent in the middle, Dhamma is munificent in the end."

## Historical Figures Mentioned

- **Tapassu and Bhallika** — First two lay disciples of the Buddha; from Balhika (Afghanistan); brought hair relics to Myanmar; became arahant (Bhallika) and sotāpanna (Tapassu) after hearing Dhamma at Rajagaha. No entity page yet.
- **Arahant Gavampati** — Resident of Mithila; went to Suvannabhūmi after attaining arahanthood; established Buddha-sāsana there 8 years after parinibbāna. No entity page yet.
- **Arahants Sona and Uttara** — Dhamma ambassadors sent by Moggaliputta Tissa post-Third Council; established pure patipatti in southern Myanmar. No entity page yet.
- **King Anuruddha (Anawratha)** — 11th-century king of Pagan who recovered the Tipitaka from Thaton, re-establishing pure Dhamma in northern Myanmar. No entity page yet.
- **Arahant Dhammadassi** — Re-established pure Dhamma in central and northern Myanmar under Anuruddha; later meditated at Sagaing Hills. No entity page yet.
- **Ledi Sayadaw** — ~150 years ago; opened Vipassana to laypeople; trained Saya Thetgyi. No entity page yet.
- **Saya Thetgyi** — Lay anāgāmi farmer-teacher trained by Ledi Sayadaw; trained Sayagyi U Ba Khin. No entity page yet.
- **Sayagyi U Ba Khin** — Direct teacher of Goenka. No entity page yet.
