# The Simple Teaching of the Buddha

## Source Metadata

- **Author:** S.N. Goenka
- **Format:** Article on the spread of the simple, anti-dogmatic Dhamma through northern India; includes Goenka's personal story and the Kālāma Sutta
- **Significance:** Explains why the Kālāma Sutta was the gateway into Dhamma for Goenka; provides a detailed treatment of how blind beliefs destroy the ability to think; uses the example of ritual river bathing as a case study

## The Kālāma Sutta: The Buddha's Instructions to the Kālāmas

The Buddha visited Kesamutti (village of the Kālāmas) and gave the following instruction:

Do not accept anything merely because:
1. It has been heard repeatedly
2. It is traditional, accepted through legends
3. It is in accordance with scripture
4. It is in accordance with logic
5. It is based on reasoning
6. Its presentation is pleasant and attractive
7. It is according to our notion and belief
8. The speaker's personality is very attractive *(the Buddha explicitly points this toward himself)*
9. The speaker is venerated by us *(again, explicitly toward himself)*

**The standard:** Accept only the truth which you know by your own experience, that these qualities are skillful, blameless, and that welfare and happiness lies in practicing these qualities.

## Goenka's Personal Entry Point

Born and raised in a staunch Hindu family in Burma, Goenka was the leader of the Indians — particularly Hindus. He considered the Buddha venerable as an incarnation of Vishnu, but believed the Buddhist precepts were "meant for wicked people" and would lead one to hell.

When circumstances brought him to Sayagyi U Ba Khin's Vipassana Centre, he picked up a book on the Kālāma Sutta while waiting. His reaction:

> "I was convinced that there was no place for Gurudom here in the name of Dhamma. I realized that this person does not give importance merely to acceptance and consequently is not spreading blind belief. On the other hand, he gives importance to understanding the truth by one's own experience."

This assurance that there was no beguilement led him to sit in the course.

## How Blind Beliefs Destroy Thinking

> "When a person drowns himself in blind beliefs, then he does not even make use of his common sense."

Example of river-bathing ritual: If bathing in a sacred river washed away sins, then fish, frogs, tortoises, and crocodiles — always in the river — would all be guaranteed heaven. Thieves and murderers who bathe would qualify for heaven while honest people who don't bathe would not.

> "When such wrong beliefs become prevalent then misdeeds are encouraged instead of good deeds."

The underlying mechanism: selfish priests authenticate blind beliefs with scriptural justifications and threaten hellfire for questioning. Fear prevents rational thinking.

---

## Ritual Bathing as Case Study

In hot climate, bathing in rivers is natural and hygienic. The problem: *linking this activity with Dhamma* — asserting it washes away sins. This:
1. Pollutes the pure Dhamma based on the principle of kamma (action and its result)
2. Exploits weak people with false assurances
3. Makes the inferiority complex of people worse (told they are too weak to eradicate defilements through meditation)
4. Spreads easily and takes strong roots across generations

The Buddha's alternative: practice Dhamma based on wholesome actions, not on ritual.
