# The Buddha and the Teacher-Disciple Tradition

A discourse by [[sn-goenka]] given on Dhamma Cakka Pavattana Day (Asadha Pūrnimā), calling meditators to express deep gratitude to the Buddha and the teacher-disciple lineage by maintaining Dhamma's pristine purity — specifically by keeping it free from sectarian identification.

## Summary

Goenka opens with reflection on the Buddha's incomparable sacrifice: having the potential to attain arahanthood quickly (as the Brahmin Sumedha in a past life, in the presence of Dīpankara Buddha), he instead chose countless more lifetimes of effort to become a Sammāsambuddha — so that he could save countless beings. "What great compassion! What a great sacrifice!"

The discourse then addresses the teacher-disciple tradition that preserved Dhamma in Myanmar for centuries after it was lost in India (~500 years after the Buddha). Goenka calls for deep gratitude to Sayagyi U Ba Khin as the immediate predecessor in this chain.

The central argument: true gratitude is not expressed in praise but in keeping Dhamma in its pristine purity — never allowing it to become "Hindu dharma," "Bauddha dharma," "Jain dharma," or any other sectarian form. Sectarian labels reduce Dhamma to a social identity rather than a living experiential practice.

Goenka draws a sharp distinction between "belonging to a religious community" (a social function, acceptable in itself) and "calling that community's rites and rituals Dharma" (a fundamental distortion). The true definition: one is Dhammic when the mind is free of defilements; one is non-Dhammic when defilements arise, regardless of religious label.

The discourse closes with practical benchmarks for measuring one's progress: "Am I really feeling the sensations? Am I seeing them as impermanent (anicca)? Am I getting established in equanimity?"

## Key Claims and Arguments

- **Dhamma is eternal and universal law, not sectarian property**: "The truth, the law of Dhamma today is the same as it was millions of years ago... it is absolute, universal and applicable to all, and at all times, everywhere." Connects to [[universal-dhamma]].
- **True gratitude = living purity**: Praise of the teacher lineage is hollow without personal Dhamma progress and preservation of the teaching's purity.
- **Experiential knowledge over intellectual belief**: "This profound truth must not be accepted blindly just because a guru has said so... Let us know it for ourselves at the experiential level." Connects to [[saddha-enlightened-faith]].
- **Daily practice as the living test**: Morning and evening sittings serve as constant verification of whether one is truly growing in Dhamma or merely performing a ritual.
- **Self-monitoring benchmarks**: Feeling sensations → recognizing anicca → maintaining equanimity. These three are the measurable indicators of genuine Vipassana practice. Connects to [[anicca-impermanence]] and [[continuity-of-practice]].
- **Dhamma is not sectarianism**: The confusion between "dharma" and "sampradāya" (sect/tradition) has been India's spiritual misfortune for 1,500–2,000 years.
- **Mind as root; sensations as field**: "When the mind is filled with defilements, then any action that is done at the vocal or physical level will also be impure, since every action arises first in the mind." Connects to [[karma]].

## Notable Quotes

> "Had he not searched for it, we would certainly not have found this teaching. Indeed after rediscovering it, if he had not shared it so freely then we too would have been deprived of it."

> "Dhamma is all pervading, universal. How can that which rules over every tiny particle of existence, living and non-living belong to any one community?"

> "It is an immense misfortune that the words 'dharma' and 'sampradāya' have become synonymous in our country."

> "There is no harm in calling oneself a Hindu, a Buddhist or a Jain. But there is a major offence in stating that 'this is Hindu dharma, Bauddha dharma or Jain dharma' — it is vital that we understand the difference."

> "A true meditator understands the eternal law of nature or Dhamma at the experiential level. Not through blind faith in a teacher or scriptures, but through his own experience."

> "Keep checking to see if Dhamma is deepening within, if it is manifesting in your daily life... Am I really feeling the sensations? Am I really seeing and appreciating them as impermanent (anicca)? Am I getting established in equanimity? These are the benchmarks."
