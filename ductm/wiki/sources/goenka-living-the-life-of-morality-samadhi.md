# Living the Life of Morality (Samādhi Discourse) — S.N. Goenka (Hyderabad 1993)

## Source Metadata

- **Speaker:** S.N. Goenka
- **Context:** Public discourse at Hyderabad, India, 1993 — second evening in a two-part series
- **Audience:** General Indian public
- **Series:** Discourses on Samādhi (Concentration) — Anapana Meditation
- **Note:** Identical or near-identical text appears in the Sīla discourse series under the same title; this version is presented as part of the Samādhi series with the theme of Dhamma vs. sectarianism

---

## Core Argument

The word "Dharma" has been corrupted in India to mean sectarian religion, losing its original meaning as the universal law of nature. The first prerequisite of Dhamma is morality (sīla) — not because of sectarian obligation, but because breaking any moral precept requires generating impurity in the mind, and impurity punishes the person immediately and simultaneously. Morality is therefore in one's own self-interest.

---

## Key Claims and Arguments

### Dhamma vs. Sect

> "Dharma is universal, boundless, infinite and limitless. Dharma is for all, for everyone."

Sects (Hindu, Buddhist, Jain, Muslim, etc.) are limited; Dhamma is unlimited. A person may be a devout member of any sect and still not be Dhammic if they do not live morally.

### The Five Precepts as Foundation

Sīla = abstaining from actions that disturb the peace and harmony of others:
1. Not killing
2. Not stealing
3. No sexual misconduct
4. Not lying
5. Not taking intoxicants

A sixth (no gambling) and broader understanding of wealth-as-intoxicant were historically added. The concept of *samvibhāga* (sharing with society, not *dāna* which inflates ego) was part of the original moral framework.

### Three Levels of Explanation for Sīla

The enlightened ones adapted their explanations to the audience's maturity:
1. **Childlike**: "Observe precepts and gain heaven; break them and go to hell"
2. **Rational**: "Would you want someone to do this to you? Then don't do it to others" — golden rule
3. **Social**: "You are a social being; disturbing others disturbs yourself"
4. **Mature (deepest)**: "Breaking sīla requires first generating impurity in the mind — you harm yourself first, before you harm others"

### Immediate Punishment by Nature

> "As soon as a negativity arises in the mind, simultaneously misery starts arising in the mind. There is no time gap."

Unlike legal punishment (which can take years and may be avoided), Dhamma's punishment is instantaneous and unavoidable. Conversely, pure mind is rewarded with peace and happiness here and now.

### The Duryodhan Problem

Quoting from the Mahābhārata:
> *"Jānāmi dharmaṃ na ca me pravrittī, jānāmi adharmaṃ na ca me nivrittī."*  
> — "I know very well what Dharma is, but my mind does not want to follow it..."

Everyone knows intellectually what is right, yet cannot follow it — because they have no mastery over the mind. Therefore the second prerequisite of Dhamma (after sīla) is mastery of the mind through meditation practice, not mere sermons.

---

## Notable Quotes

> "Dhamma nirapekśha (indifferent to Dharma) government cannot exist. A government has to be Dharma sāpekśha: secular, non-sectarian."

> "At the deeper level of Dharma, you are not obliging anybody by practicing sīla, morality. You are obliging yourself. It is in your own self-interest."

> "You can't kill somebody unless you generate a tremendous amount of anger, hatred, ill will and animosity... You may kill that person later on, but you will be the first victim."

---

## Key Concepts

- **Sanātana Dharma** as the eternal law of nature, not sectarian religion
- **Samvibhāga** — sharing wealth back to society (contrasted with dāna which can inflate ego)
- **Immediate natural punishment** — the simultaneity of generating negativity and experiencing misery
- **Mastery of mind as prerequisite** — sīla alone is insufficient; mind-training is the second step
