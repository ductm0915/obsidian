# The Four Noble Truths

## The Four Noble Truths: Brief Summary

1. **Noble Truth of Suffering:** the entire field of suffering has to be *known by experience* (not merely accepted intellectually)
2. **Noble Truth of the Arising of Suffering:** the eradication of craving has to be *experienced*
3. **Noble Truth of Cessation of Suffering:** the realization of Nibbāna
4. **Noble Truth of the Path:** has to be *developed by repeated practice*

> "Hearing these truths and understanding them at the intellectual level is good but not sufficient. To benefit from these truths, it is essential to gain knowledge about them through experiential wisdom."

## The Arahant as Established in Wisdom (*Sthitaprajña*)

The article cites the Bhagavadgītā (2.57-58) on *sthitaprajña* — one established in wisdom — and connects this to the arahant who has fully comprehended the four Noble Truths by direct experience:

> *"Yathindakhīlo paṭhaviṃ sito siyā, catubbhi vātehi asampakampiyo..."*
> "Just as Indra's pillar, firmly embedded in the earth, remains unshaken by winds from the four directions — similarly I declare as truly virtuous that person who has fully comprehended the four Noble Truths by direct experience. Such an arahant remains unshaken under all circumstances."

---

## Two Common Misconceptions Refuted

**Misconception 1:** The teaching is utterly pessimistic — the word "suffering" is used four times while happiness is not mentioned at all.
**Response:** The cessation of suffering (*dukkhanirodha*) IS nothing but supreme happiness. This becomes clear through practice of Vipassana — not through argument.

**Misconception 2:** How can suffering be called "noble"?
**Response:** When one of the four Noble Truths is thoroughly experienced, it makes one a noble person. The experience, not the concept, is what transforms.
