# The Cliff

**The Cliff** is [[nicholas-cole]]'s conclusion technique: end a piece on the last main point — no recap, no bow — and let the reader fall off the edge.

## The Principle

Most writers feel obligated to wrap up with a summary or grand closing. Cole argues this wastes the reader's time and dilutes impact. If you don't have something *fundamentally different* to say in a conclusion, don't write one.

Instead: deliver exactly what the headline promised, then stop. The reader's reaction becomes:

> "This was so valuable. You didn't waste my time. I'm ready for the next thing."

Then they click to your next piece.

## Why It Drives Binges

Each piece ends with the reader slightly suspended — satisfied but not fully released. This creates a pull toward the next piece. Cole credits the cliff with readers reporting they "went down a rabbit hole" and read 100 articles in one sitting.

The cliff works because it **optimizes for trust and respect**, not closure. You said what you promised. You left. That signals confidence and respect for the reader's time.

## When to Use It

- Default: use the cliff unless you have something genuinely new to add
- Avoid: rehashing main points, "in conclusion…" openers, motivational platitudes

## Two Rules for Conclusions (When You Do Write One)

1. Say something **fundamentally different** from everything before it
2. If you can't — use the cliff instead
