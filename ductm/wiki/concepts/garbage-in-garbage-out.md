# Garbage In, Garbage Out

A universal law: **outputs are determined by inputs**. No matter how sophisticated the system (human mind, software algorithm, company culture), the quality of outputs cannot exceed the quality of inputs.

Originally a software engineering term; Sam Ovens extends it to human cognition, talent assessment, and success.

---

## The Three Domains

### 1. Chef's Table / Food Analogy
The world's best chefs are not distinguished by technique — they are distinguished by ingredient sourcing. Some started their own farms and obsessed over soil composition, animal diet, and climate. The input determines the output more than the processing.

### 2. Genius Social Networks (Dean Simonton research)
Simonton analyzed journals/correspondence of Newton, Galileo, Einstein, Copernicus. Finding: geniuses are created by the inputs of other geniuses.

Newton consumed 14 "idols" (Bacon, Copernicus, Galileo, Kepler, ~10 others). Einstein's greatest influence was Newton. Newton himself said: "If I have seen further, it is by standing on the shoulders of giants."

The chain: prior geniuses → Newton → Einstein → next generation. Each genius was first a high-quality input consumer.

### 3. Human Capital Assessment
Sam's hiring insight after 436 interviews:
- Asking about **outputs** (skills, experience, past work) = easily faked, unreliable
- Asking about **inputs** (who influenced you, what books shaped your thinking) = nearly impossible to fake

People who genuinely care about a field can recall names, titles, specific learnings instantly. Those who can't name a single influencer or book are universally poor performers.

---

## Practical Applications

**For yourself:**
Audit your current inputs:
- Who are the 5 people you spend most time with?
- What books have you read in the last 12 months?
- What information do you consume daily (social media? news? books? courses?)

If the answers are social media, news, and drinking friends → expect poor outputs.

**For hiring:**
Ask: "Who has influenced the way you think about [field]?" and "What books have taught you most about [field]?"
- Strong candidate: rattles off names and titles with specific learnings
- Weak candidate: vague, can't remember anyone, says "many people but I can't name them"

**For building a product or company:**
What inputs is your product made of? What quality of data, feedback, and talent is going in? The output cannot be better than the inputs.

---

## The Meta-Principle

This law holds across:
- Software systems (bad data → bad model predictions)
- Human minds (junk media → poor thinking)
- Companies (poor talent + unclear problems → poor products)
- Communities (low-quality members + weak culture → poor outcomes)
- Meals (bad ingredients → bad food regardless of chef technique)
