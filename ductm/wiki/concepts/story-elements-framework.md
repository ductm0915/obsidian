# Story Elements Framework

[[alex-hormozi]]'s anatomy of a story: seven structural components plus a dual-track delivery requirement (external + internal journey). Used for writing books, creating marketing content, and teaching through narrative.

---

## The Seven Elements

| Element | Definition | Question it answers |
|---|---|---|
| **Setting** | Where and when this is happening | Where are we? |
| **Character** | The person doing the doing | Who is this about? |
| **Desire** | What they want | What are they after? |
| **Struggle** | Why they can't get it | What's in the way? |
| **Eureka** | The moment that changes things | What shifted? |
| **Victory** | Achieving the goal | Did they get it? |
| **Resolution** | What happens after | What does it mean? |

Note: Not all stories need all seven — but the framework helps identify what's missing when a story falls flat.

---

## The Dual Journey: External + Internal

Every story has two simultaneous tracks:

- **External journey** — what you can see with your eyes if you were in the room (actions, events, outcomes)
- **Internal journey** — what you'd have to *experience as the character* (emotions, doubts, fears, realizations)

**Rule:** Weave both together. A victory that feels empty (external win, internal hollow) is still a great story — *if you tell both sides.*

**Why it matters:** Most business storytellers only tell the external journey (results, metrics, achievements). The internal journey is what creates emotional resonance and trust.

## Craft Techniques

### Show, Don't Tell

Demonstrate the situation rather than labeling it.

**Bad (telling):** *"Cheryl walked in. She was his ex-wife."*  
**Good (showing):** *"He struck the spot on his finger that was still bleached from where his wedding ring used to be."* — Stephen King

Applies equally to copy, ads, and marketing content.

### Five Senses

Set the scene by describing what the character smells, hears, sees, touches, and feels — not by narrating summary.

> *"My back was sore because I'd been sitting for hours — I could feel the back of the chair against me."*

### Selective Detail

You don't need to describe everything. Only call out details that *change the story*. If deer vs. buffalo on the wall doesn't alter meaning → omit it.

Test: *"If I changed this detail, would it change how the audience understands what happened?"* If no → cut it.

---

## Stakes and Struggle

The primary driver of interesting stories. See [[stakes-and-struggle]].

> *"I was hungry, I couldn't find food, I ordered Uber Eats, I ate."* — a story. Not interesting.

Add stakes: *"I'm diabetic. If I don't eat I could die. Vision narrowing, cold sweats. Is this how they're going to find me?"* — same plot, completely different story.

Game shows exist because they literalize stakes with money. Stakes = why the audience should care what happens next.
