# LTV to CAC

## Definition
The fundamental economic unit of any business that determines its scalability.
- **LTV (Lifetime Value):** Specifically, the Lifetime *Gross Profit* (LTGP) generated by a customer over the duration they do business with you. (Revenue minus cost of goods sold/fulfillment).
- **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost):** The total cost required (marketing, sales, software, etc.) to acquire that customer.

## The Ratio
The **LTV:CAC ratio** describes how much money it costs you to make more money. 
- A benchmark for a healthy, scalable business is typically **3:1** (you make $3 in gross profit for every $1 spent acquiring the customer).
- Exceptional scaling opportunities exist when ratios hit 10:1, 30:1, or even 100:1. When a business hits these ratios, the objective is to aggressively pump capital into acquisition to capture market share before efficiency naturally drops.

## Benchmarks by Business Model Type
The "3:1" rule only applies to fully automated businesses. As humans enter the loop, you need more padding to absorb ramp periods and performance variability of new hires:

| Human Presence | Minimum Target LTV:CAC |
|---|---|
| All automated (SaaS, software-only) | 3:1 |
| One human in loop (salesperson OR delivery) | 6:1 |
| Two humans (sales + delivery) | 9:1 |
| All human (attraction + sales + delivery) | 12:1 |

**Why the padding?** Every time you hire a new salesperson or delivery person, their early performance is below par. You need enough buffer in the ratio to absorb that ramp period without going below breakeven.

Gym Launch Year 1 LTV:CAC: **100:1** — an exceptional arbitrage window that has repeated for Hormozi four times in his career at 30:1+.

## Application in Advertising
All advertising works if the LTV is high enough. If Starbucks makes $14,000 in LTV per customer, they can afford to spend up to $4,600 to acquire a new customer and still maintain a 3:1 ratio. 
To improve this ratio, you must either:
1. Lower CAC (by getting better at front-end marketing and optimizing the [[value-equation]])
2. Increase LTV (by increasing price, reducing churn, or selling additional products/services)
