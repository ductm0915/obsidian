# AAA Method (3A Reframing)

## Definition
The AAA Method is a framework for handling objections via **Reframing** — the 1–3 sentences said after a prospect says anything but "yes" that increase the likelihood the next thing you say gets them to buy. The person asking the questions controls the conversation.

## The Three Steps
1. **Acknowledge**: Validate the prospect's concern without agreeing that it prevents a purchase. Repeat what they said back to them — this demonstrates active listening and buys you time to think.
2. **Associate**: Connect their concern to a broader context, a past experience, or another person. This gives the prospect a positive **identity label** to live up to (e.g., "you seem like a smart person"). That label can be brought back at the close to create alignment.
3. **Ask**: Redirect the conversation by asking a targeted, qualifying question. Universal go-to: *"What are you most afraid of?"* — this gets to the heart of the issue fastest.

## 5 Rules for Ethical Use
1. **Never answer a question you don't know the answer to** — reframe instead of guessing.
2. **Never answer an unasked question** — don't volunteer objections the prospect hasn't raised.
3. **The nastier the prospect, the nicer you get** — escalate kindness, not combativeness.
4. **Match or exceed emotional intensity** — validate at the level of their concern ("That's a totally reasonable concern") before reframing.
5. **Retain childlike curiosity at all times** — seek to understand, not to win. Say "Huh, that's so interesting…" to buy time and shift tone.

## Key Principle
> "You don't close sales by being right. You close sales by making the prospect right."

The goal is to understand, not to beat them. If they feel you won an argument logically, you've lost the sale.

## The Five Objection Types (from [[hormozi-sell-better-99]])

All objections trace to [[blame-distortions]] — three CBT distortions manifesting as five surface types:

### 1. Time
- **Macro** ("I'm in a busy season"): "Do you want this to last long-term? You'll always be busy — best to start when it sticks."
- **Micro** ("I have no time in my day"): "The program will remove the 90% of things creating overwhelm, not add to them."
- **When-Then fallacy** ("When I have time, I'll start"): Show the logical inversion — "I'll save money once I'm rich."

### 2. Money/Value
- **A lot is good**: Skin in the game predicts follow-through.
- **Relative value**: If it adds $10K/month, is $3K a lot? The objection isn't price — it's belief.
- **Time or money**: "You're going to buy the program anyway — question is, pay in money or pay in years."
- **Resourceful not resources**: Every self-made millionaire started broke. They were just resourceful.

### 3. Fit (Not sure it's for me)
- **New identity, new priorities**: "Show me where someone spends money and time and I'll show you their future."
- **Change the change**: "What you've been doing has been getting what you've been getting."
- **Hypothetical close**: "If this were perfect, would you do it?" — if no, the issue is trust, not fit.

### 4. Authority (Spouse/partner not present)
- "What do you think they'd object to?" — overcome the absent person's objection directly.
- "Does your spouse approve of your *current struggle*? Then why would they object to fixing it?"
- "You're asking for *permission* when you should be asking for *support*." (Support vs. Permission frame)

### 5. Avoidance / Stall ("I need to think about it")
- **Past frame**: "This isn't a fast decision — you've been making this decision for 6 years."
- **Don't let the bad investment burn you twice**: Past failures shouldn't prevent good present decisions.
- **Present frame (rocking chair close)**: "Thinking about it" means waiting for time, but time doesn't give you more information — only I do. Let's answer the questions now.
- **Future frame**: "Are you tired of another year of almost?" The cost is inaction, not investment.
- **Decision framework**: Do you believe this will work? Do you trust me to deliver? Do you believe it'll work for *you*? If yes to all three — do you have access to funds?
