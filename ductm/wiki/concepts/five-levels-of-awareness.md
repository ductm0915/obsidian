# Five Levels of Awareness

## Definition
A psychological marketing framework originally developed by Eugene Schwartz in his book *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966). It categorizes target audiences based on how much they know about their own problems and the solutions available in the market.

## The Five Levels
1. **Unaware:** The prospect doesn't know they have a problem, or won't admit it. This requires the longest "runway" and most education to make a sale.
2. **Problem Aware:** The prospect knows they have a problem (e.g., "I have back pain") but doesn't know there are specific solutions.
3. **Solution Aware:** The prospect knows solutions exist (e.g., chiropractors, massages) but doesn't know about *your* specific offering.
4. **Product Aware:** The prospect knows what you sell but isn't quite convinced it's right for them yet. Needs proof and a strong offer.
5. **Most Aware:** They know you, they trust you, and they've likely bought from you. Just tell them the price and where to buy.

## Application in Advertising
As noted by [[alex-hormozi]], "all advertising works." When ads fail, it's typically a mismatch between the messaging and the audience's level of awareness. A highly technical ad detailing specific product features will bomb with a "Problem Aware" audience. Conversely, a broad educational ad will bore a "Product Aware" audience who just wants a discount code.

The bigger the market you wish to address, the lower down the awareness funnel you must go, which requires significantly better, "front-to-back" marketing hooks.
