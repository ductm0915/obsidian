# Stakes and Struggle

[[alex-hormozi]]'s identified #1 driver of story interest. A technically complete story (setting, character, events, resolution) can still be boring if it lacks stakes. Stakes define what's at risk if the character fails; struggle is the mechanism of failure being possible.

---

## The Core Insight

> *The reason game shows are inherently interesting is that they literally put money on it.*

Stakes = why the audience should care what happens next. Without stakes, a story is just a sequence of events.

---

## Demonstration

**No stakes:**
> "I was at home. I was hungry. I couldn't find any food. I realized I had my phone and ordered Uber Eats. It arrived. I ate. I was satisfied."

A story. Not interesting.

**With stakes:**
> "I was hungry. I'm diabetic — if I don't eat I could die. There was no food in the house. As I was rummaging around, my vision started to narrow, cold sweats. I started thinking: is this how they're going to find me? My body on the floor. My family's out of town. My kids — what are they going to think?"

Same plot. Completely different experience. The only change: what was at risk.

---

## What Creates Stakes

1. **Physical stakes** — health, safety, life (diabetic example above)
2. **Financial stakes** — money at risk, business survival
3. **Relationship stakes** — missing kids' birthdays, partner's ultimatum
4. **Identity stakes** — reputation, credibility, self-concept
5. **Social stakes** — public embarrassment, community judgment
6. **Time stakes** — deadline, irreversibility, opportunity closing

The strongest stories layer multiple stake types simultaneously.

---

## Struggle Enables Stakes

Stakes alone aren't enough — there must be genuine struggle (a real possibility of failure). If the outcome is obviously predetermined, even high stakes feel hollow.

The struggle must be:
- Believable (could actually fail)
- Personal (character has skin in the game)
- Relatable (audience can imagine being there)

---

## Application in Business Storytelling

- **Founder stories**: What was *actually* at risk? Not just "we had to pivot" — but "I had $6,000 left and rent was $5,000. I had one month."
- **Customer stories**: What would have happened if they *hadn't* found the solution? Make the before-state genuinely terrible.
- **Teaching content**: What happens to someone who doesn't learn this? Name the cost explicitly.
