# Tiered Pricing Model

## Definition
A pricing architecture where each successive tier is **5–10× the price of the previous**, with roughly **20% of the previous tier's customers** upgrading. This mirrors the power law distribution of spending power — and concentrates profit at the top.

## The Rule
> For every new tier: **5–10× the price, expect ~20% uptake.**

Each tier should at minimum **double total revenue** — otherwise the operational overhead of managing it isn't worth it.

## Why 5–10×, Not 1.5–2×?
Incrementally higher prices don't work because buyer willingness-to-pay doesn't scale incrementally. A $1,000 buyer and a $1,500 buyer have effectively the same psychology — you're not reaching a different wealth band. To tap a new buyer segment, you need a price jump large enough to attract genuinely different spending power.

## Example: 1,000 Customers, Four Tiers

| Tier | Price | Customers | Revenue |
|---|---|---|---|
| Base | $10/mo | 800 | $8,000/mo |
| Tier 2 | $100/mo | 160 | $16,000/mo |
| Tier 3 | $1,000/mo | 32 | $32,000/mo |
| Tier 4 | $10,000/mo | 6 | $60,000/mo |

**Total revenue:** $116,000/mo vs. $8,000/mo base-only — a **14.5× increase** with only a fraction of additional customers.

## Profit Concentration
Once overhead is covered by the base tier, higher tiers generate almost pure profit. Hormozi's example: adding a tier that generates $100/mo in revenue when your cost is already covered can contribute $80/mo profit while your original $80/mo had only $10/mo profit — a 5× profit multiplier.

## Hormozi's Real Ladder (Acquisition.com)
- Skool hobby: ~$99/mo

- L1 advisory: $5,000
- L2 advisory: $35,000
- L3 advisory: $135,000

## Absolute Profit > Relative Thinking
Don't think in percentages — think in absolute dollars:
> 1 person × $10,000 (costs $2,000) = **same profit** as 400 people × $50 (costs $25)

Large prices in small quantities are vastly underestimated.

## How to Build It
1. **Start at the highest tier you can operationally deliver** (see [[top-down-brand-positioning]])
2. Add tiers one at a time — don't build the whole ladder at once
3. For each new tier: define what you'd be "happy to deliver for 5–10× the price"
4. Expect many more "no's" at higher tiers — that's correct, not a signal to lower price
5. Use [[close-rate-pricing-ladder]] to diagnose if your current prices are too low

## Common Mistakes
- Pricing Tier 2 at 1.5× Tier 1 — doesn't reach a different buyer segment
- Abandoning the tier because "most people say no" — of course they do; that's the math
- Serving the bottom 50% with a manually-delivered service — no margin, no leverage
