# What-Who-When Framework

## Definition

An operational execution framework that turns every meeting action item into three clear components: **What** needs to be done, **Who** owns it, and **When** it will be completed (measured in hours, not days).

## The Three Levels

### Level 1: What (most teams stop here)
"Hey, we need to do this thing." → Everyone leaves, nothing happens.

### Level 2: What + Who (better)
"This is the task. You own it." → Accountability exists, but no urgency.

### Level 3: What + Who + When (execution machine)
"This is the task. You own it. How many hours will it take?" → If they say 90 minutes, it's done by end of day, not end of week.

## Key Insight: Hours, Not Days

Ask **"how many hours will this take?"** instead of **"what day can you have it done?"**

- If someone says "Friday" (3 days away) but the task takes 90 minutes → you just lost 2.5 days.
- Asking in hours surfaces the true size of the work and allows immediate scheduling.
- **This can 7x team speed** — switching from weekly deadlines to same-day deadlines.

## Pull Up Timelines

The rationale for aggressive timelines:

> "The reason I'm going to drive this outcome even though there's no apparent outside urgency is because it assumes the future is going to be exactly like today — and one of the things I can guarantee is that the future won't be."

Use up all available renewable resources now. When the unexpected arrives, you'll want everything behind you already done.

## Stack & Recap Method

During meetings, recap each action item every time a new one is added:
- Item 1 gets repeated ~7 times by meeting's end (freshest → needs least repetition)
- Item 4 gets stated once (most recent → still top of mind)

**Nuclear option:** "Repeat it back to me." → 1 out of 3 times they get it completely wrong. Catches miscommunication before it becomes wasted work.

## More Deadlines = More Praise

> "The more deadlines you have, the more opportunities you have to say 'nice job.'"

Rather than one weekly check-in with one "good job," create 7 daily milestones with 7 opportunities for positive reinforcement.
