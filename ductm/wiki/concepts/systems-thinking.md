# Systems Thinking

**Referenced from:** [[sam-ovens]] (Billionaire Mind)
**Related:** [[second-order-consequences]], [[scale-mess-debt]]

## Core Idea

Systems thinking is the discipline of understanding how parts of a whole interrelate and work together over time — seeing the forest, not just individual trees. It rejects siloed, linear, cause-and-effect thinking in favor of recognizing feedback loops, emergent properties, and non-linear dynamics.

## Key Principles

### 1. Emergence Over Silos

Systems produce outcomes that no individual component can produce alone. A business team's output is not the sum of individual contributions — it's an emergent property of how they interact.

### 2. Feedback Loops

Every output becomes an input to the next cycle:
- **Positive feedback loops** amplify (growth → more resources → more growth)
- **Negative feedback loops** stabilize (overheating → correction → balance)

Most people see linear chains. Systems thinkers see loops.

### 3. Non-linear Effects

Small changes in one part of a system can produce disproportionately large effects elsewhere. This makes prediction difficult but leverage possible — find the right leverage point and a small intervention creates massive change.

### 4. Unintended Consequences

Actions designed to fix one part of a system often create problems elsewhere. See [[second-order-consequences]] — most business failures come from ignoring these knock-on effects.

## Application to Business

| Linear thinking | Systems thinking |
|---|---|
| "Fire the underperformer" | "Why does this role consistently underperform? What system created this?" |
| "We need more sales" | "What feedback loops drive sales? Where are the bottlenecks?" |
| "Cut costs to increase profit" | "What second-order effects will cost-cutting produce?" |
