# Sell the Vacation (Not the Plane Flight)

## Definition
A pitch principle by [[alex-hormozi]]: always sell the **final destination** (the desired outcome), never the **process of getting there** (modules, workouts, macros, tech stack, TSA lines). The vehicle is irrelevant — the dream is what people pay for.

## The Analogy
Everyone who books a flight wants to get to Maui.  
Nobody books because they love TSA, layovers, middle seats, and lost luggage.  
When you pitch your modules/deliverables/features, you're selling the **plane flight**.  
When you pitch the transformation, you're selling **Maui**.

## Different Levels of Service = Same Destination
The genius of this frame: you always sell the same thing.  
What varies is the *speed and quality of the journey*:
- Swim to Maui (DIY, risk of drowning)
- Boat (slow, no Wi-Fi)
- Commercial flight (standard, some discomfort)
- Private jet (fastest, premium)

All arrive at Maui. Price anchors naturally to the *how fast* and *how comfortable*, not *whether*.

## In Practice
Instead of: "Our program includes 12 coaching calls, a nutrition guide, and a private Slack community…"  
Say: "When you came in, you said you want to get to 140 lbs. Here's what it takes to get there — and I guarantee you get there."

The three-pillar pitch (e.g., Fitness / Nutrition / Accountability) should be delivered via **short anecdotal stories**, not feature lists:
- Explain *why* accountability matters → brush-teeth story: external accountability becomes internal habit.
- Don't describe the coaching call. Describe the transformation it creates.

## Why It Works
> "At the end of the day, the reason they're going to buy is because of how understood they feel."

People don't buy deliverables. They buy an image in their mind of who they'll be after. Pitch features and they hear *work*. Pitch outcomes and they hear *desire*.

The pitch should be ≤3 minutes — all time saved goes into making them feel heard.
