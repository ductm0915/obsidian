# Deprivation-Motivation Theory

## Definition
Motivation is proportional to the perceived gap between where you are and where you want to be. The larger the gap, the more motivational force. This explains why motivation to achieve wealth is *not* simply a function of how little money you have.

## Core Logic

**Physiological deprivation** (hunger, thirst, sleep) is automatic: the more deprived, the more motivated.

**Financial deprivation** is *psychological*: you only feel poor if you perceive a large gap between current and desired state. This gap is entirely determined by your reference group.

- A person earning $5K/month who wants $10K = 50% of goal
- A person earning $1M/month who wants $10M = 10% of goal
- **The millionaire is more deprived by this measure, and therefore more motivated.**

## Implications

### 1. Rich people are more motivated by money than poor people
Counter-intuitive but logical: rich people compare to richer peers, making the gap larger. Poor people surrounded by equally poor peers feel less deprived by comparison.

### 2. Environment sets your standard
*"The #1 predictor of wealth is the ZIP code you're born in"* — not IQ, gender, or race. The ZIP code determines your comparison set, which sets your standard, which sets your motivation level.

Corollary: "Hang around five people with 10× your net worth and you'll feel poorer than you ever did."

### 3. To get motivated: change your comparison set
You can manufacture deprivation by surrounding yourself with people who have more. This creates the felt gap that drives motivation without requiring you to actually be poor.

### 4. Compare to future self (clean version)
If comparing to richer people feels unhealthy, compare to the person you *genuinely want to become*. This creates the gap without envy.

## Sell at the Point of Deprivation
This principle also applies to sales timing (see [[sell-at-point-of-deprivation]]): sell at maximum gap/hunger, not after the client has already consumed value (and felt temporary satisfaction).
