# Fear vs. Regret

## Core Principle

**Change is scary, but so is regret. The life you live depends on the one you fear most.**

The key equation for taking action: **fear of regret must supersede fear of rejection.** The more successful version of you doesn't lack fear — they have a different hierarchy of fears.

> *"Losers fear rejection, winners fear regret."* — [[alex-hormozi]]

---

## The Two Fears

| Fear | Type | What triggers it | Long-term consequence |
|------|------|------------------|-----------------------|
| **Rejection** | Immediate social pain | Reaching out, trying, failing publicly | Avoidance → stagnation → regret |
| **Regret** | Delayed existential pain | Looking back on unlived choices | Drives action in the present |

The trap: rejection is vivid and near, regret is abstract and distant. Our nervous system defaults to avoiding the vivid. The reframe: make regret *vivid* — and rejection abstract.

---

## Fear Only Exists in the Vague

Fear disappears when you spell it out in specific steps. Hormozi's personal process for quitting his consulting job:

> *"There's this huge amorphous fear, but I've just noticed that fear only exists in the vague. It doesn't exist in the specific."*

**Plan B Decomposition technique:** When afraid, ask: what *exactly* happens if I fail?
1. Would I be homeless? (Probably not — know enough people to get food)
2. What's the worst? (Couch surf, some shame)
3. Then what? (Apply for the job back, or use the story at a business school interview)

Once decomposed: "My plan B is just a cool experience I can talk about at an interview. That doesn't actually sound that bad."

In the developed world, **the only real downside risk is the opinion of people who will call you a failure — people you don't care about anyway.** You won't die. You'll just learn.

---

## Distance = Measure of Power

> *"You can measure how powerful someone is by the distance between when they make a decision and when it happens in reality."*

- Hormozi took 6 months to act on a decision to quit his job
- Long gap = impotence; short gap = power
- "If you want to feel impotent, take as long as possible between decision and action"

---

## The Rejection Paradox

Most attempts fail — failure is a **prerequisite** for success, not evidence against it.

> *"Greatness rejects all first-time applicants."*

Getting a job = multiple interviews, rejections, resumes → one "sale" (someone says yes). Business works identically. The volume of rejection required is the same; the question is only whether you can endure it.

Champions distinction: they don't love winning more than others — they **hate losing more**. And they expected to win because of their preparation level, not optimism.

---

## Application

- Before any fear-driven avoidance: decompose the fear into specific steps and trace what actually happens
- Reconnect with your future self — what will you think at 70 about this moment of hesitation?
- Use the "die a pansy" heuristic: "I cannot die a pansy. I have to be able to make a jump."
- Audit the gap between your decisions and your actions — it's a measure of current power
