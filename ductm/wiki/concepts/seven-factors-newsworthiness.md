# Seven Factors of Newsworthiness

A framework from journalism adapted by [[alex-hormozi]] for writing better stories, copy, and ads. The more of these seven factors present in a story, the more inherently interesting it becomes. Originally from a textbook on ad writing shared with Hormozi by Dr. Kashi.

---

## The Seven Factors

### 1. Recency
How close was the event to when you're telling the story?

- Recent = more interesting (why "hop on trends fast" works)
- The older the story, the harder you must work on the other 6 factors to compensate

### 2. Impact
How relevant is this event to the reader/listener?

- A story with no connection to the audience's life has limited pull
- **Author's job:** select stories that connect, AND explicitly bridge why this matters to the specific reader
- Then tie to the action you want them to take

### 3. Prominence
Are prominent, recognizable characters involved?

- Kim Kardashian sneezing = interesting; random person sneezing = not
- Involve prominent figures (directly or as comparison/contrast) to borrow their story gravity
- Explains why Hormozi name-drops Arnold Schwarzenegger, Stephen King, Ben Horowitz, etc.

### 4. Proximity
How close to home is this for the audience?

- A house fire → somewhat interesting; *your neighbor's* house fire → much more interesting
- For local ads: "Towson, Maryland" headline outperforms "Baltimore, Maryland"
- The closer the geography, industry, role, or life situation → the more compelling

### 5. Conflict
Is there tension, opposition, or unresolved mystery?

- Natural conflict (weather, market crash) or interpersonal conflict both work
- Conflict creates unresolved tension → the brain needs resolution → attention stays
- Related to [[open-loop-technique]]: conflict is what makes the open loop compelling

### 6. Unusualness
Is there something odd, bizarre, or unexpected?

- Pattern interruption: the brain expects → gets something different → diverts resources to reconcile
- If you meet expectations, the brain dismisses the story ("matches known pattern, nothing to learn here")
- If you break expectations, the brain allocates attention to resolve the mismatch

### 7. Updatability
How many times can you give updates on the same story?

- News media exploits this ("breaking news — live from the scene")
- In content: multi-part series, cliffhanger episodes, ongoing sagas
- Less applicable to fixed single-scene stories, but relevant for ongoing brand narratives

---

## How to Apply

**When choosing which story to tell:** rank candidate stories on all 7 factors. The one with the most factors wins.

**When telling a chosen story:** if some factors are weak, engineer them:
- Recency: frame in present tense ("I'm dealing with this right now")
- Impact: explicitly state the bridge ("This matters to you because...")
- Proximity: use the audience's specific language, industry, and situation

**Minimum viable story:** 2–3 factors = already more interesting than most content. Don't need all 7.
