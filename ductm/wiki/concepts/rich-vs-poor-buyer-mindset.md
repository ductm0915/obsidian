# Rich vs. Poor Buyer Mindset

## Definition
Wealthy and non-wealthy buyers have fundamentally different psychological frameworks for evaluating purchases. Confusing the two is a primary cause of mispricing and ineffective sales.

## The Core Distinction

| | Poor Buyer | Rich Buyer |
|---|---|---|
| **First question** | "How much does it cost?" | "For what?" |
| **Mental frame** | **Cost** (absolute number) | **Ratio** (value-to-price, ROI) |
| **Response to high price** | "That's expensive." | "What do I get?" |
| **Shopping behavior** | Low-to-high (find cheapest) | **High-to-low** (find best) |
| **What they want** | Savings | Speed, ease, guarantee, quality |
| **Negotiation style** | Nickels and dimes on every line | Won't quibble if value is clear |

## The $20,000 Test
> Tell a poor person: "I've got something for $20,000." → "That's expensive."
> Tell a rich person: "I've got something for $20,000." → "For what?"

If it's a Class A share of Berkshire Hathaway worth $800K, or a brand-new Lamborghini, $20K is a steal. The rich person evaluates the **deal**, not the sticker.

## Why Rich Buyers Are Actually Easier Clients
1. **Lower relative cost as % of net worth**: $100K from someone with $10M net worth = 1% of their wealth. $100 from someone with $1,000 = 10%. The poorer client is proportionally more demanding.
2. **They know what they want**: Rich buyers have usually bought high-quality things before. They're not evaluating whether the category is worth it — they're evaluating whether *you* are worth it.
3. **They tolerate ambiguity better**: They're comfortable with risk. They're used to non-guaranteed outcomes. This makes the sales conversation smoother.

## What Rich Buyers Pay For
The three things wealthy buyers want:
1. **Fast** — time is their scarcest resource
2. **Easy** — reduced friction and effort
3. **Guaranteed** — certainty of outcome (or as close as possible)

Price is almost secondary to these three. A $100K engagement that saves 200 hours is a bargain.

## Selling to the Wrong Buyer Creates False Negatives
If you're pitching high-ticket to bottom-50% prospects, all the "no's" are correct — they genuinely can't afford it. But this creates a false belief that "the market doesn't want it." In reality, you're just talking to the wrong market.

> "If you pitch your 10× price to the bottom 50%, none of them are going to say yes. You're going to mistakenly believe this is a bad idea. But you're just not talking to the people who have the money."

## Belief Implications for Service Businesses
Because entrepreneurs often come from the same wealth band as their early customers, they develop price sensitivity calibrated to that band. The antidote is exposure: every high-ticket sale recalibrates what feels "normal."

> "You almost choke on the price... you almost have an emotional reaction to zeros."
