# Close Rate Pricing Ladder

## Definition
A diagnostic framework: **your close rate tells you whether your price is right.** If too many people are saying yes, you're underpriced. If almost no one is saying yes, you have an avatar, sales motion, or pricing problem.

## The Ladder

| Close Rate | What It Means | Implied Adjustment |
|---|---|---|
| 80%+ | Underpriced by 3–4× | If charging $100, price should be $300–$400 |
| 60–80% | Underpriced by 2–3× | Should be $200–$300 |
| 50–60% | Underpriced by 1.5–2× | Should be $150–$200 |
| 40–50% | Underpriced by 1.25–1.5× | Should be $125–$150 |
| 30–40% | **Appropriately priced** (with proper sales motion) | Stay here; optimize sales process |
| <30% | Avatar problem or sales motion problem | Fix these before touching price |

**Note:** The 30–40% "appropriately priced" bracket assumes a proper sales motion: prospects are educated before the close call, and the close call is about personalization — not a cold pitch.

## For SaaS Companies (Exceptions)
Lowering price → higher growth rate but lower gross margin. Raising price → higher gross margin but lower growth rate. You want to maximize both. This trade-off doesn't apply to service businesses.

**For all other businesses:** service-based businesses only go up. As you get good → demand rises → you can't serve everyone → raise price → higher margins → hire better talent → virtuous cycle.

## Why This Is Counterintuitive
Most entrepreneurs lower price when sales are slow. The diagnostic says: if close rate is 30%+ with a proper sales motion, the price is fine. If close rate is low (< 30%), the issue is usually:
1. Wrong avatar (selling to people who can't benefit or afford)
2. Broken sales motion (prospects not pre-educated before close call)

Fix those before moving price.

## Interaction with LTV:CAC
Close rate directly impacts CAC. Better close rate (at the same price) = lower CAC = better [[ltv-cac-ratio]] ratio. Close rate is therefore a leading indicator of unit economics health.

## Absolute Profit vs. Relative Thinking
Don't evaluate high-ticket by percentage — think in absolute dollars:
> 1 person × $10,000 (costs $2,000) = **same profit** as 400 people × $50 (costs $25)

Large prices in small quantities are consistently underestimated.

## The "It's Super Expensive" Pre-Frame
Before quoting price, say: *"I should warn you, it's super expensive."*
- Rich prospects: mentally anchor at their own definition of expensive (usually high) → when you quote, they're relieved
- Non-rich prospects: brace themselves for a big number → less shock
- Also gives the salesperson a breath before delivering, reducing voice hesitation

## The Virtuous Cycle
High price → higher gross margins → hire better talent → better service → better reputation → more demand → higher price. Pricing communicates quality as much as it generates revenue. Rich buyers actively shop high-to-low — they interpret higher prices as higher quality by default.
