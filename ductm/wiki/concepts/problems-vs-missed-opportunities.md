# Problems vs Missed Opportunities

## Definition

A mental model for prioritization: the real opportunities in your business lie in the **problems you already know about but haven't solved**, not in the infinite universe of things you *could* be doing.

## Key Distinctions

| | Problems | Missed Opportunities |
|---|---|---|
| **Quantity** | Finite — you can list them all | Infinite — the better you get, the more you see |
| **Nature** | Hairy, unsexy, often unclear how to solve | Shiny, exciting, wrapped in novelty |
| **Effect of chasing** | Compounding — solving them strengthens the core | Half-built bridges — resources spent, no dollar crosses |
| **Emotional pull** | Low — you'd rather avoid them | High — gateway drug to mediocrity |

## The Half-Built Bridge Analogy

When you chase a missed opportunity, you start building a bridge to the dollar on the other side. You hit problems (every opportunity has them). Instead of solving them, you abandon that bridge and start a new one. Repeat. Result: many half-built bridges, zero dollars crossing.

> "Missed opportunities are the gateway drug to mediocrity."

## Why Entrepreneurs Are Wired Wrong for This

Starting a business = being rewarded for pursuing a missed opportunity (you weren't doing this thing, you started, you got rewarded). But that behavior only works **once** — at founding. After that, you must unlearn it and learn to endure and solve problems.

## Practical Tool: The Big Money Ideas List

1. When a shiny idea hits, write it on a "Big Money Ideas" list
2. Don't tell anyone. Don't deploy resources. Let it marinate.
3. When bandwidth opens up, pick the biggest one from the list
4. Often, a month later you won't even care about the idea anymore

> Abraham Lincoln used to write scathing letters when angry, seal them, and put them in his desk. Next day, he rarely sent them. Same principle.

## Connection to Focus

This is a subset of a broader focus-as-strategy principle. Compounding in business happens from doing 1–2 things exceptionally well — not many things mediocre.
