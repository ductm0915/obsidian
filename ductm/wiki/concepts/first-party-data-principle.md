# First-Party Data Principle

**Source:** [[skool-games-q4-2025]] (Sam Ovens + Alex Hormozi)

Your own experimental results from actually doing the work are more accurate and relevant than third-party advice — other people sharing what they think worked for them.

> "The further on I got, the more first-party data I had from doing it — and that's obviously so much more accurate and relevant to you than third-party data, which is other people sharing what they think worked for them."

## The Two Stages of Learning

**Stage 1 — No first-party data yet:**
- You don't know what approach to take
- Consuming books, courses, content is appropriate
- You're pattern-matching to find a direction to commit to

**Stage 2 — You have first-party data:**
- Your results from actual execution are the most trustworthy signal
- When something significant affects your business, you will *see it* in your metrics
- Consumption should become reactive: only consume to solve a *specific active problem*

## Why This Matters

The "information overwhelm" most creators feel comes from:
1. Treating all information as equally relevant (it isn't)
2. Consuming proactively rather than reactively
3. Not having enough first-party data yet to know what's signal vs. noise

Sam's example: He barely reads books anymore (last one was 2 years ago). Early stage he consumed a lot because he didn't know what to do. Now his data tells him what to do.

## Practical Application

- **Skool Traffic Sources feature:** French creator spending all time on Instagram, YouTube delivering more revenue. Two seconds of first-party data → entire strategy changed.
- **Signal vs. noise filter:** "If something is significant enough to actually affect my business, I will change. Until then, I continue doing what I know works."
- **Don't change mid-test:** "Don't change things when a solution is on the way" (Leila Hormozi) — mid-flight pivots kill the data and the plane

## The Cascade Logic

First-party data → identify what's working → **[[repeat-successful-actions]]** → get more first-party data → tune consumption even more narrowly → repeat
