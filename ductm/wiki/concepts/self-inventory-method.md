# Self-Inventory Method

## What It Is

A structured process for extracting yourself from the day-to-day without breaking the business. The goal: document everything you do, then assign each item to a person, process, or project.

## Steps

### 1. Time Study

Track every 15-minute block for one week. No special software needed — a spreadsheet with time intervals and a one-word description every 15 minutes. Effect: forces hyper-awareness of actual time use. Hormozi notes it tends to be the most productive week of your life because of the Hawthorne effect.

Apply to key employees too — use it to reduce dependency on any single "key man," not just the owner.

### 2. List Everything

From the time study, compile a master list of every activity and decision. Be as granular as possible.

### 3. Red / Yellow / Green Triage

For each item, assign:

| Color | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 🟢 Green | Existing team member can take this now | Teach → hand off |
| 🟡 Yellow | Needs a process or project built first | Build the system, then hand off |
| 🔴 Red | Need a hire you don't currently have | Recruit and hire |

**Solve in order:** Green first (quick wins), Yellow next (medium-term), Red last (requires recruiting).

### 4. Build Decision Infrastructure

For each delegated item, create:
- **If-then decision trees** for common scenarios ("change card → do this," "refund request → do this")
- **Spending authorization thresholds** — amount below which the person decides independently
- **KPI scorecard** — for each role: "How does what you do make the company money?"

The KPI question is a forcing function. Most employees cannot answer it clearly. Making them answer it gives them ownership and meaning.

## Key Belief to Install

*"If I can do it right with part of my time, someone else can do it better with all of their time."*

No one can be as good as you with 1/10th the reps. But given full ownership and the right learning path, most competent people can reach 80% of your level. 80% with specialization beats 100% from a distracted generalist.
