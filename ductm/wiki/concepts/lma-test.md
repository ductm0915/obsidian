# LMA Test — Leave It Alone Test

## What It Is

A practical stress test for whether a business is genuinely owner-independent. The owner takes 90 days off — no calls, no decisions — and observes what happens.

**The phone test:** Your phone should not ring. If it does, something isn't properly delegated.

## Three Outcomes and What They Mean

| Outcome | Diagnosis | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Business burns down | Owner is still the operating system | Back to [[self-inventory-method]] |
| Business stays flat or declines slightly | Some key man dependency remains | Identify what broke; fix and re-test |
| Business grows without the owner | You have a real, sellable business | Ready for expansion or exit |

**The baseline goal:** It doesn't burn down.
**The premium goal:** It's in a *better position* when you return.

## Why the Premium Goal Matters for Valuation

A business that improves when the owner is absent is, effectively, a **self-growing annuity**. Investors pay a higher multiple for upward trajectories. This is what creates the [[owner-independence-valuation]] gap between Frustrated Fred and Wealthy William.

## The Iteration Loop

Take 90 days off → something breaks → fix the broken piece → take 90 days off again → repeat until nothing breaks.

This is a systematic search for residual [[key-man-risk]]. Each iteration reveals the next dependency to remove.

## Application to Multi-Location Businesses

For brick-and-mortar operators: the LMA test is the **minimum bar before opening a second location.** If the first location declines when you're absent, opening a second means double overhead, double liability, half the talent, less profit, and more risk. Overexpansion is one of the most common causes of business failure.
