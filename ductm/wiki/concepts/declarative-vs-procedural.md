# Declarative vs. Procedural Knowledge

## Definition
A distinction from educational psychology applied to content strategy:

- **Declarative knowledge:** Knowledge *about* something — what it is, why it matters, what outcomes are possible. ("There are 6 levels of making money.")
- **Procedural knowledge:** Knowledge *how* to do something — step-by-step execution. ("Here's exactly how to structure an outcome-based compensation deal.")

## The Content Strategy Rule
**Give away declarative; sell procedural.**

| Type | What to do | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Declarative | Give away free | Everyone gets value just by listening; no action required to receive benefit; creates desire for implementation |
| Procedural | Sell | Requires action to extract value; the benefit is delayed until they do it; depresses urgency to buy now |

## Why Teaching Procedural Steps Backfires
When you teach "how to do it" publicly, the prospect can:
1. Extract full value without purchasing
2. Walk away satisfied ("I know what to do now")
3. Never implement (delay = no felt result = no purchase)

Teaching *about* how it works creates a knowledge gap + desire for implementation help, without enabling DIY success.

## The Declarative Sweet Spot
Declarative teaching: *"When you cross $2M in revenue and reach the institutional buyer threshold, your multiple goes from 3× to 7×, turning a $2M business into a $35M business."*

This creates desire, intrigue, and a need for help *getting there* — without providing the roadmap.

## Application in Live Pitches
In a sales presentation or free content:
- Explain the *concept* and *outcome* richly.
- Show the *mechanism* at a macro level (why it works).
- Stop before the *tactics* (what to specifically do, in what order).
- Offer the tactics as the paid product/service.
