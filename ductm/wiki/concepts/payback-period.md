# Payback Period

## Definition
**How quickly you recover the cost of acquiring a customer (CAC) from that customer's revenue.**

Formula: Time from customer acquisition until cumulative revenue from that customer equals the CAC.

## Target: 30 Days
Hormozi's rule of thumb: **payback period ≤ 30 days.**

**Why 30 days:** Nearly every business can access a 30-day interest-free credit card. If you can recover CAC within the credit cycle, you can grow infinitely without capital — spend $100 to get a customer, collect $100+ before the card bill is due, repeat.

This creates **self-funding growth** at any scale: no external capital needed, no cash flow crunch, pure compounding.

## What Enables Short Payback Periods
1. **High upfront payment** (pay-before-delivery model, Level 3 in [[six-levels-compensation]])
2. **Strong LTV:CAC** — if the ratio is high enough, initial payment alone covers CAC
3. **Money model optimization** — front-loading cash from existing customers (deposits, annual prepayments, layaway)

## Gym Launch Example
First year LTV:CAC = 100:1. Payback period was nearly instantaneous because gym owners paid upfront and the model drove immediate cash collection.

## Trade-off with Aggressive Growth
Longer payback periods (90 days) are acceptable when:
- You have external funding (VC/PE)
- You have a large base of recurring customers generating stable float
- You're a large company with capital reserves (e.g., Apple Card generating float)

For bootstrapped businesses: shorter payback = more freedom.

## Improving Payback Period Without Changing Core Business
- **Front-load payments:** Collect more upfront (deposits, annual billing, retainers)
- **Reduce CAC:** Better close rates, better targeting, referrals
- **Raise prices:** Same CAC, higher first payment = faster payback
