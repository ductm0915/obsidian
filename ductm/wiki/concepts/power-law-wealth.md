# Power Law Wealth

## Definition
Wealth and customer profit both follow a **power law**: a tiny minority holds a wildly disproportionate share. The business implication is stark — competing for the majority means fighting over scraps.

## The Wealth Distribution Data
If US household wealth ($163T) were $100 split among 100 people representing income percentiles:

| Group | Share |
|---|---|
| Bottom 50% | $2.50 |
| Next 40% | ~$28 |
| Top 9% | ~$38 |
| Top 1% | **$32** |

The top 1% holds more wealth than the bottom 90% **combined**. The top 10% collectively hold ~$70 of the $100.

**Corollary:** The top 10% of Americans have a net worth of $1M+. That's 1 in 10 people — not rare. They have the money. They're just not being sold to correctly.

## 80/20 Applied Recursively to Customer Profit
The same law governs your customer base:
- 20% of customers → 80% of profit
- 4% of customers → 64% of profit
- 1% of customers → 51% of profit

This is not a coincidence — it reflects the same underlying distribution of spending power.

## The Business Implication: Go Where the Money Is
Most businesses unconsciously target the bottom 50% because that's who they see every day. They compete with dozens of other businesses over $2 of available wealth. The antidote:

1. **Price for the people who have money** — not for yourself or your peer group
2. **Build tiers** that capture different wealth bands (see [[tiered-pricing-model]])
3. **Qualify leads** — only pitch expensive things to people with the capacity to pay
4. **Don't underprice** — cheap prices attract the wrong customers and signal low quality

## Key Insight: Profit Concentration Mirrors Wealth Concentration
Just as wealth is concentrated at the top, **profit is concentrated in your highest-tier customers**. Base tiers cover overhead; top tiers generate almost all profit. This is why adding a single high-ticket tier can 5–10× bottom-line profit without proportionally more work.
