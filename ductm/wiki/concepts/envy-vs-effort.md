# Envy vs. Effort

## Core Principle

> *"If people worked for their goals as hard as they envy others for achieving them, they would already have achieved them themselves."*

Envy is misallocated effort. The same mental energy spent on comparison or resentment could be redirected to self-improvement — with compounding returns.

---

## Three Hard Truths About Competition

### 1. No one is doing as well as you think
By comparison, you're actually better off than you believe. The visible success of others is heavily filtered (best moments, curated presentation). Their private reality is far closer to yours.

### 2. You win by growing, not by beating others
> *"You don't win by beating people. You win by growing into your potential and allowing them to shrink into irrelevance by consequence."*

Victory is not a zero-sum game you play against competitors — it's a self-expansion game you play against your own unlived potential.

### 3. Your biggest threat is a mediocre version of yourself
> *"Your biggest threat is not your competition. It's a mediocre version of you that never realized what you could become."*

The competitor who could most efficiently neutralize you is the version of you that never shipped, never committed, never compounded. Not the person down the street.

---

## The Copying Paradox

Hormozi noticed: **the people who get copied most by competitors are the people who ignore the competition copying them.**

The mechanism: if you always start from the customer (what do they actually need?), your work naturally leads the market. Competitors who don't know how to think about first principles just copy you. Meanwhile, you're already on to the next improvement.

Inversion: *"The real threat is that no one copies you at all because you're doing nothing."*

---

## Redirecting Envy Energy

When you feel envy or comparison pressure:
1. Notice the energy being spent (tearing down, comparing, resenting)
2. Redirect it inward: *"What would it look like if I applied this same intensity to not being good enough?"*
3. All that energy → self-improvement, not competitor analysis

> *"Only one of those things will help you."*
