# Selling From Your Wallet

## Definition
**Selling from your wallet** means pricing your product based on what *you* would be willing to pay — rather than what your target customer can and will pay. It's one of the most common and costly mistakes in pricing.

## How It Manifests
- Setting prices based on what your friends/family (who may also be in the bottom 50% of earners) tell you is "reasonable"
- Feeling nauseous quoting a number, so quoting a lower one
- Adding a second tier at 1.5× instead of 5–10×, because the jump "feels too big"
- Believing "no one will pay that" without actually testing it

## Hormozi's Story
Hormozi was canceling gym launch deals when one persistent client asked his price. He said **$6,000** — intending the client to refuse so he could end the call. The client said "done." He raised the price to $8K on the next call, then $10K. By end of day: **8 clients, $60K collected, in a morning.** His prior reference point was $500 packages.

> "I said a number I wanted the person to say no to — and then they said yes. That was the moment my belief was broken."

## Why This Happens
1. **Reference point anchoring**: Your own income/wealth shapes what "feels like a lot of money"
2. **Social proof from the wrong group**: People ask friends who are in the same income band they're trying to escape
3. **Projection**: Assuming prospects share your financial constraints and emotional reaction to zeros

## The Diagnostic
If your close rate is 80%+ you are almost certainly selling from your wallet. At 80% close rate, you're probably **3–4× underpriced** (see [[close-rate-pricing-ladder]]). The market is paying you, but nowhere near what they'd pay.

## The Mindset Shift Required
> "Forever imagine that everyone is rich."

The top 10% of Americans have $1M+ net worth (1 in 10 people). They're not looking for deals. They're looking for **fast, easy, and guaranteed**. The price you're afraid to quote is not "too expensive" to them — it's only too expensive to you.

## The Belief Breakthrough Pattern
The belief breaks when you quote a "scary" number and the client says yes. After that, the previous number feels like undercharging. Most entrepreneurs need to manufacture this moment intentionally — by saying a number they "expect rejection" on.

## Tactical Workaround (While the Belief Is Forming)
1. **Write + slide**: If you freeze quoting verbally, write the number on paper and slide it across
2. **"It's super expensive" pre-frame**: Before quoting, say "I should warn you, it's super expensive." This anchors the prospect's expectation upward and gives you a breath before delivering the number
3. **Value vs. price reframe**: Separate from the number — "We have value and we have price. Move one of them. Either do less or charge more."
