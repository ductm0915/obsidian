# Shame vs. Guilt

## The Distinction

> *"Guilt is when you break your own rules. Shame is when you break other people's rules."*

| | **Guilt** | **Shame** |
|--|-----------|-----------|
| **Whose rules?** | Yours | Others' |
| **Source** | Internal value violation | Social judgment / conformity |
| **Productive?** | Sometimes — signals misalignment with your own values | Rarely — signals compliance pressure from others |
| **Response** | Realign your behavior with your values | Question whether you endorse the rules |

Example: being gay in a religious community → shame (breaking community rules) + possibly guilt (if you share those rules) OR neither (if in a liberal environment). Same action, entirely different emotional response based on whose rules you're adopting.

---

## The Key Question

> *"Whose rules are you following? And who wrote them?"*

The people who are successful are usually the ones shouting from rooftops that the rules are different than you think. The rules you're too ashamed to break were written by people who have never taken the risks you're considering — and are often designed to maintain a power structure that keeps "have-nots" from taking action.

> *"Shame was invented by people who have to prevent people who have not from taking action."*

---

## Shame as an Action Blocker

Shame is most damaging when it prevents asymmetric-upside actions: reaching out to a stranger, making an ask, trying something and failing publicly, starting when you have nothing.

The actual risk/reward of trying:
- **Worst case:** You get a no and learn from the experience → you get better
- **Best case:** Sale, relationship, partnership, breakthrough

> *"Trying has an asymmetric risk-reward return. Worst case is you get a no and you learn from it... Life gives you unlimited shots while you're alive."*

---

## Fear in the Vague

Shame (like all fear) **only exists in the vague**. Spell it out specifically and it loses its grip.

If I reach out to someone, the worst realistic outcome is:
1. They don't respond → nothing lost
2. They say no → I learned something; slightly embarrassed for a short time
3. They say yes → opportunity created

The amorphous dread of shame collapses when subjected to specific decomposition. See also: [[fear-vs-regret]] for the same decomposition technique applied to bigger decisions.

---

## The Fog Metaphor

> *"If you've ever walked through very dense fog, you can only see a couple steps in front of you. The only thing you can do in fog is start walking. As you walk, more steps become available to you."*

Shame-driven paralysis is refusing to walk because you can't see the whole path. But: (1) you don't have the skill/resources yet to see that far anyway, and (2) the path only reveals itself through movement. The misconception is that you need full visibility before you start.

---

## Play the Cards You're Dealt

Hormozi's father, responding to Hormozi's reluctance to use his advantages: *"Do you think Shaq, when he was 7 feet tall, was like 'It wouldn't be fair for me to play basketball. Other people aren't as tall as me'? You play the cards you're dealt."*

Refusing to use advantages out of fear others will discount your success is: (1) still a form of shame-driven behavior, and (2) counterproductive — people will discount your success regardless of circumstances, so ignoring them and using your advantages is strictly dominant.
