# Blame Distortions (Three Sources of Objections)

## Definition
[[alex-hormozi]] traces all sales objections to **three core cognitive distortions** originally identified by Dr. Albert Ellis (the founder of Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, CBT). These distortions are irrational thought patterns people use to cast blame and avoid decisions.

> "The objections that people offer you — where they cast their power to — are distortions of reality."

Understanding the *source* of an objection (not just the surface wording) tells you exactly where you are in the conversation and which layer to address.

## The Three Distortions

### 1. Circumstances
**Irrational belief**: "I must get what I want, when I want it. If I don't, I can't stand it."  
**In sales, sounds like**: "I don't have time." / "I can't afford it." / "The program doesn't fit my lifestyle."  
**Surface objections**: Time (macro/micro/when-then), Money/Value, Fit

### 2. Others
**Irrational belief**: "Other people must treat me fairly. If they don't, they deserve punishment."  
**In sales, sounds like**: "I have to talk to my spouse." / "My partner has to approve this." / "My employees won't support it."  
**Surface objections**: Authority (decision maker not present)

### 3. Self
**Irrational belief**: "I must do well or else I am worthless."  
**In sales, sounds like**: "I need to think about it." / "Let me sleep on it." / "I'm not sure yet."  
**Surface objections**: Avoidance / Stall

## The Onion of Blame
Prospects layer their objections from outside → in:
```
Layer 1 (outermost): Circumstances — "I don't have time/money/fit"
Layer 2: Others — "My spouse/partner won't let me"
Layer 3 (core): Self — "I need to think about it" (fear of deciding)
```
- Peeling each layer moves you closer to the real decision.
- "I need to think about it" is actually **good news** — you're talking to someone with decision power who just needs help making it.
- Circumstances objections are the easiest surface deflections — people use them reflexively.

## The Five Manifestations

| Distortion | Objection Type | Examples |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| Circumstances | **Time** | "Busy season," "micro no time," "when-then fallacy" |
| Circumstances | **Money/Value** | "Can't afford it," "too expensive," "not worth it" |
| Circumstances | **Fit** | "Not sure it's for me," "I don't like X aspect" |
| Others | **Authority** | "Have to ask spouse / partner / boss" |
| Self | **Avoidance** | "I need to think about it," "let me sleep on it" |

## The When-Then Fallacy
A specific Circumstances-type distortion: "When I have X, then I will do Y" — inverting the sequence needed to achieve X.  
Examples: "I'll save money when I'm rich." / "I'll buy the program when I have more money."  
The program *is* what gets them the money. The prerequisite is the outcome.

## How to Use This Framework
1. **Listen for which layer** the objection comes from (surface words + context).
2. **Never disagree** — acknowledge, redirect: "Totally get it. Do you think you'll be busy again in the future?"
3. **Peel the layer** by offering a logical refutation. Their resistance signals you're close.
4. The goal: get them to say "Well, that's not a good reason" about each layer until only the real decision remains.
