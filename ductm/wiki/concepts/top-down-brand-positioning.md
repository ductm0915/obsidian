# Top-Down Brand Positioning

## Definition
Start your brand at the **highest price point** you can credibly deliver, then work your way down to more affordable offerings over time. The brand story flows naturally ("I made something exclusive, now I'm making it accessible") — the reverse doesn't work.

## The Tesla Model
- Roadster ($250K, limited production) → Model S → Model 3/Y
- Narrative: "This amazing, fast, expensive car — now here's a more affordable version for the rest of you."
- **Reverse narrative**: "I'm a budget brand — now buy my luxury car." Doesn't land.

## Why Top-Down Works Better

### 1. Brand Anchoring
Launching premium first anchors the entire brand as high-quality. All subsequent lower tiers **look like deals** compared to the anchor, even though they're still profitable.

### 2. Operational Logic
High-ticket customers are:
- **Fewer in number** — manageable before you have scale
- **Less demanding as a % of net worth** — a $100K client whose net worth is $10M is spending 1% of their wealth. A $100 client whose net worth is $1,000 is spending 10%. The cheaper client is proportionally more demanding.
- **Higher-margin** — serving 1 person at $100K is far easier than serving 1,000 people at $100

### 3. Operational Readiness
Most service businesses don't have the systems to handle mass-market volume. Starting at the top means you build skills, margins, and reputation before tackling the volume problem.

## The Honda vs. Rolls-Royce Asymmetry
- Rolls-Royce launching a more affordable car → credible (luxury going mass)
- Honda launching a luxury car → hard (budget brand going luxury)

A brand is easier to extend downward than upward. Launch premium first.

## Tactical Implication: Start as High as You Can
Even if you eventually plan to serve lower price points, **start with the unscalable high-ticket version** first:
- Proof of concept with high margins
- Builds case studies and reputation
- Lets you refine the delivery before automating it for scale

## Common Objection: "But I Need Volume to Survive"
The counter: serving 1 customer at $100K is almost always easier than serving 1,000 at $100. Only go mass-market when you have the capital and systems — and that capital comes from serving the top first.
