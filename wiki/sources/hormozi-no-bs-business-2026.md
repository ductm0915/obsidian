# No BS Business Advice to Get Rich in 2026

## Metadata
- **Author:** [[alex-hormozi]]
- **Format:** YouTube video transcript (year-end recap)
- **Context:** Alex's annual review of lessons learned running the Acquisition.com portfolio — went from ~$50M EBITDA to just under $100M. First 100 days of the year: zero days off.

---

## Summary

Alex Hormozi's comprehensive year-end business review covering portfolio growth strategies, personal growth lessons, operational frameworks, branding insights, and cultural philosophy. The video distills what worked and what didn't across the Acquisition.com portfolio companies.

---

## Key Claims & Frameworks

### 1. Set Fewer Goals, Plan Only 12 Weeks

Long-term planning beyond 12 weeks is largely wasted effort. Half the plan becomes irrelevant due to changing conditions. Inspired by Jensen Huang's approach at Nvidia: focus on what you can do today.

> "I pretty much only care what we're going to do in the next 12 weeks."

Growth is not a straight line — it's periods of stagnation punctuated by breakthroughs.

### 2. Focus as Strategy — One Priority

The CEO's job is to pick **one** priority that, if solved, makes all other problems less important. Concentrate all resources (people 1–5) on solving problem A, rather than splitting across A–E.

> "A good strategist says: given the resources I currently have, what one thing could I do that would make all of these problems disappear?"

This is [[leverage]] applied to prioritization. The "one thing" is often building a better product or a stronger brand — because those solve multiple downstream problems simultaneously.

### 3. Supply vs Demand Constrained Businesses

Every business sits on a supply-demand curve. The first step is knowing which side constrains you:

- **Supply constrained:** Cleaning companies, accounting firms, law firms, high-end consulting. Plenty of demand; hard to find people who can deliver. *Real business = attracting, hiring, training, managing, incentivizing people.*
- **Demand constrained:** E-commerce, B2B SaaS, weight loss, fitness, music lessons. Plenty of suppliers; hard to find customers.

Once identified, gain **leverage** over the constraint to solve it at scale, once and for all.

**Example:** A home services company struggles to attract plumbers. Each plumber = $400K/year gross profit. Frame hiring as an investment: what would you pay one-time to unlock $400K/year? Internal referral bonuses of $25K–$50K become obvious.

### 4. Talent as Highest Leverage

The single biggest portfolio growth driver was placing key leaders in companies. The best-performing companies were the ones where they placed ~6 great leaders.

Key principles:
- **Fewer, better people** — incentivize them heavily
- **CEO must actively recruit** — top talent won't respond to traditional recruiters
- **Internal referral bonuses** should be proportional to the gross profit that person generates
- **Talent only gets better in the future** (quote from Shaan Puri / [[leila-hormozi]]'s network)
- **Work with people who move towards you** — stop trying to sell people on your values

> "If your team doesn't admire some aspects of you, you either have to change them or you have to change you."

### 5. Three Portfolio Growth Themes

1. **Leadership/Talent Recruiting** — placing key players
2. **Focus** — ruthless prioritization, deleting everything non-essential
3. **CRO (Conversion Rate Optimization)** — running 3–10 split tests on key pipeline points. One portfolio company got +20%, +50%, and +28% lifts from three winning tests.

**Hack:** Check the conversion rates of third-party tools you integrate. Swapping tools for better UX/conversion can yield monster lifts.

### 6. [[six-horsemen-stagnation]] — Six Reasons Businesses Get Stuck

Each presents a "rock and hard place" scenario: short-term pain vs. pain forever.

1. **Underpriced** — At full capacity but not making money. Fix: raise prices using the 5+20 rule (every 5 sales, bump price 20%).
2. **Overcompensation** — Employees with revenue shares that scale faster than the business. Fix: shore up infrastructure first, then renegotiate with a transition period.
3. **Infinite Avatars** — Serving too many customer types. Fix: 80/20 your customers, niche down, price for the value you deliver to your best 20%.
4. **Overextension** — Opening location 2/3 before location 1 is truly self-running. Fix: hire an operator or prune back. Run a 6-month test of the business operating without you.
5. **More Than One Business** — The fallacy that Zuckerberg had a side hustle. Fix: pick one, go all-in.
6. **No Data** — Can't diagnose problems without metrics. Fix: start collecting data. Test: if this metric changes (up or down), does it change our behavior? If not, don't track it.

### 7. [[scale-framework]] — SCALE (for Scaling)

A micro-cycle that exists within every process:
- **S**tart — begin doing the thing
- **C**ompound — do more of it (increase volume)
- **A**ugment — do it better (improve quality)
- **L**everage — make it consistent, automate, reduce volatility
- **E**xpand — take it to the next territory/channel/product

Then loop back to Start for the new territory.

### 8. Optimization Framework (for Fixing)

When coming in to fix something that already exists (inspired by Elon Musk):
1. **Question the requirements** — why are we doing everything we're doing?
2. **Delete** — remove everything that doesn't directly create the department's output
3. **Optimize** — rank remaining activities by results/resources ratio
4. **Pull up timelines** — ask "how many hours?" not "what day?" → can 7x team speed
5. **Automate** — make it consistent and self-running

### 9. [[problems-vs-missed-opportunities]]

> "The opportunities you're looking for are in the problems you need to solve."

- **Problems are finite.** You can list them all.
- **Missed opportunities are infinite.** The better you get, the more you see.
- Chasing missed opportunities = building half-built bridges that never carry a dollar across.
- Compounding in business happens from doing 1–2 things exceptionally well, not many things mediocre.
- **Practical tool:** Keep a "Big Money Ideas" list. Add shiny ideas there. Let them marinate. Often, a month later you won't even care about them anymore.

### 10. Rush is Imaginary

Rush is entirely linked to your **competition reference point** — who you choose to compare yourself to. It's arbitrary and made up.

> "Unless you have a network-effect, winner-take-all market... rush is actually linked to one thing: my competition reference point."

Three doors: (1) stop comparing, (2) change who you compare to, (3) compare to your heroes at the same stage.

### 11. Branding — 70/30 Rule & B2B Strategy

**New Balance story:** CMO flipped spend from 70% bottom-of-funnel / 30% top-of-funnel to 30/70. First 18 months: less money. Month 19: explosive growth. Corroborates the 3:1 value-to-ask ratio.

**B2B branding strategies:**
1. Help them accomplish their goals for free (content, education)
2. Help someone *like them* accomplish their goals (social proof/case studies)
3. Accomplish the aspirational outcome yourself (or help others do it)

Aspirational outcomes get "regressed down" — if Northface survives Everest, it'll keep me warm walking to my car. If Hormozi did a $46M exit, his advice probably helps my $2M business.

Brand and product are interwoven long-term. 3:1 ratio of value-giving to asking.

### 12. [[kind-not-nice]] — Culture & Feedback

> "Criticism is the gap between desired and actual. An insult is assigning a negative label."

- **Criticism:** "The expectation is 9:00 AM. You arrived at 9:05. Here's what needs to change." → Factual, actionable.
- **Insult:** "You're lazy." → Emotional, destructive.

Framework: State the gap + tell them what to do instead (not just what not to do).

Reed Hastings frame: "We are a professional sports team trying to win championships. We are not a family."

### 13. [[what-who-when]] — Operational Execution

For every action item:
- **What** is the task?
- **Who** owns it?
- **When** — "How many hours will this take?" (not "what day")

Stack and recap: repeat each item multiple times during the meeting. First item gets repeated ~7 times by the end. Optionally: "repeat it back to me" (1/3 times they get it wrong).

### 14. Pay for Performance & Technical vs Management Tracks

- Align compensation with business output. Back-test new comp structures against prior months.
- For transitions: let existing employees keep current salary + add performance bonus on top. New hires get performance-based from day one.
- Create **two career tracks**: technical (individual contributor excellence) and management (people leadership). Single management track creates perverse incentives: hoarding knowledge, empire-building headcount, ass-kissing over output.

### 15. Problems & Solutions Have Delays

You're living with problems you created 6 months ago. Solutions won't bear fruit immediately.

> "Set the evaluation timeline before implementing the solution, then stick to it."

When diagnosing a decline: look at where growth first stopped (not where decline started). Often the cause is a **who** — and often it's someone you *added*, not someone you lost.

### 16. [[sawdust-business]] — Value from Waste

Lumber mills turned sawdust into plywood. Airbnb turned spare bedrooms into revenue.

Acquisition.com example: unused office space + portfolio team with spare bandwidth → workshops → generated deal flow, cash flow, content, and talent flow simultaneously. Four boxes checked from one resource that was already paid for.

> "Write down everything you do regularly. Ask: is there a way to recombine these into something valuable at no extra cost?"

### 17. IRL vs AI — Polar Opposites, Double Down on Both

- **IRL:** Unmet demand for real human connection post-COVID. In-person customers have dramatically higher brand affinity, loyalty, referrals, ratings, purchase frequency.
- **AI:** Efficiency lever. Use AI well, don't try to be an AI company.
- IRL is harder to scale but worth scaling because it's more valuable.

### 18. Content Strategy Lessons

- **Back to business:** Wide content (vlogs, meals, workouts) → fewer absolute business owners opting in. Returning to business-focused content restored growth.
- **Pre-production > post-production** for educational content.
- **3 P's introduction:** Proof, Promise, Plan — in the first 30 seconds of every video.
- **Platform MVP of the year:** Email (written word is Hormozi's best format).
- **Unexpected traffic source:** Amazon books → 15% of acquisition.com traffic indirectly.

### 19. Personal Optimization

- **Sleep Big 3:** Pitch black, cold temperature (mattress pad), earplugs (−4 bpm resting heart rate)
- **Work environment:** Blackout windows, two desks (standing + sitting), kneeling chair (eliminated shoulder/neck pain)
- **Fitness:** Reduced to 2x/week (Sat/Sun, go hard, no rush), transitioned to longevity at age 35 (from 250 lbs to 203 lbs)

### 20. School Investment

- First brand deal ever. Platform for community-based businesses.
- Average paid community makes $1,360/month.
- CRO lesson: highly optimized pages resist change — 16 major split tests, only 2 won.
- #1 reason for cancellation = overwhelm. Deletion and simplicity > adding features.
- **Flywheel:** Make media → attract business owners → do deals → get outcomes → make media about outcomes.
